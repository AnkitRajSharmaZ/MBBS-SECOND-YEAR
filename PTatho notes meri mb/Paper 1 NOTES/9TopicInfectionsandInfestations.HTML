TOPIC 9: INFECTIONS AND INFESTATIONS

Syllabus Scope: Pathogenesis and Pathology of Malaria and Leprosy.

1. MALARIA

Definition: Malaria is a life-threatening protozoal disease transmitted by the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito. It is characterized clinically by cyclic high-grade fever (febrile paroxysms), chills, anemia, and splenomegaly.

Etiology

  • Causative Agents (Genus Plasmodium):
    • P. vivax: Benign Tertian (Most common in India).
    • P. falciparum: Malignant Tertian (Most severe/fatal).
    • P. malariae: Quartan.
    • P. ovale: Benign Tertian.
  • Vector: Female Anopheles Mosquito (Definitive Host - Sexual cycle occurs here).
  • Human Host: Intermediate Host (Asexual cycle occurs here).

Life Cycle (Brief Overview)

1. Exo-erythrocytic (Hepatic) Schizogony:

Sporozoites injected by mosquito → Enter Hepatocytes → Multiply asexually → Form Hepatic Schizonts → Rupture → Release Merozoites.

*Note: P. vivax and P. ovale have dormant forms called Hypnozoites which cause Relapse.

2. Erythrocytic Schizogony (Blood Stage):

Merozoites invade RBCs → Trophozoite (Ring form) → Erythrocytic Schizont → Rupture RBCs → Release Merozoites + Pigment + Toxins → Clinical Fever (Rigors).

Pathogenesis

The pathology is primarily due to the destruction of Red Blood Cells (RBCs) and the host immune response.

1. Anemia (Hemolysis)

  • Direct Hemolysis: Parasites rupture infected RBCs.
  • Splenic Sequestration: The spleen destroys both infected and uninfected RBCs (which are coated with complement/antibodies).
  • Bone Marrow Suppression: TNF-α inhibits erythropoiesis.

2. Cytoadherence and Sequestration (Specific to P. falciparum)

  • P. falciparum infected RBCs develop "knobs" on their surface containing parasite-derived proteins like PfEMP1 (P. falciparum Erythrocyte Membrane Protein 1).
  • These knobs bind to adhesion molecules on endothelial cells (e.g., ICAM-1, CD36) in small vessels of the brain, kidneys, and lungs.
  • Consequences:
    • Sequestration: Parasitized cells stick to vessel walls and disappear from peripheral blood.
    • Rosetting: Infected RBCs stick to uninfected RBCs, forming clumps.
    • Blockage: Microcirculation is blocked, leading to tissue ischemia and hypoxia (e.g., Cerebral Malaria).

3. Host Resistance Factors (Genetic Protection)

Certain genetic traits provide resistance against malaria:

  • Sickle Cell Trait (HbAS): Heterozygotes are protected against severe P. falciparum malaria. (Parasite cannot thrive in HbS cells; early clearance by spleen).
  • Duffy Antigen Negative: Resistance to P. vivax (Duffy antigen is the entry receptor for P. vivax). Common in West Africa.
  • G6PD Deficiency: Protects against severe malaria.

Pathology (Morphology) of Organs

1. Spleen (Splenomegaly)

Acute Malaria:

  • Gross: Moderately enlarged (200-500g), soft, hyperemic. Cut surface is dark red or Slate-Grey.
  • Microscopy:
    • Congestion of red pulp sinusoids.
    • Macrophages laden with Malarial Pigment (Hemozoin).
    • Parasitized RBCs may be seen.

Chronic Malaria ("Ague Cake Spleen"):

  • Gross: Massive enlargement (>1000g), firm and hard. Capsule is thickened. Cut surface is grey/black.
  • Microscopy: Fibrosis of red pulp, scarring, and heavy pigment deposition.

2. Liver

  • Gross: Enlarged and slate-grey (due to pigment).
  • Microscopy:
    • Kupffer Cell Hyperplasia: Hypertrophied and loaded with black hemozoin pigment.
    • Hepatocytes may show fatty change or necrosis (in shock).

3. Brain (Cerebral Malaria)

  • Caused by P. falciparum.
  • Gross: Brain is edematous. Vessels are congested (cherry-pink color).
  • Microscopy:
    • Capillaries are plugged with parasitized RBCs.
    • Ring Hemorrhages: Small petechial hemorrhages around necrotic vessels.
    • Durck’s Granuloma: Areas of necrosis/micro-infarcts surrounded by a glial reaction (microglia).

4. Kidney (Blackwater Fever)

  • Seen in severe P. falciparum malaria.
  • Mechanism: Massive intravascular hemolysis → Hemoglobinuria.
  • Morphology: Kidneys are dark brown/black. Tubules are blocked by Hemoglobin casts. Features of Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN) are seen.

2. LEPROSY (HANSEN'S DISEASE)

Definition: A slowly progressive, chronic granulomatous infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium leprae, primarily affecting the cooler parts of the body: Skin, Peripheral Nerves, Upper Respiratory Tract, Eyes, and Testes.

Etiology

  • Agent: Mycobacterium leprae.
    • Acid-fast bacilli (weakly acid-fast compared to TB).
    • Obligate intracellular (cannot be grown on artificial media).
    • Multiplies best at 32-34°C (hence affects extremities/skin).
  • Transmission: Respiratory droplets (nasal secretions) from lepromatous patients. Prolonged skin contact.
  • Incubation Period: Very long (2 to 40 years).

Classification

Ridley-Jopling Classification (Based on Immunity):

  1. Tuberculoid (TT): High Immunity.
  2. Borderline Tuberculoid (BT)
  3. Mid-Borderline (BB)
  4. Borderline Lepromatous (BL)
  5. Lepromatous (LL): Low Immunity.

Also: Indeterminate Leprosy (Early stage, not yet classified).

Pathogenesis (Immunology)

The type of leprosy depends on the Host's T-Cell Mediated Immunity (CMI).

  • Tuberculoid (TT): Host has a strong Th1 Response (IL-2, IFN-γ).
    • Activates Macrophages → Kills bacteria → Forms Epithelioid Granulomas.
    • Localized disease (Paucibacillary).
  • Lepromatous (LL): Host has a specific T-cell Anergy (Failure of Th1).
    • Weak CMI. Dominant Th2 Response (IL-4, IL-10).
    • B-cells make antibodies (Hypergammaglobulinemia) which are ineffective.
    • Macrophages cannot kill bacteria → Bacilli multiply unchecked (Multibacillary).

Differences: Tuberculoid vs. Lepromatous Leprosy

Feature Tuberculoid Leprosy (TT) Lepromatous Leprosy (LL)
Cell Mediated Immunity High (Strong Th1). Absent/Low (Anergy).
Lepromin Test Positive. Negative.
Bacterial Load Scanty or Absent (Paucibacillary). Numerous (Multibacillary). Globi present.
Skin Lesions Few (< 5), asymmetrical. Hypopigmented macular patches with raised margins. Dry and hairless. Numerous, symmetrical. Macules, papules, nodules. Leonine Facies (Lion-like face) due to skin thickening. Loss of eyebrows (Madarosis).
Nerve Involvement Early, localized, and asymmetrical. Nerves are thickened and palpable (e.g., Ulnar). Anesthesia is prominent. Late, diffuse, and symmetrical. "Stocking and glove" anesthesia.
Microscopy (Histology) 1. Epithelioid Granulomas (well-formed).
2. Lymphocytic infiltration.
3. Granulomas erode the epidermis (No Grenz zone).
4. Nerve destruction is severe.		 1. Virchow Cells (Foamy histiocytes/macrophages).
2. Abundant bacilli in clumps (Globi).
3. Clear zone between epidermis and granuloma (Grenz Zone).
4. Nerves are intact initially.

Lepra Reactions

Sudden exacerbations of the disease, often after starting treatment.

  • Type 1 Reaction (Reversal Reaction):
    • Seen in Borderline cases (BT, BB, BL).
    • Due to upgrading of CMI (Type IV Hypersensitivity).
    • Existing lesions become red and swollen. Nerves become painful (Neuritis).
  • Type 2 Reaction (Erythema Nodosum Leprosum - ENL):
    • Seen in Lepromatous cases (BL, LL).
    • Due to Antigen-Antibody complex deposition (Type III Hypersensitivity).
    • Characterized by tender red skin nodules, fever, lymphadenitis, and iridocyclitis.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Malaria. Describe the Pathogenesis and Pathology of the Spleen and Liver in Malaria.

Introduction: Malaria is a protozoal disease caused by Plasmodium species. It involves a complex interplay between the parasite, RBCs, and the host reticuloendothelial system.

Body:
Pathogenesis:

  • Hemolysis: Rupture of parasitized RBCs releases parasite antigens and Hemozoin pigment.
  • Phagocytosis: The reticuloendothelial cells (macrophages) of the spleen and liver become hyperplastic to clear debris and pigment.

Pathology of Spleen:
  • Gross: In acute cases, it is enlarged, soft, and dark red. In chronic cases ("Ague cake"), it is massive, hard, and slate-grey/black due to fibrosis and heavy pigment.
  • Microscopy: Sinusoids are congested. Macrophages are laden with malarial pigment. Fibrosis is seen in chronic stages.

Pathology of Liver:
  • Gross: Enlarged and greyish discoloration.
  • Microscopy: Kupffer cells are enlarged (hyperplastic) and contain black hemozoin pigment. Hepatocytes may show necrosis.

Conclusion: The slate-grey discoloration of organs due to hemozoin is the hallmark gross feature of malaria.

Q2: Classify Leprosy. Discuss the differences between Tuberculoid and Lepromatous Leprosy in detail.

Introduction: Leprosy is classified based on the Ridley-Jopling scale which reflects the host's cell-mediated immunity (CMI). The spectrum ranges from Tuberculoid (TT) to Lepromatous (LL).

Body:
1. Immunological Basis:

  • TT: Strong CMI (Th1 response). Body limits infection. Lepromin positive.
  • LL: Weak/Absent CMI (Th2 response). Infection disseminates. Lepromin negative.

2. Clinical Differences:
  • TT: Few hypopigmented anesthetic patches. Nerve thickening is early and asymmetrical.
  • LL: Numerous nodules, Leonine facies, Madarosis. Nerve involvement is late and symmetrical.

3. Histological Differences:
  • TT: Epithelioid granulomas that touch the epidermis. No bacilli found.
  • LL: Foamy macrophages (Virchow cells) with Globi (bacilli). A clear "Grenz zone" separates the lesion from the epidermis.

Conclusion: Tuberculoid leprosy represents a contained infection due to good immunity, while Lepromatous leprosy represents disseminated infection due to anergy.
