TOPIC 16: APLASTIC ANEMIA

Syllabus Scope: Enumeration of etiology, pathogenesis, and findings (pancytopenia, hypocellular marrow).

1. DEFINITION

Aplastic anemia is a syndrome of bone marrow failure characterized by:

  1. Pancytopenia: Deficiency of all three cell lines in peripheral blood (Anemia, Neutropenia, Thrombocytopenia).
  2. Hypocellular Bone Marrow: Replacement of hematopoietic tissue by fat (Aplasia/Hypoplasia).

Note: The term "aplastic anemia" is slightly misleading; it is actually a pancytopenia due to marrow aplasia, not just anemia.

2. ETIOLOGY (CAUSES)

Most cases are idiopathic (unknown cause). The known causes are classified into primary (inherited) and secondary (acquired).

A. Acquired Aplastic Anemia (95% of cases)

  • Idiopathic (65%): No identifiable cause (Primary Acquired).
  • Chemical Agents:
    • Benzene (Dose-related).
    • Insecticides (DDT), Solvents.
  • Drugs:
    • Dose-dependent (predictable): Cytotoxic drugs (Cancer chemotherapy), Antimetabolites.
    • Idiosyncratic (unpredictable): Chloramphenicol (most classic), Phenylbutazone, Gold salts, Anticonvulsants.
  • Viruses:
    • Viral Hepatitis (Non-A, Non-B, Non-C type usually).
    • EBV, CMV, HIV.
    • Parvovirus B19 (Causes Pure Red Cell Aplasia, not typically aplastic anemia, but relevant).
  • Radiation: Whole-body irradiation.
  • Miscellaneous: Pregnancy, Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria (PNH).

B. Inherited Aplastic Anemia (Rare)

  • Fanconi Anemia: Autosomal recessive disorder.
    Features: Hypoplastic marrow, short stature, microcephaly, thumb/radius anomalies, caf√©-au-lait spots. Defect in DNA repair.
  • Dyskeratosis Congenita: Defect in Telomerase (short telomeres).

3. PATHOGENESIS

Two major mechanisms have been proposed, often overlapping:

1. Immune-Mediated Suppression (Most Common)

This is the mechanism in idiopathic and many drug/virus-induced cases.

  • Mechanism: An extrinsic antigen (virus/drug) alters the stem cells.
  • T-Cell Attack: Activated Cytotoxic T Lymphocytes (CD8+) produce cytokines like IFN-gamma and TNF-alpha.
  • Effect: These cytokines suppress hematopoietic stem cell growth and induce Apoptosis (cell death) of CD34+ progenitors.
  • Evidence: Response to immunosuppressive therapy (ATG, Cyclosporine).

2. Intrinsic Stem Cell Abnormality

  • Genetic damage to stem cells (e.g., by radiation or benzene) renders them incapable of division and differentiation.
  • Defects in Telomerase leads to premature shortening of telomeres and stem cell senescence (aging).

4. CLINICAL FEATURES

Symptoms are related to the deficiency of the three cell lines (Pancytopenia). Onset is usually insidious.

  • Anemia (RBC loss): Progressive weakness, fatigue, pallor, dyspnea, palpitations.
  • Thrombocytopenia (Platelet loss): Bleeding tendency.
    • Petechiae, Ecchymoses (bruises).
    • Gum bleeding, Epistaxis (nosebleeds).
    • Menorrhagia.
  • Neutropenia (WBC loss): Recurrent infections.
    • Fever, chills.
    • Ulceronecrotic lesions in mouth and pharynx.

Important Negative Finding: Splenomegaly is typically ABSENT. The presence of a large spleen should raise suspicion of other diagnoses (e.g., Leukemia, Hypersplenism).

5. LABORATORY FINDINGS (INVESTIGATIONS)

A. Peripheral Blood Findings

  • Pancytopenia: Low Hb, Low WBC, Low Platelets.
  • Anemia: Usually Normocytic Normochromic (sometimes Macrocytic).
  • Reticulocyte Count: Markedly Decreased (Corrected retic count < 1%). This distinguishes it from hemolytic anemia.
  • WBC: Neutropenia (often severe < 500/uL). Relative Lymphocytosis (lymphocytes are spared as they live longer).
  • Platelets: Thrombocytopenia (< 20,000/uL is severe).
  • Morphology: No abnormal or immature cells (blasts) seen.

B. Bone Marrow Examination (Confirmatory)

Bone Marrow Aspiration and Biopsy are essential.

  • Aspiration: Often yields a "Dry Tap" (nothing comes out because the marrow is empty).
  • Biopsy (Trephine): The most reliable test.
    • Hypocellularity: Markedly reduced hematopoietic cells.
    • Fatty Replacement: Marrow space is largely replaced by Fat cells (Adipocytes).
    • Residual Cells: Only scattered lymphocytes, plasma cells, and stromal cells remain.
    • Iron Stain: Increased iron stores (due to lack of utilization).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Aplastic Anemia. Enumerate its etiology and describe the Bone Marrow findings.

Introduction: Aplastic anemia is a syndrome of bone marrow failure characterized by peripheral pancytopenia and marrow hypocellularity.

Body:
Etiology:

  • Primary (Idiopathic): 65% cases.
  • Secondary: Drugs (Chloramphenicol, Cytotoxics), Chemicals (Benzene), Viruses (Hepatitis, EBV), Radiation.
  • Inherited: Fanconi Anemia.

Bone Marrow Findings:
Aspiration often yields a "dry tap". Biopsy shows a profoundly hypocellular marrow where hematopoietic elements are replaced by fat cells (adipocytes). The few remaining cells are mostly lymphocytes and plasma cells. There is no fibrosis.

Conclusion: A biopsy is mandatory to distinguish it from other causes of pancytopenia like Aleukemic Leukemia or Myelodysplasia.

Q2: Discuss the Pathogenesis and Clinical features of Aplastic Anemia.

Introduction: Aplastic anemia results from injury to the pluripotent stem cell.

Body:
Pathogenesis: The most widely accepted mechanism is Immune-mediated suppression. An initiating event (virus/drug) alters stem cell antigens, triggering T-cells (CD8+) to secrete cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF-α) which kill stem cells or suppress their division.
Clinical Features: Symptoms arise from pancytopenia.

  • Anemia: Pallor, fatigue.
  • Thrombocytopenia: Petechiae, bleeding gums.
  • Neutropenia: Fevers, infections.

Crucial Point: Splenomegaly is notably absent.

Conclusion: It is a life-threatening condition requiring immunosuppression or bone marrow transplantation.
