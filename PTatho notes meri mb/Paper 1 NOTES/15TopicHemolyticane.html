TOPIC 15: HEMOLYTIC ANEMIA

Syllabus Scope: Definition, Classification, Pathogenesis, Lab Indices, Sickle Cell Anemia, Thalassemia, and Acquired Hemolytic Anemias.

1. DEFINITION AND CLASSIFICATION

Definition: Hemolytic anemias are defined as anemias resulting from an increase in the rate of red blood cell (RBC) destruction, leading to a shortened RBC lifespan (normal: 120 days). The bone marrow compensates by increasing erythropoiesis (reticulocytosis).

Classification of Hemolytic Anemias

A. Hereditary (Intrinsic Defects)

  • Membrane Defects: Hereditary Spherocytosis, Hereditary Elliptocytosis.
  • Enzyme Defects: G6PD Deficiency, Pyruvate Kinase Deficiency.
  • Hemoglobinopathies: Sickle Cell Anemia, Thalassemia.

B. Acquired (Extrinsic Defects)

  • Immune-Mediated: Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA), Drug-induced.
  • Traumatic/Mechanical: Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia (DIC, TTP), Cardiac valve hemolysis.
  • Infections: Malaria.
  • Membrane Defect: Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria (PNH) - *Unique acquired intrinsic defect*.

2. GENERAL PATHOGENESIS AND CLINICAL FEATURES

Pathogenesis

Hemolysis can occur in two sites:

  1. Extravascular Hemolysis (Most common): Occurs within phagocytes of the spleen and liver. RBCs are less deformable or coated with antibodies (e.g., Spherocytosis, Sickle cell).
    • Consequence: Jaundice, Splenomegaly.
  2. Intravascular Hemolysis: RBCs lyse within the blood vessels due to mechanical injury or complement fixation (e.g., Malaria, Transfusion reaction).
    • Consequence: Hemoglobinemia, Hemoglobinuria (Red urine), Hemosiderinuria.

Clinical Features

  • Pallor, Fatigue (Anemia).
  • Jaundice (Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia).
  • Splenomegaly (Work hypertrophy of spleen).
  • Pigment Gallstones (Bilirubin stones).
  • Dark Urine (in intravascular hemolysis).

Hematologic Indices & Lab Findings

  • Hemoglobin: Low.
  • Reticulocyte Count: Increased (> 2%). Indicates marrow response.
  • Bilirubin: Increased (Unconjugated/Indirect).
  • LDH (Lactate Dehydrogenase): Increased (released from lysed RBCs).
  • Haptoglobin: Decreased (binds free hemoglobin).
  • Urine: Urobilinogen increased. Hemoglobinuria (if intravascular).

3. SICKLE CELL ANEMIA (HbS)

Definition: A hereditary hemoglobinopathy caused by a structural defect in the beta-globin chain.

Pathogenesis

  • Mutation: Point mutation at position 6 of the β-globin gene. Valine substitutes Glutamic acid (Glu → Val).
  • Mechanism:
    1. Upon deoxygenation (hypoxia, acidosis, dehydration), HbS molecules polymerize into long needle-like fibers.
    2. This distorts the RBC into a Sickle shape (Drepanocyte).
    3. Sickling causes membrane damage (Ca2+ influx) and makes RBCs sticky.
  • Consequences:
    • Vaso-Occlusion: Sickle cells block microvessels → Ischemia & Pain crises.
    • Hemolysis: Rigid cells are destroyed in the spleen (Extravascular).

Clinical Features

  • Vaso-occlusive Crises: Severe bone pain (Hand-Foot syndrome in infants), Chest syndrome, Stroke.
  • Autosplenectomy: Spleen initially enlarged, then fibrosed and shrunken due to repeated infarcts.
  • Aplastic Crisis: Parvovirus B19 infection causes temporary marrow shutdown.

Peripheral Blood Picture

  • Sickle Cells (Drepanocytes): Crescent/boat-shaped cells.
  • Target Cells.
  • Howell-Jolly Bodies: Nuclear remnants seen after autosplenectomy.
  • Polychromasia: Reticulocytes.

4. THALASSEMIA

Definition: A group of inherited disorders caused by reduced or absent synthesis of one or more globin chains (α or β).

Pathogenesis (Beta-Thalassemia Major)

  • Defect: Mutation in β-globin gene → Absent (β0) or reduced (β+) β-chains.
  • Imbalance: Excess unsubstituted α-chains precipitate within RBC precursors.
  • Consequences:
    • Ineffective Erythropoiesis: Marrow precursors die before release (Apoptosis).
    • Hemolysis: Released RBCs are destroyed by the spleen.
    • Iron Overload: Due to repeated transfusions and increased absorption.

Clinical Features

  • Severe anemia starting at 6-9 months (after HbF declines).
  • Skeletal Deformities: "Chipmunk facies", "Hair-on-end" appearance on X-ray (Marrow expansion).
  • Hepatosplenomegaly.

Peripheral Blood Picture

  • Microcytic Hypochromic Anemia (MCV < 70 fL).
  • Target Cells (Codocytes): Prominent.
  • Basophilic Stippling: Aggregated ribosomes.
  • Nucleated RBCs: Normoblasts seen in peripheral blood (Marrow stress).

5. ACQUIRED HEMOLYTIC ANEMIA

A. Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA)

Caused by antibodies against own RBCs. Diagnosed by Coombs Test (DAT).

  • Warm Antibody Type (IgG): Active at 37°C. Spleen destroys IgG-coated RBCs.
    Etiology: SLE, Lymphoma, Drugs (Methyldopa).
    Smear: Microspherocytes (Spleen bites off membrane).
  • Cold Antibody Type (IgM): Active at 4°C. IgM fixes complement.
    Etiology: Mycoplasma pneumonia, Mononucleosis.
    Smear: RBC Agglutination (Clumping).

B. Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia (MAHA)

Mechanical fragmentation of RBCs as they pass through fibrin strands in blocked vessels.

  • Etiology: DIC, TTP, HUS, Malignant Hypertension.
  • Smear: Schistocytes (Helmet cells, fragmented RBCs).

6. PERIPHERAL BLOOD PICTURE SUMMARY

Condition Characteristic RBC Shape/Finding
Hereditary Spherocytosis Spherocytes (Small, dark, no central pallor).
Sickle Cell Anemia Sickle cells, Howell-Jolly bodies.
Thalassemia Target cells, Nucleated RBCs, Basophilic Stippling.
G6PD Deficiency Bite cells, Blister cells (Heinz bodies).
Microangiopathic (DIC/TTP) Schistocytes (Helmet cells).
Autoimmune (Warm) Microspherocytes, Polychromasia.
Autoimmune (Cold) RBC Agglutination.

7. PREPARATION OF PERIPHERAL SMEAR

To identify hemolytic anemia, a well-made smear is essential.

Steps (Wedge Technique)

  1. Sample: Use EDTA venous blood or fresh finger prick.
  2. Drop: Place a small drop of blood 1 cm from the end of a clean glass slide.
  3. Spreader: Place a smooth-edged "spreader" slide at a 30-45° angle in front of the drop.
  4. Spread: Pull back to touch the drop (let it spread along width), then push forward rapidly and smoothly.
  5. Dry & Stain: Air dry. Fix with Methanol. Stain with Leishman or Giemsa.
  6. Examine: Look at the Tail (Feathered edge) where cells are separated. Look for Polychromasia (blue-grey young RBCs) and specific shapes (Spherocytes, Sickle cells).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Hemolytic Anemias. Describe the pathogenesis and lab diagnosis of Sickle Cell Anemia.

Introduction: Hemolytic anemias are characterized by reduced RBC lifespan. They are classified into Intracorpuscular (Hereditary) and Extracorpuscular (Acquired) defects.

Body:
Pathogenesis of SCA: It is caused by a point mutation (Glu → Val) in the β-globin chain. Deoxygenation causes HbS to polymerize, distorting RBCs into rigid sickles. These obstruct vessels (Vaso-occlusive crisis) and are destroyed in the spleen (Hemolysis).
Lab Diagnosis:

  • CBC: Low Hb, High Retic count.
  • Smear: Sickle cells, Target cells, Howell-Jolly bodies.
  • Sickling Test: Sodium metabisulfite induces sickling.
  • Electrophoresis: HbS band present (Definitive).

Conclusion: SCA is a severe hemolytic disorder with multisystem ischemic complications.

Q2: Discuss the Peripheral Blood findings in Thalassemia and Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia.

Introduction: Peripheral smear examination is a rapid diagnostic tool for distinguishing hemolytic anemias.

Body:
1. Thalassemia Major: The smear shows severe microcytic hypochromic anemia. Key features are Target cells, Nucleated RBCs (erythroblastemia due to marrow stress), and Basophilic Stippling. Anisopoikilocytosis is marked.
2. Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (Warm Type): The hallmark finding is the Spherocyte (small, hyperchromic RBC without central pallor), formed when splenic macrophages remove antibody-coated membrane. Polychromasia (Reticulocytes) is prominent. Cold type shows Agglutination.

Conclusion: Spherocytes suggest immune hemolysis (Coombs positive), while Target cells suggest hemoglobinopathy.
