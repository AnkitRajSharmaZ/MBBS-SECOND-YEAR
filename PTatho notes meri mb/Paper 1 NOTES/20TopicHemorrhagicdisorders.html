TOPIC 20: HEMORRHAGIC DISORDERS

Syllabus Scope: Normal Hemostasis, Etiology/Pathogenesis of Vascular and Platelet Disorders (ITP, TTP), Coagulation Disorders (Hemophilia A/B, vWD), Differentiation, and DIC.

1. NORMAL HEMOSTASIS

Definition: Hemostasis is the physiological process that stops bleeding at the site of vascular injury while maintaining normal blood flow elsewhere in the circulation.

The 4 Key Steps of Hemostasis

  1. Arteriolar Vasoconstriction: Immediate neurogenic reflex and release of Endothelin (potent vasoconstrictor) from endothelium reduces blood flow to the injured area.
  2. Primary Hemostasis (Platelet Plug):
    • Adhesion: von Willebrand Factor (vWF) acts as a bridge between the platelet receptor (GpIb) and exposed subendothelial collagen.
    • Activation: Platelets change shape (from discs to spiky spheres) and release granules (ADP, Thromboxane A2).
    • Aggregation: Fibrinogen binds to the platelet receptor (GpIIb-IIIa), linking platelets together to form the initial soft plug.
  3. Secondary Hemostasis (Fibrin Clot): Tissue Factor (Factor III) released from injured cells activates the coagulation cascade. This generates Thrombin, which converts soluble Fibrinogen into insoluble Fibrin strands, cementing the platelet plug.
  4. Clot Stabilization & Resorption: The clot contracts (Retraction). Later, the fibrinolytic system (Plasmin) dissolves the clot to restore flow.

Role of Endothelium

  • Antithrombotic Properties (Normal): Secrete PGI2 (Prostacyclin) and NO (Nitric Oxide) to inhibit platelets; Thrombomodulin to activate Protein C (anticoagulant).
  • Prothrombotic Properties (Injured): Secrete vWF and Tissue Factor to promote clotting.

2. CLASSIFICATION OF HEMORRHAGIC DISORDERS

Bleeding disorders are caused by defects in vessel walls, platelets, or coagulation factors.

A. Vascular Disorders (Non-Thrombocytopenic Purpura)

Bleeding is due to vessel wall fragility, not platelet or factor deficiency.

  • Infections: Meningococcemia (Vasculitis), Rickettsia.
  • Drug Reactions: Leukocytoclastic vasculitis.
  • Scurvy (Vitamin C Deficiency): Defective collagen synthesis leads to fragile vessels and perifollicular hemorrhage.
  • Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP): An immune complex disease (IgA) causing vasculitis in children. Features: Purpuric rash, abdominal pain, arthritis, glomerulonephritis.
  • Hereditary Hemorrhagic Telangiectasia (Weber-Osler-Rendu): Autosomal dominant. Dilated tortuous vessels with thin walls (bleeding from nose/tongue).

B. Platelet Disorders

Characterized by Mucocutaneous Bleeding (Petechiae, Purpura, Epistaxis, Menorrhagia).

1. Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

Definition: An autoimmune disorder with isolated thrombocytopenia.

  • Pathogenesis:
    • Autoantibodies (IgG) form against platelet membrane glycoproteins (GpIIb-IIIa or GpIb).
    • Opsonized platelets are destroyed by Fc-receptor-bearing macrophages in the Spleen (Extravascular hemolysis).
    • The spleen is the site of both antibody production and platelet destruction.
  • Classification:
    • Acute ITP: Children, post-viral, self-limiting.
    • Chronic ITP: Adult women (20-40y), insidious, long-lasting.
  • Morphology:
    • Peripheral Blood: Thrombocytopenia, large platelets (Megathrombocytes).
    • Bone Marrow: Increased Megakaryocytes (compensatory hyperplasia).
    • Spleen: Normal size (Splenomegaly is notably absent).

2. Thrombotic Microangiopathies (TTP & HUS)

  • Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP): Deficiency of ADAMTS13 enzyme (cleaves vWF multimers). Large vWF multimers cause platelet aggregation → Microthrombi. Pentad: Fever, Thrombocytopenia, Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia, Neurologic deficits, Renal failure.
  • Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS): Caused by E. coli O157:H7 Shiga toxin. Dominant renal failure.

3. Functional Platelet Defects (Qualitative)

  • Bernard-Soulier Syndrome: Defect in GpIb (Adhesion). Giant platelets.
  • Glanzmann’s Thrombasthenia: Defect in GpIIb-IIIa (Aggregation).

C. Coagulation Disorders

Characterized by Deep Tissue Bleeding (Hemarthrosis, Muscle hematomas).

1. Hemophilia A (Classic Hemophilia)

Genetics: X-linked Recessive. Mutation in F8 gene (Xq28). Most common severe mutation is Intron 22 Inversion.

Pathogenesis: Deficiency of Factor VIII disrupts the Intrinsic Pathway (Factor X activation complex).

Clinical Features:

  • Hemarthrosis: Spontaneous bleeding into joints (knees, elbows) → Synovitis → Ankylosis (deformity).
  • Muscle Hematomas.
  • Delayed post-traumatic bleeding.
  • Note: Platelet count and Bleeding Time are Normal.

Lab Diagnosis: Prolonged aPTT. Normal PT. Low Factor VIII assay.

2. Hemophilia B (Christmas Disease)

Clinically identical to Hemophilia A but caused by Factor IX deficiency. X-linked recessive. Requires Factor IX replacement.

3. Von Willebrand Disease (vWD)

Definition: Most common inherited bleeding disorder. Autosomal Dominant.

Pathogenesis: Defect in vWF. vWF has a dual role: 1. Platelet Adhesion (binds GpIb). 2. Carrier for Factor VIII (stabilizes it).

Clinical: Mixed picture of platelet-type bleeding (mucosal) and coagulation-type bleeding (if severe Factor VIII loss).
Labs: Prolonged Bleeding Time AND Prolonged aPTT (Unique feature). Ristocetin cofactor assay is abnormal.

3. DIFFERENTIATION: PLATELET VS. COAGULATION DISORDERS

Essential for clinical diagnosis.

Feature Platelet / Vascular Disorders Coagulation Factor Disorders
Examples ITP, TTP, Bernard-Soulier. Hemophilia A/B, Vitamin K Deficiency.
Site of Bleeding Superficial: Skin, Mucous membranes (Gums, Nose, Menorrhagia). Deep: Joints (Hemarthrosis), Muscles, Retroperitoneal.
Lesions Petechiae (< 2mm), Purpura, Ecchymoses (small). Large spreading Hematomas. Petechiae are rare.
Bleeding after trauma Immediate. Delayed (starts hours later due to unstable clot).
Family History Rare (mostly acquired). Common (often X-linked).
Screening Tests Abnormal Bleeding Time. Low Platelets. Prolonged PT or aPTT. Normal Platelets.

4. DISSEMINATED INTRAVASCULAR COAGULATION (DIC)

Definition: An acute, subacute, or chronic thrombo-hemorrhagic disorder characterized by the excessive activation of coagulation, leading to the formation of widespread microvascular thrombi and simultaneous depletion of clotting factors and platelets (Consumption Coagulopathy).

Etiology (Triggers)

  • Obstetric Complications: Abruptio placentae, Amniotic fluid embolism, Retained dead fetus (Tissue Factor release).
  • Infections: Gram-negative Sepsis (Endotoxins), Meningococcemia (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome), Malaria.
  • Neoplasms: Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia (M3), Mucin-secreting Adenocarcinomas (Pancreas, Stomach).
  • Massive Tissue Injury: Trauma, Burns, Crush injury.

Pathogenesis (Mechanism)

  1. Systemic Activation: Widespread release of Tissue Factor or endothelial injury triggers uncontrolled generation of Thrombin.
  2. Microthrombosis: Fibrin is deposited in microcirculation → Ischemia & Organ Failure (Kidney, Brain, Lung).
    • Microangiopathic Hemolysis: RBCs are fragmented by fibrin strands → Schistocytes (Helmet cells).
  3. Consumption: Platelets and factors (V, VIII, Fibrinogen) are "consumed" rapidly.
  4. Fibrinolysis: Plasminogen is activated to Plasmin to dissolve clots → Releases Fibrin Degradation Products (FDPs/D-Dimer). FDPs act as anticoagulants.
  5. Hemorrhage: The combination of factor consumption + FDP inhibition results in severe bleeding from multiple sites (IV sites, gums).

Laboratory Findings (Diagnosis)

  • Platelet Count: Decreased (Thrombocytopenia).
  • Prothrombin Time (PT) & aPTT: Prolonged (Consumption of factors).
  • Fibrinogen: Reduced (Hypofibrinogenemia).
  • D-Dimer / FDPs: Raised (Specific marker for fibrinolysis).
  • Peripheral Smear: Schistocytes (Fragmented RBCs).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Hemophilia A. Discuss its Inheritance, Clinical Features, and Laboratory Diagnosis.

Introduction: Hemophilia A is the most common hereditary coagulation disorder, caused by a deficiency of Factor VIII.

Body:
Inheritance: X-linked Recessive. Affects males; females are carriers. 30% cases are new mutations.
Clinical Features: Severity depends on factor levels (<1% Severe, 1-5% Moderate, >5% Mild).

  • Hemarthrosis: Bleeding into weight-bearing joints (knees) causing pain, swelling, and eventual ankylosis.
  • Hematomas: Deep muscle bleeds.
  • Post-surgical bleeding: Excessive bleeding after circumcision or dental extraction.

Lab Diagnosis:
  • Screening: Prolonged aPTT. Normal PT, Bleeding time, and Platelet count.
  • Confirmatory: Factor VIII assay (Activity level). Mixing studies correct the aPTT (unless inhibitors are present).

Conclusion: Management involves Factor VIII replacement therapy. Inhibitor development is a major complication.

Q2: Define DIC. Describe its Etiopathogenesis and Stages.

Introduction: Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) is a complex thrombo-hemorrhagic syndrome occurring as a secondary complication of systemic disease.

Body:
Etiopathogenesis:

  • Initiation: Systemic activation of coagulation by Tissue Factor (e.g., in trauma, obstetric cases) or Endothelial injury (e.g., sepsis).
  • Propagation: Widespread formation of fibrin microthrombi leads to organ ischemia (e.g., Cortical necrosis of kidneys).
  • Consumption: Massive clotting consumes platelets and factors V, VIII, leading to a deficiency state.
  • Fibrinolysis: Secondary activation of plasminogen produces FDPs (D-dimer), which possess anticoagulant properties, aggravating bleeding.

Stages (Clinical):
  • Thrombotic Phase: Microvascular occlusion → Organ failure.
  • Hemorrhagic Phase: Consumption of factors → Spontaneous bleeding.

Conclusion: DIC is a medical emergency with high mortality. Treatment focuses on the underlying cause and supportive blood products.
