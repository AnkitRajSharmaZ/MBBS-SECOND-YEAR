Healing & Repair

MBBS Pathology • General Pathology | Detailed Notes for University Exams

1. Introduction

Definitions

  • Healing (Repair): The restoration of tissue architecture and function after an injury.
  • Regeneration: The complete replacement of damaged cells by new cells of the same type, resulting in perfect restoration of normal function. (e.g., Liver regeneration after partial hepatectomy).
  • Repair by Scar Formation (Fibrosis): Replacement of injured tissue by connective tissue (collagen), leading to scar formation. This occurs when the injured tissues are incapable of complete restitution or the supporting structures are severely damaged.

Healing involves two distinct processes:

  1. Regeneration: Growth of cells to replace lost tissues.
  2. Fibrosis (Scarring): Laying down of collagen.

2. Regeneration & Cell Cycle

The ability of tissues to regenerate depends on the proliferative capacity of their cells. Based on this, cells are divided into three groups:

Classification of Cells (Based on Proliferative Capacity)

1. Labile Cells (Continuously Dividing Cells)

These cells are continuously being lost and replaced by maturation from stem cells and by proliferation of mature cells.

  • Examples: Hematopoietic cells in bone marrow, Surface epithelia (skin, oral cavity, vagina, cervix), Epithelium of GIT and urinary tract.
  • Significance: Have excellent capacity for regeneration.

2. Stable Cells (Quiescent Cells)

These cells are normally in the G0 stage (resting) and have a low rate of replication. However, they can undergo rapid division in response to injury (re-enter G1 phase).

  • Examples: Parenchyma of solid organs (Liver, Kidney, Pancreas), Endothelial cells, Fibroblasts, Smooth muscle cells.
  • Significance: Regenerate well if the stromal framework is preserved.

3. Permanent Cells (Non-dividing Cells)

These cells have left the cell cycle and cannot undergo mitotic division in postnatal life.

  • Examples: Neurons, Cardiac muscle cells, Skeletal muscle cells.
  • Significance: Injury to these tissues always results in Scar Formation (Repair), never regeneration.

3. Repair by Scar Formation (Fibrosis)

If tissue injury is severe or affects chronic inflammation, repair occurs by replacement with connective tissue. It involves four sequential steps:

  1. Angiogenesis (Neovascularization): Formation of new blood vessels from pre-existing vessels.
    • Driven by growth factors like VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor) and FGF.
    • Provides oxygen and nutrients to the healing tissue.
  2. Formation of Granulation Tissue: This is the hallmark of healing.
    • Definition: Highly vascularized, loose connective tissue containing new capillaries, proliferating fibroblasts, and inflammatory cells (macrophages).
    • Gross Appearance: Pink, soft, granular appearance on the surface of wounds.
  3. Maturation and Remodeling:
    • Granulation tissue is progressively replaced by a mature scar.
    • Collagen deposition increases, and vascularity decreases.
    • Remodeling: Balance between collagen synthesis and degradation. Mediated by MMPs (Matrix Metalloproteinases).

4. Cutaneous Wound Healing

Wound healing in the skin illustrates the general principles of repair. It is divided into two types:

A. Healing by First Intention (Primary Union)

Definition: Healing of a clean, uninfected surgical incision approximated by surgical sutures.

  • Conditions: Minimal tissue loss, clean wound margins, no infection, minimal clot.
  • Mechanism: Epithelial regeneration dominates over fibrosis.

Sequence of Events:

  • Immediately (0 hours): Incision causes bleeding. Coagulation pathway activates. A blood clot containing fibrin and platelets fills the gap. Surface clot dries to form a scab.
  • 24 Hours (Day 1):
    • Neutrophils appear at margins (acute inflammation).
    • Basal cells of epidermis show mitotic activity and migrate to meet in the midline beneath the scab.
  • Day 3:
    • Neutrophils are replaced by Macrophages (clean up debris).
    • Granulation tissue invades the incision space.
    • Collagen fibers appear vertically at margins.
  • Day 5:
    • Maximum Neovascularization occurs.
    • Collagen fibers bridge the incision.
    • Epidermis recovers normal thickness.
  • Week 2:
    • Accumulation of collagen and proliferation of fibroblasts continue.
    • Leukocytes, edema, and vascularity diminish (blanching begins).
  • End of Month 1:
    • Scar is cellular connective tissue covered by normal epidermis.
    • Dermal appendages (hair follicles, glands) are permanently lost in the line of incision.
    • Tensile strength increases over months.

B. Healing by Second Intention (Secondary Union)

Definition: Healing of large open wounds with extensive tissue loss, necrosis, or infection (e.g., Abscess, large ulcer, infarction).

  • Conditions: Extensive tissue loss, margins cannot be approximated, gaping wound.
  • Mechanism: Combination of regeneration and scarring.

Key Differences from Primary Union:

  1. Larger Clot and Debris: Requires more intense inflammatory reaction to clean up.
  2. Much more Granulation Tissue: Needed to fill the large gap. Resulting in a larger scar.
  3. Wound Contraction: This is a unique feature of secondary union.
    • The wound size decreases by 50-80% within weeks.
    • Mediated by Myofibroblasts (modified fibroblasts with contractile properties like smooth muscle).

Differences between Primary and Secondary Union

Feature Primary Union Secondary Union
Nature of Wound Clean, surgical incision, margins approximated Large, infected, gaping, margins not approximated
Tissue Loss Minimal Extensive
Granulation Tissue Scanty Abundant
Wound Contraction Absent / Minimal Prominent (due to Myofibroblasts)
Scar Small, linear, neat Large, irregular
Healing Time Short Prolonged
Outcome Excellent Often poor (contractures)

5. Factors Affecting Wound Healing

A. Systemic Factors

  • Nutrition: Protein deficiency and Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) inhibit collagen synthesis. Zinc is required for DNA synthesis.
  • Metabolic Status: Diabetes Mellitus is associated with delayed healing and increased risk of infection.
  • Circulatory Status: Inadequate blood supply (atherosclerosis, venous stasis) impairs healing.
  • Hormones: Glucocorticoids (Steroids) have anti-inflammatory effects and inhibit collagen synthesis, resulting in weak scars.

B. Local Factors

  • Infection: The single most important cause of delayed healing. Prolongs inflammation.
  • Mechanical Factors: Early motion of wound can separate edges.
  • Foreign Bodies: Sutures, glass, steel fragments obstruct healing.
  • Size, Location, and Type of wound: Wounds in rich vascular areas (face) heal faster than poor vascular areas (foot).

Complications of Wound Healing

  1. Deficient Scar Formation:
    • Wound Dehiscence: Rupture of a wound (common in abdominal surgery due to coughing/vomiting).
    • Ulceration: Due to inadequate vascularization.
  2. Excessive Formation of Repair Components:
    • Keloid: Accumulation of exuberant amounts of collagen giving rise to a raised scar that grows beyond the boundaries of the original wound. (Genetic predisposition, common in African Americans).
    • Hypertrophic Scar: Excess collagen but confined within the boundaries of the wound. Often regresses.
    • Proud Flesh (Exuberant Granulation Tissue): Excessive granulation tissue protrudes above the skin level and blocks re-epithelialization.
  3. Contractures: Excessive wound contraction leads to deformities (common in burns, palms, soles).

Q2. Describe the factors affecting wound healing.

Answer:

Wound healing is a complex process influenced by multiple factors which can be classified into Systemic and Local factors.

A. Systemic Factors:

  1. Nutrition:
    • Protein Deficiency: Impairs collagen synthesis.
    • Vitamin C Deficiency (Scurvy): Essential for hydroxylation of proline/lysine in collagen. Deficiency leads to weak wounds that break open.
    • Zinc Deficiency: Required for enzyme activity and DNA synthesis.
  2. Metabolic Status: Diabetes Mellitus is a major cause of delayed healing due to microangiopathy (poor blood flow) and susceptibility to infection.
  3. Circulatory Status: Adequate blood supply is crucial. Arteriosclerosis or venous stasis (varicose veins) delays healing.
  4. Hormones: Glucocorticoids have anti-inflammatory effects and inhibit collagen synthesis, resulting in weak scar strength. However, they are useful in reducing corneal scarring.
  5. Age: Healing is generally faster in young individuals.

B. Local Factors:

  1. Infection: The most common cause of delayed healing. It prolongs the inflammatory phase and increases tissue destruction.
  2. Mechanical Factors: Early mobilization or tension on wound edges can cause dehiscence (separation).
  3. Foreign Bodies: Presence of steel fragments, glass, or bone pieces obstructs the healing process.
  4. Size and Location: Small wounds heal faster. Wounds in highly vascular areas (e.g., face) heal faster than those in poorly vascularized areas (e.g., foot, tendon).

Q3. Differentiate between Keloid and Hypertrophic Scar.

Answer: Both are complications of wound healing characterized by excessive collagen accumulation.
