TOPIC 11: ENVIRONMENTAL & NUTRITIONAL DISEASES

Syllabus Scope: Pathogenesis of disorders caused by Air Pollution, Tobacco, Alcohol, Obesity, and Protein-Calorie Malnutrition (PCM).

1. AIR POLLUTION

Air pollution involves exposure to airborne substances that have adverse effects on health. The lungs are the primary target.

A. Mechanism of Injury

The pathogenic effects depend on the size of the particle and its solubility.

  1. Particulate Matter (PM):
    • Coarse Particles (>10μm): Trapped in nose/upper airways (mucociliary elevator).
    • Fine Particles (< 2.5μm, PM2.5): Reach the alveoli. Phagocytosed by Alveolar Macrophages → Release inflammatory mediators (TNF, IL-6) → Systemic Inflammation.
  2. Ozone (O3): Generates free radicals → Epithelial injury → Inflammation. Harmful to asthmatics.
  3. Carbon Monoxide (CO): Binds Hemoglobin with 200x affinity compared to O2 → Carboxyhemoglobin → Systemic Hypoxia.

B. Pathogenesis of Specific Disorders

  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD): Chronic inflammation (Neutrophils, Macrophages) + Protease-antiprotease imbalance (oxidants inactive α1-antitrypsin) → Alveolar wall destruction (Emphysema).
  • Pneumoconiosis: Fibrotic lung reaction to inhaled dust (Silica, Asbestos, Coal). Macrophages ingest dust → Fibrogenic cytokines (TGF-β) → Fibrosis.
  • Lung Cancer: Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) cause DNA mutations (p53, KRAS).

2. TOBACCO SMOKING

Smoking is the most preventable cause of human death. It contains >4000 chemicals, including 60 carcinogens.

Pathogenesis of Smoking-Related Diseases

  1. Carcinogenesis:
    • Polycyclic Hydrocarbons & Nitrosamines: Metabolized by CYP450 enzymes to electrophilic intermediates → Form DNA Adducts → Mutations in Oncogenes (KRAS) and Tumor Suppressors (p53).
    • Targets: Lung, Larynx, Oral cavity, Esophagus, Bladder, Pancreas.
  2. Chronic Lung Disease (COPD):
    • Smoke attracts Neutrophils → Release Elastase.
    • Smoke creates ROS → Inactivates α1-Antitrypsin (the antiprotease).
    • Result: Unchecked elastase destroys alveolar walls → Centrilobular Emphysema.
  3. Cardiovascular Disease (Atherosclerosis):
    • Endothelial Injury: Direct toxicity, hypoxia (CO), oxidative stress.
    • Pro-thrombotic State: Increased platelet aggregation.
    • Lipid Profile: Increases LDL, decreases HDL.
    • Vasoconstriction: Nicotine stimulates catecholamine release → Increased HR and BP.

3. ALCOHOL (ETHANOL)

Chronic alcoholism leads to multi-organ damage, primarily affecting the liver.

A. Metabolism of Ethanol

  1. Alcohol Dehydrogenase (ADH): Main pathway in cytosol. Converts Ethanol → Acetaldehyde (Produces NADH).
  2. Microsomal Ethanol Oxidizing System (CYP2E1): Active at high alcohol levels. Produces ROS.
  3. Catalase: Minor pathway in peroxisomes.

B. Pathogenesis of Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)

1. Alcoholic Steatosis (Fatty Liver):

  • Increased NADH/NAD+ ratio → Shunts substrate away from catabolism toward lipid biosynthesis.
  • Impaired assembly/secretion of lipoproteins.
  • Result: Accumulation of triglycerides in hepatocytes (Reversible).

2. Alcoholic Hepatitis:

  • Acetaldehyde Toxicity: Forms adducts with proteins → Immune attack.
  • Oxidative Stress: ROS from CYP2E1 damage membranes (lipid peroxidation).
  • Inflammation: Gut permeability increases ("Leaky gut") → Endotoxin (LPS) reaches liver → Activates Kupffer Cells → Release TNF-α, IL-6.
  • Mallory-Denk Bodies: Clumped intermediate filaments in damaged cells.

3. Alcoholic Cirrhosis:

  • Chronic inflammation activates Stellate Cells (Ito cells) → Transformation into Myofibroblasts → Collagen deposition (Fibrosis) → Nodules.

4. PROTEIN-ENERGY MALNUTRITION (PEM)

A spectrum of disorders caused by inadequate intake of protein and/or calories. Severe forms are Marasmus and Kwashiorkor.

5. OBESITY

Definition: Accumulation of excess body fat sufficient to adversely affect health (BMI > 30 kg/mÂ²).

A. Pathogenesis

Obesity is a disorder of energy regulation involving the Leptin-Melanocortin pathway.

  1. Neurohumoral Mechanisms:
    • Leptin: "Satiety hormone" from Adipose tissue. Inhibits appetite. Obese individuals often have Leptin Resistance (high leptin, but brain doesn't respond).
    • Ghrelin: "Hunger hormone" from Stomach. Increases appetite.
    • Adiponectin: "Good adipokine". Increases insulin sensitivity. Decreased in obesity.
  2. Adipose Tissue as an Endocrine Organ:
    • Excess adipose tissue (especially visceral fat) is pro-inflammatory.
    • Macrophages infiltrate fat and secrete TNF-α, IL-6 → Chronic Low-Grade Inflammation → Insulin Resistance.
  3. Gut Microbiome: Altered in obese individuals (harvest more energy from food).

B. Consequences of Obesity

  1. Metabolic Syndrome: Central obesity + Insulin resistance + Dyslipidemia (High TG, Low HDL) + Hypertension. → High risk of Type 2 Diabetes.
  2. Cardiovascular: Atherosclerosis, Myocardial Infarction, Heart Failure.
  3. Liver: Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) → NASH → Cirrhosis. (Mechanism: Insulin resistance increases free fatty acid flux to liver).
  4. Respiratory: Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA), Pickwickian syndrome (Hypoventilation).
  5. Cancer: Increased risk of Esophagus, Endometrium, Gallbladder, and Kidney cancers. (Due to Hyperinsulinemia and high IGF-1).
  6. Musculoskeletal: Osteoarthritis (wear and tear).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Malnutrition. Differentiate between Kwashiorkor and Marasmus with pathogenesis.

Introduction: Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM) is a spectrum of disorders caused by inadequate intake of protein and calories. It causes significant morbidity in developing countries.

Body:
1. Marasmus (Severe Wasting):

  • Etiology: Deficiency of both calories and protein (Starvation).
  • Pathogenesis: Adaptive response. The body breaks down fat (lipolysis) and muscle (proteolysis) to provide energy. Serum albumin is maintained.
  • Clinical: Loss of subcutaneous fat, muscle wasting, "Old man" face, No edema.

2. Kwashiorkor (Edematous Malnutrition):
  • Etiology: Protein deficiency usually with adequate calories (e.g., weaning on starchy diet).
  • Pathogenesis: Maladaptive. Severe Hypoalbuminemia leads to decreased oncotic pressure and edema. Reduced synthesis of lipoproteins (ApoB) leads to failure of fat export from liver → Fatty Liver.
  • Clinical: Generalized edema, hepatomegaly (fatty liver), skin lesions ("Flaky paint"), hair changes ("Flag sign").

Conclusion: Kwashiorkor is clinically more deceptive due to edema but biologically more severe due to visceral protein depletion.

Q2: Discuss the Pathogenesis of Alcoholic Liver Disease.

Introduction: Alcohol is a direct hepatotoxin. Its metabolism generates toxic byproducts that damage the liver parenchyma.

Body:
1. Metabolism & Toxicity: Ethanol is converted to Acetaldehyde by ADH and CYP2E1. Acetaldehyde causes lipid peroxidation and forms protein adducts, triggering an immune response.
2. Steatosis (Fatty Change): Increased NADH levels inhibit fatty acid oxidation and promote lipid synthesis. Fat accumulates in hepatocytes.
3. Inflammation (Hepatitis): Alcohol increases gut permeability ("leaky gut"), allowing endotoxins to enter the portal circulation. This activates Kupffer cells to release TNF-α, causing hepatocellular necrosis and "Mallory-Denk bodies".
4. Fibrosis (Cirrhosis): Chronic inflammation activates hepatic Stellate cells, which transform into myofibroblasts and deposit collagen (fibrosis), distorting the liver architecture.

Conclusion: The spectrum progresses from reversible fatty liver to irreversible cirrhosis if alcohol consumption continues.

Q3: Describe the Pathogenesis of Obesity and its association with Type 2 Diabetes.

Introduction: Obesity is a state of excess adipose tissue mass. It acts as a chronic proinflammatory state.

Body:
Pathogenesis: It is a disorder of the energy homeostasis system. Key players include:

  • Leptin: Secreted by fat cells to suppress appetite. Obese individuals exhibit Leptin Resistance.
  • Adiponectin: Normally insulin-sensitizing; levels are reduced in obesity.
  • Inflammation: Excess visceral fat is infiltrated by macrophages, which secrete TNF-α and IL-6.

Association with Diabetes (Insulin Resistance):
The free fatty acids (FFAs) and inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α) released by visceral fat interfere with insulin signaling in muscle and liver tissues. This leads to Insulin Resistance. The pancreas compensates by secreting more insulin (Hyperinsulinemia), but eventually, beta-cells fail, leading to overt Type 2 Diabetes.

Conclusion: Obesity is the single most important risk factor for Type 2 Diabetes due to lipotoxicity and inflammation.
