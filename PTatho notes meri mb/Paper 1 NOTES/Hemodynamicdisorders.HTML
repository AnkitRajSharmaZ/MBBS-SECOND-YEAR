Hemodynamic Disorders

Subject: Pathology (MBBS Phase II) | Reference: Ramdas Nayak & Robbins

1. Edema

Definition

Edema is defined as an accumulation of excessive fluid in the interstitial tissue spaces or body cavities.

The fluid may be:

  • Transudate: Protein-poor fluid (Specific gravity < 1.012). Caused by hemodynamic disturbances (e.g., Heart failure).
  • Exudate: Protein-rich fluid (Specific gravity > 1.020). Caused by inflammation (increased vascular permeability).

Terminology for Fluid in Body Cavities

  • Hydrothorax: Fluid in pleural cavity.
  • Hydropericardium: Fluid in pericardial cavity.
  • Hydroperitoneum (Ascites): Fluid in peritoneal cavity.
  • Anasarca: Severe, generalized edema with profound subcutaneous tissue swelling.

Pathogenesis (Physiologic Mechanisms)

Edema is caused by an imbalance in Starling's Forces governing fluid movement across capillary walls. The normal fluid balance depends on:

  1. Increased Hydrostatic Pressure: Pushes fluid out of vessels.
    • Impaired Venous Return: Congestive Heart Failure (CHF), Constrictive Pericarditis, Venous obstruction (thrombosis).
    • Arteriolar Dilation: Heat, Neurohumoral dysregulation.
  2. Reduced Plasma Oncotic Pressure: Decreased albumin (which holds fluid in) allows fluid to leak out.
    • Decreased Synthesis: Liver Cirrhosis, Protein Malnutrition (Kwashiorkor).
    • Increased Loss: Nephrotic Syndrome (Proteinuria), Protein-losing enteropathy.
  3. Lymphatic Obstruction: Impairs drainage of interstitial fluid (Lymphedema).
    • Causes: Filariasis (Elephantiasis), Neoplastic obstruction, Post-surgical/Post-radiation.
  4. Sodium and Water Retention: Increases hydrostatic pressure (expanded volume) and reduces oncotic pressure (dilution).
    • Causes: Acute renal failure, Glomerulonephritis.
    • Mechanism: Activation of Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS).
  5. Inflammation: Increased vascular permeability (Exudate).

Clinical Correlations

Type of Edema Pathogenesis Clinical Features
Cardiac Edema (CHF) Increased hydrostatic pressure + Na+ retention (RAAS activation). Dependent edema (legs on standing, sacrum on lying down). Pitting edema.
Renal Edema (Nephrotic Syn.) Reduced oncotic pressure (Hypoalbuminemia). Generalized edema. Starts in loose tissues like eyelids (Periorbital edema).
Pulmonary Edema Increased hydrostatic pressure (Left Heart Failure). Fluid fills alveoli. Lungs become heavy, wet. Patient has dyspnea.
Cerebral Edema Trauma, Infection, Ischemia. Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP). Can cause herniation and death.

2. Hyperemia and Congestion

Both terms indicate a local increase in the volume of blood in a particular tissue.

Differences

Feature Hyperemia Congestion
Process Active process. Passive process.
Mechanism Increased arterial inflow (arteriolar dilation). Impaired venous outflow.
Color Red (Erythema) due to oxygenated blood. Blue-Red (Cyanosis) due to deoxygenated blood (stasis).
Examples Inflammation, Exercise, Blushing. Cardiac failure, Venous obstruction.

Morphology of Chronic Venous Congestion (CVC)

1. CVC Liver ("Nutmeg Liver")

  • Cause: Right-sided Heart Failure.
  • Gross: Cut surface shows a mottled appearance resembling a Nutmeg.
    • Red dots: Congested central veins (Centrilobular necrosis/hemorrhage).
    • Yellow areas: Normal or fatty peripheral parenchyma.
  • Microscopy:
    • Centrilobular necrosis: Hepatocytes around central vein die due to hypoxia.
    • Central vein: Dilated and congested.
    • Peripheral cells: May show fatty change.

2. CVC Lung

  • Cause: Left-sided Heart Failure (Mitral stenosis).
  • Gross: Lungs are heavy, wet, and brownish (Brown Induration).
  • Microscopy:
    • Alveolar septa are thickened and fibrotic.
    • Alveoli contain numerous Heart Failure Cells.
    • Heart Failure Cells: Hemosiderin-laden macrophages (macrophages that have eaten RBCs leaked from congested capillaries).

Hemorrhage

Definition: Extravasation of blood from vessels into the extravascular space.

  • Petechiae (1-2mm): Pinpoint hemorrhages (e.g., thrombocytopenia).
  • Purpura (3-5mm): Slightly larger (e.g., vasculitis).
  • Ecchymoses (1-2cm): Subcutaneous bruises. Color changes: Red (Hb) → Blue-Green (Bilirubin) → Gold-brown (Hemosiderin).
  • Hematoma: Massive accumulation of blood within a tissue.

3. Shock

Definition

Shock is a state of systemic hypoperfusion due to either reduced cardiac output or reduced effective circulating blood volume. It results in hypotension, impaired tissue perfusion, and cellular hypoxia.

Pathogenesis (Cellular Level)

Hypoperfusion → Cellular Hypoxia → Anaerobic Glycolysis → Lactic Acid accumulation → Metabolic Acidosis → Cell membrane injury → Lysosomal enzyme release → Cell Death.

Types of Shock

  1. Cardiogenic Shock: Failure of myocardial pump.
    • Causes: Myocardial Infarction (most common), Arrhythmias, Cardiac tamponade.
  2. Hypovolemic Shock: Loss of blood or plasma volume.
    • Causes: Hemorrhage, Severe burns, Diarrhea/Vomiting.
  3. Septic Shock: Systemic microbial infection.
    • Mechanism: Endotoxins (LPS) activate monocytes to release TNF & IL-1 → Systemic vasodilation (Hypotension) + Endothelial injury + DISseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).

Stages of Shock

Shock is a progressive disorder that evolves through three stages:

  1. Non-Progressive (Compensated) Stage: Reflex compensatory mechanisms maintain vital organ perfusion.
    • Mechanisms: Baroreceptor reflex (Tachycardia), RAAS activation (Fluid retention), Catecholamine release (Vasoconstriction).
    • Clinical: Cool clammy skin, Tachycardia.
  2. Progressive Stage: Tissue hypoperfusion and onset of circulatory imbalance.
    • Pathology: Anaerobic glycolysis → Lactic acidosis → Arterioles dilate → Blood pools in microcirculation.
    • Clinical: Confusion, Decreased urine output.
  3. Irreversible Stage: Severe cellular and tissue injury so that even if hemodynamic defects are corrected, survival is not possible.
    • Pathology: Lysosomal enzyme leakage, Acute Renal Failure (Acute Tubular Necrosis), Ischemic Bowel.

4. Thrombosis

Normal Hemostasis vs. Thrombosis

  • Hemostasis: Regulated process to stop bleeding at the site of vascular injury.
  • Thrombosis: Pathologic formation of a blood clot (thrombus) within uninjured vessels or excessive clotting after minor injury.

Etiopathogenesis: Virchow's Triad

Three primary abnormalities predispose to thrombosis:

  1. Endothelial Injury: The most important factor.
    • Exposes subendothelial collagen (Platelet adhesion) and Tissue Factor (Coagulation cascade).
    • Causes: Atherosclerosis, Hypertension, Vasculitis.
  2. Alterations in Blood Flow (Stasis or Turbulence):
    • Stasis: Seen in veins. Promotes endothelial activation and prevents washout of clotting factors.
    • Turbulence: Seen in arteries. Causes endothelial injury.
    • Causes: Aneurysms, MI (non-contractile muscle), Hyperviscosity.
  3. Hypercoagulability of Blood:
    • Primary (Genetic): Factor V Leiden mutation, Protein C or S deficiency.
    • Secondary (Acquired): Prolonged bed rest, Cancer, OCPs, DIC, Smoking.

Consequences (Fate) of a Thrombus

Once formed, a thrombus can undergo:

  1. Propagation: Accumulates more platelets and fibrin, causing vessel obstruction.
  2. Embolization: Dislodges and travels to other sites (very dangerous).
  3. Dissolution (Fibrinolysis): Cleared by plasmin (effective only for recent clots).
  4. Organization and Recanalization: Ingrowth of endothelial cells, smooth muscle, and fibroblasts. Capillary channels form within the thrombus to re-establish blood flow.

5. Embolism

Definition: An embolus is a detached intravascular solid, liquid, or gaseous mass that is carried by the blood to a site distant from its point of origin.

Common Types of Embolism

  1. Thromboembolism: (99% of cases) Dislodged thrombus.
    • Pulmonary Embolism (PE): Originates from Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) of legs. Can cause sudden death (Saddle embolus), pulmonary infarction, or pulmonary hypertension.
    • Systemic Thromboembolism: Originates from heart (mural thrombi) or aneurysms. Lodges in lower extremities (gangrene) or brain (stroke).
  2. Fat Embolism:
    • Cause: Fracture of long bones (release of marrow fat), severe burns.
    • Clinical: Fat Embolism Syndrome (Respiratory insufficiency, Neurologic symptoms, Petechial rash).
  3. Air Embolism:
    • Cause: Surgery, Decompression sickness ("The Bends" - Nitrogen bubbles).
    • Requires >100ml of air to cause symptoms.
  4. Amniotic Fluid Embolism:
    • Cause: Complication of labor. Infusion of amniotic fluid into maternal circulation via tear in placental membranes.
    • Pathology: Amniotic fluid contains thromboplastic substances → DIC.
    • Morphology: Fetal squamous cells, lanugo hair found in maternal pulmonary microcirculation.

6. Ischemia and Infarction

Ischemia

Definition: Deficiency of blood supply to a tissue due to obstruction of arterial inflow or venous outflow.

Effect: Causes hypoxia AND lack of nutrients/waste removal. Thus, ischemia injures tissue faster than hypoxia alone.

Infarction

Definition: An area of ischemic necrosis caused by occlusion of either the arterial supply or the venous drainage.

Etiology

  • Thrombotic or Embolic arterial occlusion (99% of cases).
  • Vasospasm.
  • Torsion of a vessel (e.g., Testicular torsion, Volvulus).

Types and Morphology of Infarcts

Classified based on color and presence of infection.

1. Red (Hemorrhagic) Infarcts

  • Occur in:
    • Venous occlusions (e.g., Ovarian torsion).
    • Loose tissues (e.g., Lung).
    • Tissues with dual circulation (e.g., Lung, Small Intestine).
  • Morphology: Red, firm, ill-defined. Microscopy shows frank hemorrhage along with necrosis.

2. White (Anemic) Infarcts

  • Occur in:
    • Arterial occlusions in solid organs with end-arterial circulation.
    • Examples: Heart, Spleen, Kidney.
  • Morphology:
    • Shape: Wedge-shaped with occluded vessel at apex and base at organ periphery.
    • Color: Pale/White (due to lack of blood).
    • Microscopy: Coagulative Necrosis. Preservation of basic cell outline (Ghost cells). (Exception: Brain shows Liquefactive Necrosis).

Clinical Effects

  • Myocardial Infarction: Heart failure, death.
  • Cerebral Infarction: Stroke.
  • Pulmonary Infarction: Hemoptysis, pleuritis.
  • Intestinal Infarction: Acute abdomen, peritonitis, shock.
