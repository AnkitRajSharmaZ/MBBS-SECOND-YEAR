TOPIC 12: INTRODUCTION TO HAEMATOLOGY

Syllabus Scope: Definition, Classification, Investigation of Anemia, and Peripheral Blood Smear Examination.

1. DEFINITION OF ANEMIA

Definition: Anemia is defined as a reduction in the red blood cell (RBC) mass or hemoglobin concentration below the normal range for the age and gender of the individual.

WHO Criteria for Anemia (Hemoglobin Cut-offs)

  • Men: Hb < 13 g/dL
  • Women (Non-pregnant): Hb < 12 g/dL
  • Pregnant Women: Hb < 11 g/dL
  • Infants (6 mo - 6 yrs): Hb < 11 g/dL

Functionally: It is a state where the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood is insufficient to meet the metabolic needs of the tissues (Hypoxia).

2. CLASSIFICATION OF ANEMIA

Anemia can be classified based on Morphology (Cell size/color) or Etiology (Cause).

A. Morphological Classification

Based on Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV) and MCHC.

Type MCV (fL) MCHC Examples
Microcytic Hypochromic < 80 Reduced Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA), Thalassemia, Sideroblastic Anemia, Anemia of Chronic Disease.
Normocytic Normochromic 80 - 100 Normal Acute Blood Loss, Aplastic Anemia, Hemolytic Anemia, Chronic Renal Failure.
Macrocytic > 100 Normal Megaloblastic Anemia (B12/Folate deficiency), Liver Disease, Hypothyroidism, Alcoholism.

B. Etiological Classification

Based on the mechanism of anemia.

  1. Impaired Red Cell Production (Hypoproliferative):
    • Nutritional deficiency: Iron, Vitamin B12, Folate.
    • Bone marrow failure: Aplastic anemia, Pure red cell aplasia.
    • Infiltration: Leukemia, Lymphoma.
  2. Increased Red Cell Destruction (Hemolytic):
    • Intracorpuscular (Defect inside RBC): Membrane defect (Spherocytosis), Enzyme defect (G6PD), Hemoglobinopathy (Sickle cell).
    • Extracorpuscular (Defect outside RBC): Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia, Malaria, Hypersplenism.
  3. Blood Loss (Hemorrhagic):
    • Acute (Trauma).
    • Chronic (Peptic ulcer, Piles, Hookworm).

3. INVESTIGATION OF ANEMIA

A systematic approach is required to identify the cause of anemia.

A. Preliminary Investigations (Screening Tests)

  1. Complete Blood Count (CBC):
    • Hemoglobin (Hb): Determines severity.
    • RBC Count & Hematocrit (PCV): Parallel to Hb.
    • Red Cell Indices (MCV, MCH, MCHC): Crucial for classification.
    • RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width): Measures variation in RBC size (Anisocytosis). High in IDA, normal in Thalassemia trait.
  2. Peripheral Blood Smear (PBS) Examination: Confirms the automated findings and identifies abnormal shapes (e.g., Sickle cells, Spherocytes).
  3. Reticulocyte Count: Indicates bone marrow activity.
    • High (>2%): Hemolysis or response to bleeding (Hyperproliferative).
    • Low (<0.5%): Marrow failure or nutritional deficiency (Hypoproliferative).

B. Confirmatory / Specific Tests

Based on the morphological type identified in screening.

1. For Microcytic Anemia

  • Serum Ferritin: Low in IDA (Most sensitive), High in Chronic Disease.
  • Serum Iron & TIBC: Low Iron / High TIBC in IDA.
  • Hb Electrophoresis (HPLC): For Thalassemia (High HbA2/HbF).
  • Bone Marrow Iron Stain (Perls' Stain): Gold standard for iron stores.

2. For Macrocytic Anemia

  • Serum Vitamin B12 & Folate levels.
  • Serum Homocysteine & Methylmalonic Acid (MMA): Elevated in B12 deficiency.
  • Thyroid Profile (TSH): For hypothyroidism.

3. For Normocytic / Hemolytic Anemia

  • Coombs Test (DAT): Positive in Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia.
  • Osmotic Fragility Test (OFT): Increased in Hereditary Spherocytosis.
  • G6PD Screening.
  • Bone Marrow Aspiration/Biopsy: For Aplastic Anemia or Leukemia.
  • Stool Examination: Occult blood (GI bleed) or parasites (Hookworm).

4. PERIPHERAL BLOOD PICTURE IN ANEMIA

A. How to Perform (Prepare) a Peripheral Smear

Method: Wedge technique (Push smear).

  1. Place a small drop of blood (EDTA or finger prick) on a clean glass slide, 1 cm from the end.
  2. Take a "spreader" slide and place it at a 30-45Â° angle in front of the drop.
  3. Pull back the spreader to touch the drop (allow blood to spread along the width).
  4. Push the spreader forward rapidly and smoothly.
  5. Result: A tongue-shaped smear with a thick head, body, and a thin Feathered Edge (Tail).
  6. Staining: Air dry and stain with Leishman or Giemsa stain. Examine the "Tail" area where RBCs are separated.

B. Identification and Description of Findings

The peripheral smear provides morphological clues to the diagnosis.

1. RBC Size (Anisocytosis)

  • Microcytes: Small RBCs (< 6μm). Seen in IDA, Thalassemia.
  • Macrocytes: Large RBCs (> 9μm).
    • Oval Macrocytes: Megaloblastic Anemia.
    • Round Macrocytes: Liver disease, Alcoholism.

2. RBC Color (Hemoglobinization)

  • Hypochromia: Increased central pallor (> 1/3rd diameter). Seen in IDA.
  • Polychromasia: Bluish-grey RBCs (Reticulocytes). Indicates active marrow (Hemolysis).
  • Dimorphic Picture: Dual population (e.g., Microcytic + Normocytic). Seen in Sideroblastic anemia or Iron + B12 deficiency.

3. RBC Shape (Poikilocytosis)

Shape Description Associated Condition
Spherocyte Small, round, dense, no central pallor. Hereditary Spherocytosis, Autoimmune Hemolysis.
Target Cell Central hemoglobin spot (Bull's eye). Thalassemia, Liver disease, Post-splenectomy.
Sickle Cell Crescent/Sickle shaped. Sickle Cell Anemia.
Schistocyte Fragmented RBC (Helmet cell). Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia (DIC, TTP), Mechanical valves.
Tear Drop Cell Pear-shaped (Dacrocyte). Myelofibrosis (Marrow infiltration).
Bite Cell RBC with a "bite" taken out. G6PD Deficiency (Heinz bodies removed by spleen).

4. RBC Inclusions

  • Basophilic Stippling: Fine blue dots (RNA). Seen in Lead Poisoning, Thalassemia.
  • Howell-Jolly Bodies: Nuclear remnants (DNA). Seen in Post-Splenectomy.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Anemia. Classify Anemia based on Morphology and Etiology.

Introduction: Anemia is functionally defined as a reduction in the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood, usually due to a decrease in hemoglobin concentration below the normal range for age and sex (e.g., <13 g/dL in men, <12 g/dL in women).

Body:
1. Morphological Classification (Based on MCV):

  • Microcytic Hypochromic (MCV < 80 fL): Iron deficiency, Thalassemia.
  • Normocytic Normochromic (MCV 80-100 fL): Acute blood loss, Aplastic anemia.
  • Macrocytic (MCV > 100 fL): Megaloblastic anemia (B12/Folate deficiency).

2. Etiological Classification:
  • Decreased Production (Dyshemopoietic): Marrow failure, Nutritional deficiency.
  • Increased Destruction (Hemolytic): Intracorpuscular (Membrane/Enzyme defect) or Extracorpuscular (Immune/Infection).
  • Blood Loss: Acute or Chronic hemorrhage.

Conclusion: Morphological classification guides the initial investigation (e.g., Iron profile for microcytic), while etiological classification directs definitive treatment.

Q2: Enumerate the Laboratory Investigations for a case of Anemia.

Introduction: Investigation of anemia involves a stepwise approach starting with screening tests to establish the type, followed by specific tests to confirm the cause.

Body:
A. Basic Screening Tests:

  1. Complete Blood Count (CBC): Hb, Hematocrit, RBC count.
  2. Red Cell Indices: MCV (Size), MCH, MCHC (Hb content), RDW (Variation).
  3. Peripheral Blood Smear (PBS): To assess RBC morphology (e.g., Hypochromia, Spherocytes).
  4. Reticulocyte Count: To differentiate Hemolytic/Hemorrhagic (High) from Hypoproliferative (Low) anemia.

B. Specific Confirmatory Tests:
  • For Microcytic: Serum Ferritin, Serum Iron, TIBC, Hb Electrophoresis (HPLC).
  • For Macrocytic: Serum Vitamin B12, Serum Folate, Bone Marrow Aspiration (Megaloblasts).
  • For Hemolytic: Coombs test (Direct/Indirect), G6PD assay, Osmotic Fragility Test.
  • For Blood Loss: Stool for occult blood, Urine routine.

Conclusion: A combined interpretation of Indices and PBS is the most powerful tool in the initial evaluation of anemia.
