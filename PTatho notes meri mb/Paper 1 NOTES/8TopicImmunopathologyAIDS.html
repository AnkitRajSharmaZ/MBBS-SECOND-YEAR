TOPIC 8: IMMUNOPATHOLOGY AND AIDS

Syllabus Focus: Immunity, Hypersensitivity, HLA & Transplant, Autoimmunity (SLE, RA, etc.), and HIV/AIDS.

1. PRINCIPLES AND MECHANISMS OF IMMUNITY

Definition: Immunity is the resistance exhibited by the host against invasion by foreign antigens, including microorganisms.

Types of Immunity

1. Innate (Natural/Native) Immunity:

  • First line of defense; present from birth.
  • Characteristics: Non-specific, rapid response, No memory.
  • Components:
    • Physical Barriers: Skin, mucous membranes.
    • Cells: Neutrophils, Macrophages, NK cells, Dendritic cells.
    • Proteins: Complement system, C-reactive protein (CRP).
  • Mechanism: Recognizes "Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns" (PAMPs) via "Pattern Recognition Receptors" (e.g., Toll-like Receptors/TLRs).

2. Adaptive (Acquired) Immunity:

  • Second line of defense; develops after exposure.
  • Characteristics: Specific, diverse, Immunologic Memory, Self-tolerance.
  • Types:
    • Humoral Immunity: Mediated by B-cells & Antibodies (extracellular microbes).
    • Cell-Mediated Immunity (CMI): Mediated by T-cells (intracellular microbes like viruses, TB).

Cells of the Immune System

  • T Lymphocytes (60-70% of blood lymphocytes): Mature in Thymus.
    • CD4+ Helper T Cells: The "Generals". Recognize antigen with MHC Class II. Secrete cytokines to activate B cells and Macrophages.
    • CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells: The "Soldiers". Recognize antigen with MHC Class I. Directly kill infected cells.
  • B Lymphocytes (10-20%): Mature in Bone Marrow. Differentiate into Plasma cells to secrete antibodies (IgM, G, A, E, D).
  • Natural Killer (NK) Cells: Part of innate immunity. Kill tumor/virus cells that have lost MHC Class I.
  • Antigen Presenting Cells (APCs): Dendritic cells (most potent), Macrophages, B cells.

2. HYPERSENSITIVITY REACTIONS

Definition: An exaggerated or injurious immune response to an antigen resulting in tissue damage.

Type Mechanism Mediators Examples
Type I (Immediate/ Anaphylactic) IgE-mediated. Antigen binds to IgE on sensitized Mast cells & Basophils → Degranulation. Histamine (vasodilation, permeability), Leukotrienes, Prostaglandins. Anaphylaxis, Bronchial Asthma, Allergic Rhinitis (Hay fever), Urticaria.
Type II (Antibody-Mediated/ Cytotoxic) IgG or IgM binds to fixed antigens on cells/tissues. Causes injury by:
1. Phagocytosis (Opsonization).
2. Inflammation (Complement).
3. Cellular dysfunction.		 Antibodies, Complement (C3b, MAC). Transfusion reactions, Erythroblastosis fetalis, Goodpasture Syndrome, Myasthenia Gravis, Graves' Disease.
Type III (Immune Complex) Antigen-Antibody Complexes form in circulation and deposit in vessels/tissues → Activate Complement → Inflammation (Neutrophils). Immune complexes, C5a, Neutrophil enzymes. SLE, Serum Sickness, Arthus Reaction, Post-streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN).
Type IV (Cell-Mediated/ Delayed) T-cell mediated. No antibodies.
1. CD4+ (Delayed): Release cytokines (IFN-γ) → Macrophage activation → Granuloma.
2. CD8+ (Cytotoxicity): Direct killing.		 Th1/Th17 cells, Cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF), CD8+ T cells. Tuberculosis, Contact Dermatitis, Type 1 Diabetes, Rheumatoid Arthritis, Graft Rejection.

3. HLA SYSTEM AND TRANSPLANTATION

The HLA System (MHC)

The Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) in humans is called HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen). Genes are on Chromosome 6.

  • MHC Class I (HLA-A, B, C): Present on all nucleated cells and platelets. Recognized by CD8+ T cells.
  • MHC Class II (HLA-DP, DQ, DR): Present on APCs (Dendritic cells, Macrophages, B cells). Recognized by CD4+ T cells.
  • Significance: Essential for antigen presentation, transplant matching, and disease associations (e.g., HLA-B27 & Ankylosing Spondylitis).

Mechanisms of Transplant Rejection

Rejection is the immune destruction of the graft by the host.

  1. Hyperacute Rejection:
    • Time: Minutes to hours (on the operating table).
    • Cause: Preformed antibodies in the recipient against donor antigens (e.g., ABO mismatch).
    • Morphology: Thrombosis of graft vessels, ischemic necrosis. The kidney becomes cyanotic and flaccid.
  2. Acute Rejection:
    • Time: Days to weeks (or months if immunosuppression stops).
    • Mechanism:
      • Acute Cellular: T-cells destroy graft parenchyma (Tubulitis in kidney).
      • Acute Humoral: Antibodies cause vasculitis (Necrotizing vasculitis).
    • Treatment: Responds well to immunosuppression.
  3. Chronic Rejection:
    • Time: Months to years.
    • Mechanism: Chronic inflammation leading to Fibrosis and Arteriolosclerosis (thickening of vessel walls).
    • Result: Progressive loss of organ function (e.g., rising creatinine). Irreversible.

Graft Versus Host Disease (GVHD): Seen in Bone Marrow Transplants. Donor T-cells attack the immunodeficient Recipient. Targets: Skin (rash), Liver (jaundice), Gut (diarrhea).

4. AUTOIMMUNITY

Definition: Immune reaction against self-antigens due to a loss of Self-Tolerance.

Mechanisms of Tolerance

  • Central Tolerance: Deletion of self-reactive T and B clones during maturation in Thymus and Bone Marrow (Negative selection).
  • Peripheral Tolerance: Silencing of self-reactive cells that escape to the periphery via Anergy (inactivation), Suppression (by Regulatory T cells / Tregs), or Apoptosis.

Pathogenesis of Autoimmunity

  • Genetic Susceptibility: Strong association with HLA genes (e.g., HLA-DR3 in SLE).
  • Environmental Triggers:
    • Infections: Molecular Mimicry (microbial antigens resemble self-antigens, e.g., Rheumatic Fever).
    • Tissue Injury: Exposure of sequestered antigens (e.g., sperm, eye lens).

Enumeration of Autoimmune Disorders

  • Systemic: SLE, Rheumatoid Arthritis, Sjogren Syndrome, Systemic Sclerosis, Polyarteritis Nodosa.
  • Organ-Specific: Hashimoto Thyroiditis, Graves' Disease, Type 1 Diabetes, Myasthenia Gravis, Pernicious Anemia.

5. SYSTEMIC LUPUS ERYTHEMATOSUS (SLE)

Definition: A chronic, multisystem autoimmune disease characterized by a vast array of autoantibodies, particularly Antinuclear Antibodies (ANAs).

Etiology and Pathogenesis

  1. Genetic Factors: Familial association; HLA-DR2, HLA-DR3; Deficiency of Complement (C1q, C2, C4).
  2. Environmental Factors: UV Radiation (damages DNA, induces apoptosis), Drugs (Procainamide, Hydralazine), Sex Hormones (Estrogen - more common in females).
  3. Immunologic Mechanism:
    • Failure of self-tolerance in B and T cells.
    • Defective clearance of Apoptotic bodies (nuclear material persists).
    • Type III Hypersensitivity: Antigen-Antibody complexes deposit in tissues (Kidney, Skin).
    • Type II Hypersensitivity: Autoantibodies against blood cells (RBCs, Platelets).
Important Autoantibodies in SLE:
  • ANA (Antinuclear Antibody): Sensitive but not specific. Best screening test.
  • Anti-dsDNA: Highly specific for SLE; correlates with disease activity and nephritis.
  • Anti-Smith (Anti-Sm): Highly specific for SLE.
  • Antiphospholipid Antibodies: Associated with thrombosis and fetal loss (secondary APLA syndrome).

Morphology (Pathology)

  1. Kidney (Lupus Nephritis): Most serious complication. Deposition of immune complexes in glomeruli.
    • Class I: Minimal mesangial.
    • Class II: Mesangial proliferative.
    • Class III: Focal proliferative.
    • Class IV: Diffuse proliferative (Most common and severe; "Wire-loop" lesions).
    • Class V: Membranous.
    • Class VI: Advanced sclerosing.
  2. Skin: Malar (Butterfly) Rash over face. Microscopy shows liquefactive degeneration of the basal layer and edema. Immunofluorescence shows "Lupus Band Test" (Ig/Complement at dermo-epidermal junction).
  3. Heart: Libman-Sacks Endocarditis (Sterile, warty vegetations on both sides of valves). Pericarditis is the most common cardiac finding.
  4. Joints: Non-erosive synovitis (little deformity compared to RA).
  5. LE Cell: A neutrophil or macrophage that has engulfed the denatured nuclear material (hematoxylin body) of another cell.

Clinical Features: Young female, butterfly rash, fever, joint pain, photosensitivity, proteinuria, pleuritis.

6. HIV AND AIDS

Definition: AIDS is caused by the retrovirus HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus), leading to profound immunosuppression due to the depletion of CD4+ T cells.

Etiology

  • Agent: HIV-1 (Global) and HIV-2 (West Africa).
  • Structure: RNA virus with enzyme Reverse Transcriptase. Envelope has glycoproteins gp120 and gp41.
  • Transmission: Sexual contact (most common), Parenteral (blood sharing), Vertical (Mother-to-child).

Pathogenesis

  1. Binding and Entry: HIV gp120 binds to CD4 molecule on T-cells, Macrophages, Dendritic cells. Requires co-receptors (CCR5 or CXCR4).
  2. Reverse Transcription: Viral RNA → DNA (using Reverse Transcriptase).
  3. Integration: Viral DNA integrates into Host Genome (Provirus).
  4. Replication: When the T-cell is activated (e.g., by infection), the virus replicates, buds out, and kills the cell.
  5. Mechanisms of CD4 Loss: Direct lysis by virus, destruction by CD8+ T cells, apoptosis, loss of precursors in thymus.
  6. Result: CD4 count drops. Inversion of CD4:CD8 ratio (Normal 2:1 → becomes 0.5:1). Loss of help for B cells and Macrophages.

Natural History (Stages)

  1. Acute Retroviral Syndrome (3-6 weeks): Flu-like symptoms (fever, sore throat), high viremia, transient drop in CD4.
  2. Chronic (Latent) Phase (7-10 years): Asymptomatic or minor infections. Virus replicates in lymph nodes. CD4 slowly declines.
  3. Crisis Phase (AIDS): CD4 count < 200 cells/μL. Breakdown of host defense. Appearance of "AIDS-defining illnesses".

Pathology (Clinical Manifestations)

  • Opportunistic Infections:
    • Protozoa: Toxoplasmosis (CNS), Cryptosporidiosis (Diarrhea).
    • Fungi: Pneumocystis jiroveci (PCP) pneumonia, Candidiasis (Oral thrush/Esophagitis), Cryptococcus (Meningitis).
    • Viruses: CMV (Retinitis), HSV, VZV.
    • Bacteria: Tuberculosis (M. tuberculosis & M. avium complex).
  • Tumors (Neoplasms):
    • Kaposi Sarcoma: Vascular tumor caused by HHV-8. Red-purple patches on skin/mouth.
    • Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma: High-grade B-cell lymphomas (Burkitt, DLBCL) linked to EBV.
    • Cervical Carcinoma: Invasive cancer due to HPV.
  • CNS Involvement: AIDS-Dementia Complex (directly by HIV in microglia).

7. OTHER AUTOIMMUNE DISEASES (BRIEF)

  • Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA): Chronic systemic inflammation affecting joints. Mediated by CD4+ Th1/Th17 cells. Formation of Pannus (granulation tissue) destroys cartilage/bone. Marker: Anti-CCP and Rheumatoid Factor.
  • Sj√∂gren Syndrome: Destruction of lacrimal (dry eyes) and salivary glands (dry mouth) by T-cells. Marker: Anti-SS-A (Ro) and Anti-SS-B (La).
  • Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma): Excessive Fibrosis of skin and organs. Endothelial injury → vascular damage → fibroblast activation. Marker: Anti-Scl-70 (Diffuse) and Anti-Centromere (CREST).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Hypersensitivity. Classify it and describe Type I Hypersensitivity in detail.

Introduction: Hypersensitivity is an exaggerated or injurious immune response to an antigen that results in tissue damage. It is classified into four types (Gell and Coombs classification).

Body:
Classification: Type I (Immediate), Type II (Antibody-mediated), Type III (Immune Complex), Type IV (Cell-mediated).
Type I Hypersensitivity:
Mechanism: It is mediated by IgE antibodies attached to mast cells. Upon re-exposure to an allergen (e.g., pollen, dust), the antigen cross-links the IgE, causing mast cell degranulation.
Mediators:

  • Primary (Immediate): Histamine (vasodilation, bronchospasm), Proteases.
  • Secondary (Late phase): Leukotrienes (LTC4, LTD4), Prostaglandins.

Clinical Examples: Anaphylaxis (Systemic shock), Bronchial Asthma, Allergic Rhinitis.

Conclusion: Type I reactions are rapid and potentially fatal (anaphylaxis) requiring immediate epinephrine. They are driven by Th2 response and IgE production.

Q2: Discuss the Pathogenesis and Morphology of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE).

Introduction: SLE is a multisystem autoimmune disorder caused by a wide array of autoantibodies, primarily antinuclear antibodies (ANAs).

Body:
Pathogenesis: It involves a breakdown of self-tolerance.

  • Genetic: HLA-DR2/DR3 linkage.
  • Environmental: UV light induces apoptosis of cells, exposing nuclear antigens.
  • Mechanism: Defective clearance of apoptotic bodies leads to persistence of nuclear antigens. B-cells produce Anti-dsDNA and Anti-Sm antibodies. These form Immune Complexes (Type III reaction) which deposit in small vessels and glomeruli, activating complement and causing inflammation.

Morphology:
  • Kidney: Lupus Nephritis (Class I-VI). "Wire-loop" lesions in diffuse proliferative type.
  • Skin: Malar butterfly rash. Liquefactive degeneration of basal cells.
  • Heart: Libman-Sacks endocarditis (vegetations on both sides of valves).

Conclusion: SLE is a prototype of immune complex disease. Renal failure is a common cause of death.

Q3: Write a short note on the Pathogenesis of AIDS.

Introduction: AIDS is the end-stage of HIV infection, characterized by profound immunodeficiency due to the loss of CD4+ T cells.

Body: The HIV virus (gp120) binds to the CD4 receptor and CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptors on T-helper cells. The viral RNA is reverse-transcribed and integrated into the host DNA.
Mechanisms of T-cell Loss:

  1. Direct cytopathic effect (lysis) during viral budding.
  2. Killing of infected CD4 cells by CD8+ Cytotoxic T cells.
  3. Chronic activation causing apoptosis.

When the CD4 count falls below 200/μL, cell-mediated immunity fails, allowing opportunistic infections (TB, Pneumocystis) and tumors (Kaposi sarcoma) to develop.

Conclusion: The hallmark of HIV pathogenesis is the progressive depletion of CD4+ T cells and inversion of the CD4:CD8 ratio.
