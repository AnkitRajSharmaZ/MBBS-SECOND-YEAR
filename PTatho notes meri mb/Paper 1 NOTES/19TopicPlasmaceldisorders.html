TOPIC 19: PLASMA CELL MYELOMA

Syllabus Scope: Features of Plasma Cell Myeloma (Multiple Myeloma).

1. DEFINITION AND INTRODUCTION

Definition: Multiple Myeloma (MM) is a malignant neoplasm of plasma cells that is characterized by the accumulation of clonal plasma cells in the bone marrow, the production of a monoclonal immunoglobulin (M protein) in the serum and/or urine, and associated end-organ damage (CRAB criteria).

Key Terminology

  • Monoclonal Gammopathy: Expansion of a single clone of immunoglobulin-secreting cells.
  • M-Protein (Paraprotein): The monoclonal antibody produced (usually IgG or IgA).
  • Bence Jones Protein: Free light chains (kappa or lambda) excreted in the urine.

2. PATHOGENESIS

The disease typically evolves from a premalignant stage called MGUS (Monoclonal Gammopathy of Undetermined Significance).

A. Molecular Pathogenesis

  • Genetic Aberrations: Frequent translocations involving the IgH locus on Chromosome 14q32 (e.g., t(11;14), t(4;14)). Deletions of 13q and 17p (TP53) are poor prognostic markers.
  • Bone Marrow Microenvironment: Adhesion of myeloma cells to stromal cells triggers the production of cytokines, most importantly Interleukin-6 (IL-6). IL-6 promotes the survival and proliferation of myeloma cells.

B. Mechanism of Clinical Features

  1. Bone Destruction (Lytic Lesions): Myeloma cells upregulate RANKL expression on stromal cells. RANKL activates Osteoclasts (bone-eating cells). Simultaneously, they suppress Osteoblasts (bone-forming cells) via Wnt pathway inhibitors. This leads to purely lytic "punched-out" lesions.
  2. Renal Failure (Myeloma Kidney): Excess free light chains (Bence Jones proteins) are toxic to renal tubules. They combine with Tamm-Horsfall protein in the distal tubules to form obstructing casts. Hypercalcemia also damages the kidney (nephrocalcinosis).
  3. Immunosuppression: Suppression of normal plasma cells leads to hypogammaglobulinemia, increasing susceptibility to bacterial infections.

3. CLINICAL FEATURES (CRAB CRITERIA)

The hallmark manifestations are summarized by the mnemonic CRAB:

  • C - HyperCalcemia: Due to extensive bone resorption. Causes confusion, weakness, constipation, and stones.
  • R - Renal Insufficiency: Elevated creatinine due to cast nephropathy.
  • A - Anemia: Normocytic normochromic anemia due to marrow replacement by tumor cells and renal failure (low EPO). Causes fatigue and pallor.
  • B - Bone Lesions: Bone pain (especially back pain), pathologic fractures, and vertebral collapse.

*Note: Updated criteria (SLiM-CRAB) allow diagnosis with >60% marrow plasma cells, Light chain ratio >100, or MRI lesions even without symptoms.

Other Features:
• Recurrent infections (Pneumonia, Pyelonephritis).
• Hyperviscosity syndrome (rare in IgG myeloma, common in IgA/IgM): Visual disturbances, neurological symptoms.
• AL Amyloidosis (10% cases): Macroglossia, Carpal tunnel syndrome.

4. MORPHOLOGY (PATHOLOGY)

A. Bone Marrow

  • Cellularity: Hypercellular marrow.
  • Plasma Cells: > 10% Clonal Plasma Cells (often >30%). They may be arranged in sheets, clusters, or interstitial infiltrates.
  • Microscopic Features of Neoplastic Plasma Cells:
    • Perinuclear Halo: Clear zone near the nucleus (Golgi apparatus).
    • Eccentric Nucleus: "Cartwheel" or "Clock-face" chromatin pattern.
    • Binucleate/Multinucleate forms.
    • Inclusion Bodies:
      • Russell Bodies: Eosinophilic globules of immunoglobulin in the cytoplasm.
      • Dutcher Bodies: Intranuclear inclusions (PAS positive).
      • Mott Cells: Cells filled with multiple grape-like vacuoles (Grape cells).
      • Flame Cells: Fiery red cytoplasm (seen in IgA myeloma).

B. Peripheral Blood Smear

  • Rouleaux Formation: RBCs stack like coins due to high serum protein (globulins) reducing negative charge (Zeta potential).
  • Plasma Cells: Occasionally seen (Plasma Cell Leukemia if >2,000/uL).
  • Anemia: Normocytic normochromic.

C. Gross Pathology (Bones)

  • Punched-out Defects: Sharp, round, lytic defects seen in the skull ("Raindrop skull"), ribs, vertebrae, and pelvis. No reactive bone formation (sclerosis).

5. INVESTIGATIONS (LAB DIAGNOSIS)

Diagnostic Triad

  1. Bone Marrow: >10% clonal plasma cells.
  2. Serum/Urine Protein: Monoclonal M-protein spike.
  3. End Organ Damage: CRAB features.
  1. Serum Protein Electrophoresis (SPEP):
    • Shows a localized band/spike in the gamma-globulin region (M-Spike or M-Component).
    • Most common: IgG (55%), followed by IgA (25%).
  2. Immunofixation: Confirms the type of heavy and light chain (e.g., IgG Kappa).
  3. Urine Electrophoresis (UPEP): Detects Bence Jones Proteins (Free light chains).
  4. Serum Free Light Chain Assay: Abnormal Kappa/Lambda ratio.
  5. Biochemistry:
    • Hypercalcemia.
    • Elevated Creatinine and Urea.
    • Elevated Total Protein (with reversed Albumin/Globulin ratio).
    • Beta-2 Microglobulin: Important prognostic marker (High levels = Poor prognosis).
  6. Radiology (Skeletal Survey): X-rays show punched-out lytic lesions. MRI/PET is more sensitive.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Multiple Myeloma. Describe its Pathogenesis and Clinical Features (CRAB).

Introduction: Multiple Myeloma is a malignant neoplasm of plasma cells characterized by the production of monoclonal immunoglobulin (M-protein) and lytic bone lesions.

Body:
Pathogenesis:

  • Genetic: IgH translocations (14q32) and deletions (13q).
  • Microenvironment: IL-6 produced by stromal cells is the key growth factor.
  • Bone Disease: Myeloma cells activate RANKL, stimulating osteoclasts to resorb bone, leading to lytic lesions and hypercalcemia.

Clinical Features (CRAB):
  • C: Calcium elevation (Hypercalcemia) → Confusion, weakness.
  • R: Renal impairment → Cast nephropathy (Bence Jones proteins).
  • A: Anemia → Fatigue.
  • B: Bone pain/Lesions → Pathologic fractures, punched-out lytic lesions.

Conclusion: Diagnosis requires demonstrating marrow plasmacytosis (>10%), M-protein spike, and end-organ damage.

Q2: Describe the Laboratory Findings and Morphology of Multiple Myeloma.

Introduction: Lab diagnosis hinges on demonstrating the monoclonal protein and the proliferation of atypical plasma cells.

Body:
1. Peripheral Smear:

  • Rouleaux formation (Coin stacking of RBCs) due to hypergammaglobulinemia.
  • Leukoerythroblastic picture may be seen.

2. Bone Marrow:
  • Hypercellular with >10% plasma cells.
  • Morphology: Binucleate forms, eccentric nuclei, perinuclear halo. Presence of Russell bodies (cytoplasmic), Dutcher bodies (intranuclear), and Mott cells (grape-like).

3. Electrophoresis:
  • Serum: Tall, narrow M-spike in the gamma region.
  • Urine: Bence Jones proteinuria.

Conclusion: Elevated Beta-2 Microglobulin and Albumin levels are used for staging (ISS system).
