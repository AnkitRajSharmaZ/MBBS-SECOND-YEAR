TOPIC 14: MACROCYTIC ANEMIA

Syllabus Scope: B12 Metabolism, Etiology of B12 Deficiency, Laboratory Investigations, Peripheral Smear, and Megaloblastic vs Non-Megaloblastic Anemia.

1. VITAMIN B12 (COBALAMIN) METABOLISM

Vitamin B12 is essential for DNA synthesis and neurologic function. Humans cannot synthesize it and must obtain it from animal products (meat, dairy, eggs).

Absorption and Transport Pathway

  1. Ingestion: Vitamin B12 in food is bound to proteins.
  2. Stomach (Release): Acid and Pepsin release B12 from food proteins.
    • Free B12 binds to Haptocorrin (R-binder) secreted by saliva/stomach (protects it from acid).
    • Parietal Cells in the gastric fundus secrete Intrinsic Factor (IF).
  3. Duodenum (Digestion): Pancreatic proteases degrade R-binder, releasing B12.
    • B12 now binds to Intrinsic Factor (IF) to form the B12-IF complex.
  4. Ileum (Absorption): The B12-IF complex travels to the Distal Ileum.
    • It binds to the Cubilin receptor on ileal enterocytes and is absorbed.
  5. Plasma Transport: Within the enterocyte, B12 is released from IF and binds to Transcobalamin II for transport to tissues (Bone marrow, Liver).

Biochemical Function

B12 is a cofactor for two key enzymes:

  • Methionine Synthase: Converts Homocysteine → Methionine (Requires Methyl-THF). Essential for DNA synthesis.
    Deficiency leads to: Trapping of Folate as Methyl-THF ("Folate Trap"), impairing DNA synthesis.
  • Methylmalonyl-CoA Mutase: Converts Methylmalonyl-CoA → Succinyl-CoA.
    Deficiency leads to: Accumulation of Methylmalonic Acid (MMA), causing Demyelination (Neurologic damage).

2. ETIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS OF B12 DEFICIENCY

Etiology

  1. Decreased Intake: Strict Vegans (rare, takes years due to large liver stores).
  2. Impaired Absorption (Most Common):
    • Gastric Causes: Pernicious Anemia (Autoimmune destruction of Parietal cells), Gastrectomy, Atrophic Gastritis (Achlorhydria).
    • Intestinal Causes: Ileal resection (Crohn's disease), Malabsorption syndromes (Sprue), Blind Loop Syndrome (Bacterial overgrowth), Fish Tapeworm (Diphyllobothrium latum).
  3. Increased Demand: Pregnancy, Hyperthyroidism, Cancer.

Pathogenesis of Pernicious Anemia

An autoimmune disorder characterized by the destruction of gastric parietal cells.

  • Mechanism: Autoreactive T-cells destroy parietal cells.
  • Autoantibodies:
    • Type I (Blocking): Blocks B12 binding to Intrinsic Factor.
    • Type II (Binding): Blocks B12-IF complex binding to Ileal receptor.
    • Anti-Parietal Cell Antibody: Against proton pump (H+/K+ ATPase).
  • Result: Lack of Intrinsic Factor → B12 malabsorption → Megaloblastic Anemia + Neurologic disease (Subacute Combined Degeneration of Spinal Cord).

3. MACROCYTIC ANEMIA: CLASSIFICATION

Macrocytic anemia is defined as anemia with MCV > 100 fL. It is divided into two types based on bone marrow morphology.

Feature Megaloblastic Anemia Non-Megaloblastic Anemia
Etiology Deficiency of B12 or Folate. Drug-induced (Methotrexate, Phenytoin). Alcoholism, Liver Disease, Hypothyroidism, Reticulocytosis (Hemolysis), Myelodysplasia.
Pathogenesis Defective DNA Synthesis. Nuclear-Cytoplasmic Asynchrony (Nucleus matures slower than cytoplasm). Membrane accumulation of lipids (Liver disease) or increased young RBCs (Reticulocytes are large). DNA synthesis is normal.
MCV Often > 110 fL (Marked Macrocytosis). Usually 100 - 110 fL (Mild Macrocytosis).
Neutrophils Hypersegmented (>5 lobes). Normal segmentation.
RBC Shape Macro-Ovalocytes (Large, oval cells). Round Macrocytes.
Bone Marrow Hypercellular with Megaloblasts (Sieve-like chromatin). Ineffective Erythropoiesis. Normoblastic Erythropoiesis.

4. LABORATORY INVESTIGATIONS OF MACROCYTIC ANEMIA

A. Screening Tests

  1. Complete Blood Count (CBC):
    • Hb: Low.
    • MCV: Increased (>100 fL).
    • Pancytopenia: WBC and Platelets may also be low (due to ineffective hematopoiesis).
    • Reticulocyte Count: Low (Hypoproliferative).
  2. Peripheral Blood Smear (PBS): See below.

B. Confirmatory Biochemical Tests

  1. Serum Vitamin B12: Low (< 200 pg/mL) in B12 deficiency.
  2. Serum Folate: Low (< 2 ng/mL) in Folate deficiency.
  3. Metabolite Assays (Crucial for diagnosis):
    • Homocysteine: Elevated in BOTH B12 and Folate deficiency.
    • Methylmalonic Acid (MMA): Elevated ONLY in B12 deficiency (Normal in Folate deficiency).

C. Specific Tests for Cause

  • Anti-Intrinsic Factor Antibody: Highly specific for Pernicious Anemia.
  • Anti-Parietal Cell Antibody: Sensitive but less specific.
  • Schilling Test (Obsolete): Used to differentiate dietary vs. malabsorption causes. Replaced by antibody testing.
  • Bone Marrow Aspiration: Shows Megaloblastic hyperplasia (Blue marrow). Giant Metamyelocytes.

5. PERIPHERAL BLOOD PICTURE (MEGALOBLASTIC ANEMIA)

The peripheral smear findings are classic and diagnostic.

Key Findings

  • RBCs:
    • Macro-Ovalocytes: Large, egg-shaped red blood cells. Fully hemoglobinized (no central pallor).
    • Anisopoikilocytosis: Marked variation in size and shape.
    • Inclusions: Howell-Jolly Bodies (Nuclear remnants) and Basophilic Stippling.
    • Cabot Rings: Rare, thread-like loops.
  • WBCs:
    • Hypersegmented Neutrophils: A hallmark feature. Neutrophils with 5 or more lobes (normally 3-4). Presence of even one 6-lobed neutrophil is diagnostic.
  • Platelets: May be reduced (Thrombocytopenia). Large forms may be seen.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Describe the metabolism of Vitamin B12. Add a note on the pathogenesis of Pernicious Anemia.

Introduction: Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin) is a water-soluble vitamin required for DNA synthesis and myelin maintenance. Its metabolism involves a complex interaction of binding proteins.

Body:
Metabolism:

  1. Gastric Phase: Dietary B12 is released by acid/pepsin and binds to Haptocorrin (R-binder). Parietal cells secrete Intrinsic Factor (IF).
  2. Duodenal Phase: Pancreatic enzymes degrade R-binder. B12 binds to IF.
  3. Ileal Phase: The B12-IF complex is absorbed in the distal ileum via Cubilin receptors.

Pathogenesis of Pernicious Anemia:
It is an autoimmune gastritis. Autoantibodies target parietal cells and Intrinsic Factor.
  • Destruction of parietal cells → Achlorhydria.
  • Lack of IF → Failure of B12 absorption in the ileum.
  • Result: Megaloblastic anemia and demyelinating neurologic disease.

Conclusion: The liver stores B12 for years, so deficiency develops slowly.

Q2: Differentiate between Megaloblastic and Non-Megaloblastic Macrocytic Anemia.

Introduction: Macrocytic anemia (MCV > 100 fL) is classified based on the mechanism of red cell enlargement and bone marrow morphology.

Body:

Feature Megaloblastic Non-Megaloblastic
Cause B12 or Folate Deficiency Alcohol, Liver disease, Hypothyroidism
Defect Impaired DNA Synthesis Membrane lipids / Reticulocytosis
Neutrophils Hypersegmented Normal
RBC Shape Macro-Ovalocytes Round Macrocytes
Marrow Megaloblastic (Asynchrony) Normoblastic

Conclusion: Hypersegmented neutrophils are the most reliable morphological sign distinguishing the two.
