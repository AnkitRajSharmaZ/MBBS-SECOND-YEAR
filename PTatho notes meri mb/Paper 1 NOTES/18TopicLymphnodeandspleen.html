TOPIC 18: LYMPH NODE AND SPLEEN

Syllabus Scope: Causes and differentiation of Lymphadenopathy, Tuberculous Lymphadenitis, Hodgkin's vs. Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma.

1. LYMPHADENOPATHY

Definition: An abnormality in the size (>1 cm), consistency, or number of lymph nodes. It may be localized or generalized.

Causes of Lymphadenopathy

  • Infectious:
    • Bacterial: Pyogenic bacteria (Staphylococcus, Streptococcus), Tuberculosis, Syphilis, Brucellosis.
    • Viral: Infectious Mononucleosis (EBV), CMV, HIV, Rubella.
    • Parasitic: Toxoplasmosis, Filariasis.
    • Fungal: Histoplasmosis.
  • Neoplastic (Malignant):
    • Primary: Hodgkin's Lymphoma, Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma, CLL.
    • Metastatic: Carcinomas (e.g., Virchowâ€™s node from Gastric Ca), Melanoma.
  • Immunological / Autoimmune:
    • SLE, Rheumatoid Arthritis, Sarcoidosis.
  • Iatrogenic / Drugs: Phenytoin, Serum sickness.
  • Lipid Storage Diseases: Gaucher disease, Niemann-Pick disease.

Differentiating Features of Lymphadenopathy

Feature Infectious / Inflammatory Neoplastic (Malignant) Tuberculous
Tenderness Tender (Painful due to rapid capsule stretching). Non-tender (Painless). Mildly tender or painless.
Consistency Soft or fluctuant (if abscess). Hard (Carcinoma) or Rubbery (Lymphoma). Matted (Fixed to each other).
Mobility Mobile (unless peri-adenitis occurs). Fixed (due to invasion). Fixed/Matted.
Distribution Often localized (draining an infection site). Localized or Generalized. Cervical chain (Scrofula) is most common.

2. TUBERCULOUS LYMPHADENITIS

Definition: The most common form of extrapulmonary tuberculosis, affecting the lymph nodes (formerly called "Scrofula").

Etiology and Pathogenesis

  • Causative Agent: Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
  • Pathogenesis:
    • Post-primary dissemination from lungs (lympho-hematogenous spread) or direct infection of tonsils (bovine TB).
    • Bacilli lodge in lymph nodes → T-cell mediated immunity (Type IV Hypersensitivity) → Macrophage activation → Granuloma formation → Central Caseous Necrosis.

Pathology (Morphology)

  1. Gross Appearance:
    • Nodes are enlarged, matted (stuck together due to periadenitis), and firm.
    • Cut surface shows yellow-white, cheesy Caseous Necrosis.
    • May form a "Cold Abscess" or "Collar Stud Abscess" (sinus tract rupturing through skin).
  2. Microscopy:
    • Epithelioid Cell Granulomas: Aggregates of activated macrophages (epithelioid cells).
    • Langhans Giant Cells: Large multinucleated cells with nuclei arranged in a horseshoe pattern.
    • Central Caseation: Amorphous, eosinophilic, granular necrosis (Structureless).
    • Peripheral Cuff: Lymphocytes and fibroblasts.
    • ZN Stain: Demonstrates Acid-Fast Bacilli (AFB).

3. HODGKIN'S LYMPHOMA (HL)

Definition: A B-cell neoplasm arising in lymph nodes, characterized by the presence of giant neoplastic cells called Reed-Sternberg (RS) cells within a reactive inflammatory background.

WHO Classification

  1. Nodular Lymphocyte Predominant HL: (Non-classical, rare).
  2. Classical Hodgkin Lymphoma:
    • Nodular Sclerosis (Most common).
    • Mixed Cellularity.
    • Lymphocyte Rich.
    • Lymphocyte Depleted (Worst prognosis).

Pathogenesis

  • Cell of Origin: Germinal center B-cell ("Crippled" B-cell that cannot produce antibodies).
  • Molecular: Activation of NF-κB transcription factor (prevents apoptosis).
  • EBV Association: Strong link with EBV (especially in Mixed Cellularity type). EBV genome found in RS cells.

Pathology

  • Reed-Sternberg (RS) Cell: The diagnostic cell. Large (15-45 μm), binucleate/multinucleate, with prominent eosinophilic inclusion-like nucleoli ("Owl-Eye Appearance").
    • Immunophenotype: CD15+, CD30+, CD45- (Negative for leukocyte common antigen).
  • Variants:
    • Lacunar Cells: Cell sits in an empty space (lacuna). Seen in Nodular Sclerosis.
    • Popcorn Cells (L&H cells): Puffy nucleus. Seen in Nodular Lymphocyte Predominant.

4. NON-HODGKIN'S LYMPHOMA (NHL)

Definition: A heterogeneous group of lymphoid neoplasms (B-cell or T-cell) that lack Reed-Sternberg cells. 85% are B-cell origin.

Common Subtypes and Pathogenesis

  • Follicular Lymphoma:
    • Genetics: t(14;18).
    • Gene: Overexpression of BCL2 (Anti-apoptotic). Prevents cell death.
    • Microscopy: Nodular growth pattern mimicking follicles. Centrocytes (cleaved) and Centroblasts.
  • Burkitt Lymphoma:
    • Genetics: t(8;14).
    • Gene: Overexpression of c-MYC (Growth promoter).
    • Microscopy: "Starry Sky" pattern (Macrophages eating apoptotic debris amidst sheets of blue tumor cells).
  • Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL): Most common NHL. Aggressive. Diffuse growth of large cells.

5. DIFFERENCES: HODGKIN'S VS. NON-HODGKIN'S LYMPHOMA

This is a high-yield exam topic.

Feature Hodgkin's Lymphoma (HL) Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma (NHL)
Cell Type Reed-Sternberg Cells (CD15+, CD30+). Background has many reactive cells. Monoclonal B or T cells. No RS cells.
Nodal Involvement Localized to a single axial group (Cervical, Mediastinal). Disseminated; frequent involvement of multiple peripheral nodes.
Spread Contiguous (Orderly spread to adjacent nodes). Non-contiguous (Haphazard/Skip spread).
Extranodal Involvement Rare. Common (Waldeyer ring, Gut, Skin, CNS).
Age Distribution Bimodal (Young adults & Elderly). Peak incidence in older adults (increases with age).
Constitutional Symptoms Common ("B Symptoms": Fever, Night sweats, Weight loss). Less common (except in aggressive types).
Prognosis Excellent cure rate with chemotherapy (ABVD). Variable; some are indolent (incurable), others aggressive (curable).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Lymphadenopathy. Describe the pathogenesis and pathology of Tuberculous Lymphadenitis.

Introduction: Lymphadenopathy is the enlargement of lymph nodes due to reactive or neoplastic causes. TB lymphadenitis is the commonest cause of inflammatory lymphadenopathy in developing countries.

Body:
Classification:

  • Localized: Infection (TB, Pyogenic), Metastasis.
  • Generalized: Viral (HIV, EBV), Lymphoma, Leukemia, SLE.

Pathogenesis of TB Lymphadenitis: Caused by M. tuberculosis via lymphohematogenous spread. It involves a Type IV Hypersensitivity reaction where T-cells activate macrophages to form granulomas to contain the bacilli.
Pathology:
  • Gross: Nodes are matted, firm, with a cut surface showing yellow, cheesy caseous necrosis.
  • Microscopy: The hallmark is the Caseating Epithelioid Granuloma with Langhans giant cells and a lymphocytic rim. ZN stain reveals acid-fast bacilli.

Conclusion: It is a specific granulomatous inflammation often requiring FNAC or biopsy for confirmation.

Q2: Tabulate the differences between Hodgkin's and Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma. Describe the Reed-Sternberg cell.

Introduction: Lymphomas are malignancies of the lymphoid system. The primary distinction is the presence of Reed-Sternberg cells in Hodgkin's Lymphoma.

Body:
Differences: (Refer to the table above). Key points are the contiguous spread and rarity of extranodal sites in HL compared to NHL.
Reed-Sternberg (RS) Cell:
The diagnostic tumor giant cell of HL.

  • Morphology: Large cell (15-45 μm), binucleate (mirror image nuclei), with huge eosinophilic nucleoli creating an "Owl-eye" appearance.
  • Origin: Germinal center B-cell.
  • Markers: CD15 (+), CD30 (+), CD45 (-).

Conclusion: The identification of the RS cell in an appropriate inflammatory background is essential for diagnosing Hodgkin's Lymphoma.
