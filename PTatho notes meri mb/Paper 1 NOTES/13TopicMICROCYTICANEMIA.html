TOPIC 13: MICROCYTIC ANEMIA

Syllabus Scope: Iron Metabolism, Etiology, Investigations, Differential Diagnosis, and Peripheral Smear Findings of Microcytic Hypochromic Anemia.

1. IRON METABOLISM

Iron is essential for cellular function but toxic in excess. Its levels are tightly regulated by absorption, as there is no regulated pathway for excretion.

Distribution of Iron (Total Body Iron: 2.5 - 4g)

  • Functional Iron (80%):
    • Hemoglobin (65%): In RBCs.
    • Myoglobin & Enzymes (15%): Catalase, Cytochromes.
  • Storage Iron (20%):
    • Ferritin: Soluble, readily mobilized. Stored in Liver (Hepatocytes) and Macrophages.
    • Hemosiderin: Insoluble, aggregated ferritin. Found in macrophages of marrow, spleen, and liver.

Physiology of Iron Handling

  1. Absorption: Occurs in the Duodenum.
    • Dietary iron exists as Heme (meat) and Non-heme (plants) iron.
    • Non-heme iron (Fe3+) is reduced to Fe2+ by duodenal cytochrome b.
    • Fe2+ enters enterocytes via DMT1 (Divalent Metal Transporter 1).
    • Iron is transported out of the enterocyte into plasma via Ferroportin.
  2. Transport:
    • Iron binds to Transferrin (synthesized by the liver) in plasma.
    • Transferrin delivers iron to erythroid precursors in bone marrow via Transferrin Receptors (CD71).
  3. Regulation (The Hepcidin Axis):
    • Hepcidin is the master regulator, synthesized by the liver.
    • Mechanism: Hepcidin binds to Ferroportin (on enterocytes and macrophages), causing its degradation. This blocks iron absorption and release from stores.
    • High Iron / Inflammation (IL-6): ↑ Hepcidin → ↓ Iron absorption (Anemia of Chronic Disease).
    • Iron Deficiency / Hypoxia: ↓ Hepcidin → ↑ Iron absorption.

2. MICROCYTIC HYPOCHROMIC ANEMIA

Definition: Anemia characterized by reduced size of red blood cells (MCV < 80 fL) and reduced hemoglobin content (MCHC < 32 g/dL).

Etiology

The differential diagnosis is remembered by the mnemonic "TAILS":

  • T - Thalassemia: Defective globin chain synthesis (Genetic).
  • A - Anemia of Chronic Disease (ACD): Defective iron utilization due to Hepcidin (Infections, Cancer, Autoimmune).
  • I - Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA): Absolute lack of iron (Most common).
  • L - Lead Poisoning: Inhibits heme enzymes (ALA dehydratase, Ferrochelatase).
  • S - Sideroblastic Anemia: Defective protoporphyrin synthesis (Alcohol, Drugs, Genetic).

Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

Most common cause of anemia worldwide.

  • Causes:
    • Dietary: Inadequate intake (infants, vegetarians).
    • Impaired Absorption: Celiac disease, Gastrectomy.
    • Increased Demand: Pregnancy, Infancy, Adolescence.
    • Chronic Blood Loss: GI Bleeding (Peptic ulcer, Hookworm, Colon cancer), Menorrhagia.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Fatigue, Pallor.
    • Koilonychia: Spoon-shaped nails.
    • Pica: Craving for clay/dirt.
    • Plummer-Vinson Syndrome: Atrophic glossitis, Esophageal webs, Dysphagia.

3. INVESTIGATIONS AND DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

A stepwise approach is essential to differentiate the causes.

A. Preliminary Tests (CBC & Smear)

  • Hemoglobin: Low.
  • Indices: Low MCV, Low MCH, Low MCHC.
  • RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width): Increased in IDA (Anisocytosis); Normal in Thalassemia trait.
  • Platelets: Often elevated (Reactive Thrombocytosis) in IDA.

B. Iron Studies (The Differentiator)

This is the most critical step to distinguish IDA from ACD.

Parameter Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA) Anemia of Chronic Disease (ACD) Thalassemia Trait Sideroblastic Anemia
Serum Iron Decreased Decreased Normal / High High
TIBC (Transferrin) Increased Decreased / Normal Normal Normal
% Saturation < 15% (Very Low) Low / Normal Normal / High High
Serum Ferritin Low (< 30 ng/mL) Normal / High Normal / High High
Marrow Iron Absent Present (in macrophages) Present Ring Sideroblasts

C. Special Tests

  • Hb Electrophoresis (HPLC): For Thalassemia (Elevated HbA2 > 3.5% in Beta-Thal trait).
  • Bone Marrow Aspiration: Perls' Prussian Blue stain.
    • IDA: No stainable iron.
    • Sideroblastic: Ring Sideroblasts (Iron-laden mitochondria forming a ring around the nucleus).

4. PERIPHERAL BLOOD SMEAR IN MICROCYTIC ANEMIA

The morphology provides vital clues for diagnosis.

1. Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

  • Microcytic Hypochromic RBCs: Small cells with expanded central pallor (> 1/3rd of diameter).
  • Anisopoikilocytosis: Variation in size and shape (High RDW).
  • Specific Shapes:
    • Pencil Cells: Elliptical/cigar-shaped RBCs.
    • Target Cells: Occasional.
  • Platelets: Thrombocytosis is common.

2. Thalassemia Major

  • Severe Microcytic Hypochromic anemia.
  • Target Cells (Codocytes): Prominent.
  • Basophilic Stippling: Fine blue granules in RBCs.
  • Nucleated RBCs: Due to extreme marrow stress.

3. Sideroblastic Anemia

  • Dimorphic Picture: Dual population of cells - some are microcytic hypochromic, others are normocytic.
  • Pappenheimer Bodies: Iron granules seen in RBCs (Basophilic inclusions).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Describe Iron Metabolism in detail. Add a note on Hepcidin.

Introduction: Iron is a critical micronutrient. The body has no physiological mechanism to excrete excess iron, so homeostasis is strictly regulated at the level of absorption.

Body:
1. Absorption: Occurs in the duodenum. Fe3+ is reduced to Fe2+ and enters enterocytes via DMT1. It is stored as ferritin or exported to blood via Ferroportin.
2. Transport: Iron circulates bound to Transferrin.
3. Storage: Stored as Ferritin and Hemosiderin in the liver and RES.
4. Regulation (Hepcidin): Hepcidin is an acute-phase protein produced by the liver. It binds to Ferroportin and causes its degradation, effectively locking iron inside cells.

  • Inflammation: Increases Hepcidin → Iron trapping → Anemia of Chronic Disease.
  • Hypoxia/Iron deficiency: Suppresses Hepcidin → Increased absorption.

Conclusion: The Hepcidin-Ferroportin axis is the central control point of iron metabolism.

Q2: Discuss the Differential Diagnosis of Microcytic Hypochromic Anemia.

Introduction: Microcytic hypochromic anemia (MCV < 80 fL) is caused by defects in hemoglobin synthesis, either heme (Iron deficiency, Sideroblastic) or globin (Thalassemia).

Body:
1. Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA):

  • Cause: Blood loss, nutritional.
  • Labs: Low Ferritin, High TIBC, Low Saturation. Smear shows Pencil cells.

2. Anemia of Chronic Disease (ACD):
  • Cause: Chronic inflammation (Hepcidin block).
  • Labs: High/Normal Ferritin, Low TIBC.

3. Thalassemia Trait:
  • Cause: Globin gene defect.
  • Labs: Normal Ferritin, Normal RDW. Smear shows Target cells and Basophilic stippling. Hb Electrophoresis confirms diagnosis.

Conclusion: Serum Ferritin is the single most useful test to distinguish IDA from other causes.
