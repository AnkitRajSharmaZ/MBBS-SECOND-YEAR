TOPIC 21: BLOOD BANKING AND TRANSFUSION

Syllabus Scope: ABO/Rh Systems, Compatibility Testing, Blood Components, Transfusion Reactions (Investigation), and Autologous Transfusion.

1. BLOOD GROUP SYSTEMS

A. ABO Blood Group System

Discovered by Karl Landsteiner. It is the most clinically significant system due to the presence of naturally occurring antibodies.

  • Principle (Landsteiner's Law): If an antigen is present on the RBC surface, the corresponding antibody is absent in the plasma. If an antigen is absent, the corresponding antibody is present.
  • Antigens: Located on the RBC membrane. The basic precursor is the H Antigen.
    • Gene A: Codes for N-acetylgalactosaminyltransferase → Adds N-acetylgalactosamine (A antigen).
    • Gene B: Codes for Galactosyltransferase → Adds Galactose (B antigen).
    • Gene O: Amorphic (No enzyme) → H antigen remains unchanged.
  • Bombay Phenotype (Oh): Rare individuals lack the H gene (hh). They have no H, A, or B antigens but possess anti-A, anti-B, and anti-H antibodies. They can only receive Bombay blood.
Blood Group Antigen on RBC Antibody in Plasma Genotype
A A Anti-B (IgM) AA or AO
B B Anti-A (IgM) BB or BO
AB (Universal Recipient) A and B None AB
O (Universal Donor) None (H antigen) Anti-A and Anti-B (IgM & IgG) OO

B. Rh Blood Group System

Second most important system. Highly immunogenic.

  • Antigens: D, C, E, c, e. The D antigen is the most immunogenic.
  • Rh Positive (Rh+): Presence of D antigen (>85% population).
  • Rh Negative (Rh-): Absence of D antigen.
  • Clinical Significance:
    • Rh antibodies (Anti-D) are IgG and cross the placenta.
    • They result from immunization (pregnancy or transfusion).
    • Cause Hemolytic Disease of Newborn (HDN) and Delayed Hemolytic Transfusion Reactions.

2. COMPATIBILITY TESTING (CROSS-MATCHING)

Indications

  • Before transfusion of whole blood or packed red cells.
  • To prevent transfusion reactions (Hemolysis).
  • To maximize the survival of transfused red cells.

Principles

The test detects serological incompatibility between the donor and recipient. It involves:

  1. ABO/Rh Grouping: Of both donor and recipient.
  2. Antibody Screening: Detection of unexpected antibodies in patient’s serum (Indirect Coombs Test).
  3. Cross-match: Mixing donor RBCs with patient serum.

Steps of Compatibility Testing

1. Major Cross-match (Essential):

  • Mix: Patient's Serum + Donor's RBCs.
  • Purpose: Detects antibodies in the Patient that could destroy the donor cells. This prevents fatal acute hemolytic reactions.

2. Minor Cross-match (Obsolete):

  • Mix: Patient's RBCs + Donor's Serum.
  • Purpose: Detects antibodies in donor plasma. Rarely done now as donor units are screened for antibodies beforehand.

3. Phases of Testing:

  • Saline Phase (Room Temp): Detects IgM antibodies (ABO incompatibility).
  • Albumin/Thermic Phase (37°C): Detects IgG antibodies (Rh).
  • Antihuman Globulin (AHG) Phase (Coombs): Detects incomplete antibodies (Kell, Duffy).

3. BLOOD COMPONENTS AND CLINICAL USES

Component therapy allows one unit of blood to benefit multiple patients ("Rationale of Component Therapy").

Component Composition Storage / Shelf Life Clinical Indications
Whole Blood RBCs + Plasma + Platelets (non-functional) + WBCs. 2-6°C for 35-42 days (CPDA-1). Massive Hemorrhage (loss >25% blood vol), Exchange Transfusion.
Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBC) RBCs (Hct ~70-80%). Little plasma. 2-6°C for 35-42 days. Chronic Anemia, Acute blood loss (with crystalloids), Thalassemia. Avoids volume overload.

Cryoprecipitate Factor VIII, Fibrinogen, vWF, Factor XIII. -30°C for 1 year. Hemophilia A, von Willebrand Disease, Hypofibrinogenemia (DIC).

4. TRANSFUSION TRANSMITTED INFECTIONS (TTI)

Mandatory screening is done for the "Big Five" in many countries.

  • Viral:
    • HIV 1 & 2: Risk reduced by NAAT. Window period exists.
    • Hepatitis B (HBV): HBsAg screening. Highest risk of TTI among viruses.
    • Hepatitis C (HCV): Anti-HCV screening.
    • Others: CMV (dangerous for neonates/immunocompromised), HTLV, West Nile Virus.
  • Bacterial:
    • Syphilis (Treponema pallidum): Does not survive >72hrs at 4°C. Risk mainly with platelets.
    • Bacterial Contamination: Skin flora (Staph) or Yersinia enterocolitica (grows at 4°C). Common in platelets (room temp storage).
  • Parasitic:
    • Malaria: Can survive in stored blood. Screen donors for history/smear.
    • Babesiosis, Chagas disease, Leishmaniasis.

5. TRANSFUSION REACTIONS AND INVESTIGATION

Classification

  • Acute (Immediate): < 24 hours. (Hemolytic, Febrile, Allergic, TRALI, Sepsis).
  • Delayed: > 24 hours. (Delayed Hemolytic, GVHD, Iron Overload).
  • Immune vs. Non-Immune.

A. Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction (AHTR)

  • Cause: ABO Incompatibility (Clerical error is #1 cause).
  • Pathogenesis: Preformed IgM antibodies (Anti-A/B) bind donor RBCs → Fix Complement → Intravascular Hemolysis.
  • Features: Fever, chills, Flank pain, Hemoglobinuria (Red urine), Hypotension, DIC, Renal Failure.

B. Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reaction (Most Common)

  • Cause: Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6) accumulated in bag or Recipient antibodies against Donor WBCs/HLA.
  • Features: Fever (>1°C rise), chills. No hemolysis.
  • Prevention: Leukoreduction.

C. Allergic / Anaphylactic Reaction

  • Cause: Recipient has IgA Deficiency and has Anti-IgA antibodies reacting with donor IgA.
  • Features: Urticaria, wheezing, shock. No fever.

D. TRALI (Transfusion Related Acute Lung Injury)

  • Cause: Donor Anti-HLA antibodies activate recipient neutrophils in lungs.
  • Features: Non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema (ARDS).

Steps in Investigation of Transfusion Reaction

  1. STOP the transfusion immediately. Maintain IV line with saline.
  2. Clerical Check: Check bag label, patient ID, and paperwork for errors.
  3. Sample Collection: Send blood bag, new patient blood sample (EDTA & Clot), and urine sample to lab.
  4. Visual Check:
    • Look for Pink/Red Plasma in post-transfusion sample (Hemoglobinemia = Intravascular hemolysis).
    • Look for red urine (Hemoglobinuria).
  5. Serological Tests:
    • Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT/Coombs): Positive DAT indicates antibody coating on transfused RBCs.
    • Repeat ABO/Rh grouping on pre- and post-transfusion samples.
    • Repeat Cross-match (Major).
  6. Others: Culture bag (if sepsis suspected), Bilirubin, LDH, Haptoglobin.

6. AUTOLOGOUS TRANSFUSION

Definition: Collection and reinfusion of the patient's own blood.

Indications

  • Elective major surgeries (Orthopedic - Hip/Knee replacement, Cardiac surgery).
  • Patients with Rare Blood Groups (e.g., Bombay group) or multiple antibodies.
  • Religious beliefs (some Jehovah’s Witnesses may accept autologous cell salvage).

Principles and Advantages

  • Safety: Eliminates risk of TTIs (HIV, Hep B/C) and Alloimmunization.
  • No risk of hemolytic, febrile, or allergic reactions.
  • Stimulates patient's bone marrow (Erythropoiesis).

Types / Procedures

  1. Preoperative Autologous Donation (PAD):
    • Patient donates blood weekly (up to 72 hrs before surgery).
    • Requires Hb > 11 g/dL. Oral iron is given.
  2. Acute Normovolemic Hemodilution (ANH):
    • Blood is withdrawn in the OT just before surgery.
    • Volume is replaced with crystalloids/colloids (Hemodilution).
    • Blood is reinfused at the end of surgery. Preserves fresh platelets/factors.
  3. Intraoperative Cell Salvage:
    • Blood lost in the surgical field is aspirated, washed, filtered, and reinfused immediately.
    • Contraindicated in cancer surgery or infection.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Enumerate Blood Components. Describe the Indications and Storage of Platelet Concentrate and FFP.

Introduction: Blood component therapy ensures optimal use of a single donation for multiple patients. It minimizes volume overload and transfusion reactions.

Body:
Enumeration: Whole blood, Packed Red Cells, Platelet Concentrate (RDP/SDP), Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP), Cryoprecipitate.
1. Platelet Concentrate:

  • Storage: 20-24°C (Room Temp) with continuous agitation. Shelf life: 5 days.
  • Indications: Thrombocytopenia (<10,000/uL prophylactic; <50,000/uL before surgery), DIC, Functional platelet defects.

2. Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP):
  • Storage: -30°C for 1 year. Thawed at 37°C.
  • Indications: Liver disease (multiple factor deficiency), Warfarin reversal, DIC, Massive transfusion protocol.

Conclusion: Correct storage is vital; e.g., FFP loses Factor V and VIII if not frozen rapidly.

Q2: Define Cross-matching. Describe the phases and importance of Major Cross-match.

Introduction: Cross-matching is the final check of compatibility between donor and recipient to prevent life-threatening hemolysis.

Body:
Major Cross-match: Mixing Patient Serum with Donor RBCs.
Importance: It detects antibodies in the recipient that could destroy the transfused donor cells. This is crucial because the recipient's antibody volume is large compared to the donor's plasma.
Phases:

  1. Saline Phase (RT): Detects IgM (ABO) incompatibility.
  2. Albumin/Incubation Phase (37°C): Allows IgG antibodies (Rh) to bind.
  3. AHG Phase (Coombs): Uses Anti-Human Globulin to detect coated IgG antibodies (Kell, Duffy).

Conclusion: A compatible cross-match does not guarantee normal cell survival but prevents immediate acute hemolysis.
