TOPIC 06: MALE GENITAL TRACT

Syllabus Scope: Testicular Tumors, Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH), and Carcinoma of the Prostate.

1. TESTICULAR TUMORS

Classification (WHO)

Testicular tumors are broadly divided into two main groups:

  1. Germ Cell Tumors (GCTs): 95% of cases. Aggressive but curable.
    • Seminomatous Tumors: Seminoma (Classic), Spermatocytic Tumor.
    • Non-Seminomatous GCTs (NSGCT): Embryonal Carcinoma, Yolk Sac Tumor, Choriocarcinoma, Teratoma (Mature, Immature).
  2. Sex Cord-Stromal Tumors: Leydig cell tumor, Sertoli cell tumor (Mostly benign).

Etiology and Pathogenesis

  • Cryptorchidism: Undescended testis increases risk 3-5 fold.
  • Testicular Dysgenesis Syndrome (TDS): Association of cryptorchidism, hypospadias, and poor sperm quality.
  • Genetics:
    • Isochromosome 12p [i(12p)]: Found in virtually all GCTs (except Spermatocytic tumor).
    • Germ Cell Neoplasia In Situ (GCNIS): The precursor lesion for most GCTs. Originates in utero.

Pathology (Morphology)

1. Seminoma (Most Common)

  • Gross: Homogeneous, bulky, lobulated, grey-white mass. No hemorrhage or necrosis.
  • Microscopy: Sheets of uniform cells with clear cytoplasm (glycogen), distinct cell borders, and central nuclei. The septa contain a lymphocytic infiltrate.

2. Non-Seminomatous Tumors

  • Embryonal Carcinoma: Hemorrhagic and necrotic mass. Microscopy shows sheets of undifferentiated, pleomorphic cells forming glands/papillae.
  • Yolk Sac Tumor: Most common in infants/children. Reticular network of cells. Schiller-Duvall Bodies (glomeruloid structures) are pathognomonic.
  • Choriocarcinoma: Highly aggressive. Contains Cytotrophoblasts and Syncytiotrophoblasts. Hemorrhage is prominent.
  • Teratoma: Haphazard arrangement of tissues from all 3 germ layers (cartilage, epithelium, neural tissue).

Presenting Features

  • Painless enlargement of the testis ("Heaviness").
  • Acute pain (if hemorrhage/torsion occurs).
  • Metastatic symptoms (Back pain, dyspnea).

Diagnostic Tests (Tumor Markers)

Tumor Type hCG AFP (Alpha-fetoprotein) LDH
Seminoma Elevated (15%) Normal (Never raised) Elevated
Yolk Sac Tumor Normal Elevated (Always) Elevated
Choriocarcinoma Very High Normal Elevated
Embryonal Carcinoma Elevated Elevated Elevated

Progression and Spread

  • Lymphatic: Spread to Retroperitoneal (Para-aortic) nodes first. (Exceptions: Choriocarcinoma spreads via blood early).
  • Hematogenous: Lungs (cannonball metastases), Liver, Brain.
  • Difference: Seminomas spread via lymphatics first and are radiosensitive. NSGCTs spread early via blood and are radioresistant (need chemotherapy).

2. BENIGN PROSTATIC HYPERPLASIA (BPH)

Definition: Non-malignant enlargement of the prostate due to hyperplasia of both stromal and epithelial elements.

Pathogenesis (Hormonal Dependency)

  • DHT (Dihydrotestosterone): The main mediator. Testosterone is converted to DHT by Type 2 5-alpha-reductase in stromal cells.
  • DHT binds to androgen receptors, stimulating growth factors (FGF, TGF-beta) that cause proliferation of stromal and glandular cells.
  • Estrogens may sensitize cells to androgens.

Pathology

  • Zone: Arises in the Transition Zone (Periurethral).
  • Gross: Enlarged, nodular prostate. Weight increased (up to 100g). Cut surface shows milky fluid (glands) or solid grey areas (stroma).
  • Microscopy: Nodules of hyperplastic glands (lined by two layers: inner columnar, outer basal) and hyperplastic fibromuscular stroma.

Clinical Features (Prostatism)

  • Obstructive Symptoms: Hesitancy, weak stream, straining, dribbling, retention.
  • Irritative Symptoms: Frequency, urgency, nocturia (due to bladder instability).

Diagnostic Tests

  • Digital Rectal Exam (DRE): Enlarged, smooth, firm, rubbery, non-tender gland.
  • Uroflowmetry: Decreased flow rate.
  • PSA: Mildly elevated (usually < 4-10 ng/mL).

3. CARCINOMA OF THE PROSTATE

Definition: Adenocarcinoma of the prostate. Most common cancer in men.

Pathogenesis

  • Hormonal: Androgens are essential for tumor survival (Castration induces regression).
  • Genetics: TMPRSS2-ETS fusion gene (commonest). BRCA2 mutations.
  • Precursor: High-Grade Prostatic Intraepithelial Neoplasia (HGPIN).

Pathology

  • Zone: Arises in the Peripheral Zone (Posterior). Palpable on DRE.
  • Gross: Gritty, firm, ill-defined yellow-white lesion.
  • Microscopy: Crowded, small glands lined by a single layer of cells (Basal layer is ABSENT). Nuclei have prominent nucleoli.
  • Grading: Gleason System (Based on glandular architecture, not cytology).
    Score = Primary pattern (most dominant) + Secondary pattern. (e.g., 3+4 = 7). Score 6 is low grade, 8-10 is high grade.

Diagnostic Tests

  • PSA (Prostate Specific Antigen): Elevated (> 4 ng/mL). Useful for screening and monitoring recurrence. (Note: PSA is organ-specific, not cancer-specific).
  • DRE: Hard, nodular, irregular gland.
  • Biopsy: Transrectal Ultrasound (TRUS) guided biopsy (Gold standard).

Spread

  • Direct: Seminal vesicles, bladder base.
  • Lymphatic: Obturator, iliac nodes.
  • Hematogenous: Bone Metastasis (Osteoblastic/Sclerotic lesions) to lumbar spine, pelvis, femur.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Testicular Tumors. Differentiate between Seminoma and Non-Seminomatous Germ Cell Tumors (NSGCT).

Introduction: Testicular tumors are the most common malignancy in young men. 95% are Germ Cell Tumors.

Body:
Classification (WHO):

  • Germ Cell Tumors: Seminoma, Embryonal Carcinoma, Yolk Sac Tumor, Choriocarcinoma, Teratoma.
  • Sex Cord-Stromal Tumors: Leydig cell, Sertoli cell.

Differences:
Feature Seminoma NSGCT
Age 30-40 years 20-30 years
Pathology Homogeneous, lobulated, clear cells. Heterogeneous, necrotic, hemorrhagic.
Tumor Markers AFP is normal. hCG may be high. AFP/hCG often elevated.
Spread Lymphatic (Late). Hematogenous (Early).
Radiosensitivity Highly Sensitive. Radio-resistant (Chemo used).

Conclusion: Differentiation is crucial for treatment planning; Seminomas have a much better prognosis.

Q2: Differentiate between Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) and Prostatic Carcinoma.

Introduction: Both are common prostatic lesions in elderly men but differ in location and biological behavior.

Body:

Feature BPH Carcinoma
Location Transition Zone (Periurethral). Peripheral Zone (Posterior).
Pathogenesis DHT-mediated hyperplasia. Androgen-driven neoplasia (TMPRSS2-ETS).
Microscopy Nodular hyperplasia. Two cell layers (Basal + Luminal). Adenocarcinoma. Single cell layer (Basal layer lost). Prominent nucleoli.
Clinical Urinary obstruction (Early). Asymptomatic or Bone pain (Late).
DRE Finding Firm, smooth, elastic. Hard, nodular, irregular.

Conclusion: BPH causes urinary symptoms early, whereas Cancer is often silent until metastasis, highlighting the need for PSA screening.
