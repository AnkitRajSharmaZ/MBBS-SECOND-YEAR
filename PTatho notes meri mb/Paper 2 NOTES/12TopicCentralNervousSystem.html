TOPIC 12: CENTRAL NERVOUS SYSTEM

Syllabus Scope: Meningitis (Etiology, Pathogenesis, CSF findings) and CNS Tumors (Classification, Genetics, Pathology).

1. MENINGITIS

Definition: Meningitis is an inflammatory process of the leptomeninges (arachnoid and pia mater) and the CSF within the subarachnoid space.

Etiology and Types

1. Acute Pyogenic (Bacterial) Meningitis

Medical emergency.

  • Neonates: Escherichia coli, Group B Streptococci.
  • Infants/Children: Haemophilus influenzae (rare now due to vaccination), Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • Adolescents/Young Adults: Neisseria meningitidis (Meningococcal).
  • Elderly: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Listeria monocytogenes.

2. Acute Aseptic (Viral) Meningitis

  • Usually benign and self-limiting.
  • Agents: Enteroviruses (Echovirus, Coxsackie), Mumps, HSV-2, HIV.

3. Chronic Meningitis

  • Tuberculous: Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
  • Fungal: Cryptococcus neoformans (in HIV/AIDS), Candida.
  • Spirochetal: Neurosyphilis, Lyme disease.

Pathogenesis

Most commonly spread via Hematogenous dissemination.
• Bacteria colonize nasopharynx → Enter blood → Cross Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) → Multiply in CSF (low complement/antibody levels in CSF facilitate growth).
• Bacterial toxins induce cytokine release (TNF, IL-1) → Increased BBB permeability → Cerebral Edema & Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP).

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Gross:
    • Pyogenic: Exudate in the subarachnoid space. Brain is swollen. Vessels are engorged.
      - H. influenzae: Basal exudate.
      - Pneumococcal: Exudate over cerebral convexities.
    • Tuberculous: Gelatinous or fibrinous exudate at the Base of the brain. Tubercles may be seen.
  • Microscopy:
    • Pyogenic: Numerous Neutrophils filling the subarachnoid space. Gram stain shows bacteria.
    • Tuberculous: Lymphocytes, Monocytes, and Granulomas (Caseating) with Langhans giant cells. Arteritis (inflammation of vessel walls) leading to infarction.
    • Viral: Mild lymphocytic infiltrate. No organisms.

2. CNS TUMORS

Unique features: They rarely metastasize outside the CNS but are locally destructive and lethal due to location.

Classification (WHO)

  1. Gliomas (Most common): Astrocytoma, Oligodendroglioma, Ependymoma.
  2. Neuronal Tumors: Ganglioglioma, Neurocytoma.
  3. Embryonal Tumors: Medulloblastoma.
  4. Meningiomas: Tumors of meninges.
  5. Metastatic Tumors: From Lung, Breast, Melanoma.

A. Astrocytoma

  • Diffuse Astrocytoma (Grade II): Ill-defined, infiltrating. IDH mutation positive.
  • Anaplastic Astrocytoma (Grade III): Higher cellularity, mitoses.
  • Glioblastoma (GBM) (Grade IV): Most common malignant CNS tumor in adults.
    • Morphology: Variegated appearance (necrosis and hemorrhage).
      Microscopy: Pseudopalisading Necrosis (tumor cells line up around necrotic zones) and Microvascular Proliferation (glomeruloid bodies).
    • Genetics: EGFR amplification, TERT promoter mutation.
  • Pilocytic Astrocytoma (Grade I): Benign, cystic tumor in children (Cerebellum).
    Microscopy: Bipolar hair-like cells with Rosenthal Fibers (eosinophilic corkscrew structures). BRAF mutation.

B. Meningioma

  • Definition: Benign tumor of arachnoid meningothelial cells. Attached to dura.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Firm, rounded, well-circumscribed masses pressing on brain (compression, not invasion).
    • Microscopy: Whorled pattern of cells. Psammoma Bodies (calcifications) are characteristic.
  • Genetics: Loss of NF2 gene (Merlin) on Chromosome 22.

C. Medulloblastoma

  • Definition: Highly malignant embryonal tumor in Children (Cerebellum).
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Midline cerebellar mass obstructing 4th ventricle → Hydrocephalus.
    • Microscopy: Sheets of Small Round Blue Cells (anaplastic). Homer-Wright Rosettes (neuroblastic differentiation).
  • Complications: "Drop Metastases" via CSF to spinal cord.

Clinical Presentation & Sequelae

  • Mass Effect: Headache (worse in morning), vomiting, papilledema (Raised ICP).
  • Focal Deficits: Seizures, paralysis, aphasia (depending on location).
  • Herniation: Uncal herniation (pupil dilation), Tonsillar herniation (respiratory arrest).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Identify the etiology of meningitis based on CSF findings: Turbid fluid, High Neutrophils, Low Glucose, High Protein.

Introduction: CSF analysis is critical for differentiating meningitis types.

Answer:
The pattern described—Turbid appearance, Neutrophilic pleocytosis, Markedly reduced Glucose ("Hypoglycorrhachia"), and High Protein—is diagnostic of Acute Bacterial (Pyogenic) Meningitis.
Reasoning: Bacteria consume glucose and release proteins. Neutrophils are the first responders to bacterial infection. In contrast, Viral meningitis has normal glucose and lymphocytes. TB meningitis has lymphocytes and low glucose but usually forms a cobweb clot.

Q2: Classify CNS Tumors. Describe the morphology of Glioblastoma.

Introduction: CNS tumors are classified based on their cell of origin (glial vs non-glial). Glioblastoma (GBM) is the most aggressive astrocytic tumor (WHO Grade IV).

Body:
Classification:

  • Astrocytic: Pilocytic (I), Diffuse (II), Anaplastic (III), GBM (IV).
  • Oligodendroglial: Oligodendroglioma.
  • Ependymal: Ependymoma.
  • Meningeal: Meningioma.
  • Embryonal: Medulloblastoma.

Morphology of Glioblastoma:
  • Gross: A large, infiltrating, hemorrhagic, and necrotic mass often crossing the corpus callosum ("Butterfly Glioma").
  • Microscopy: Highly anaplastic astrocytes. The two diagnostic hallmarks are:
    1. Pseudopalisading Necrosis: Tumor cells crowd around irregular areas of necrosis.
    2. Microvascular Proliferation: Proliferating blood vessels form glomeruloid tufts.

Conclusion: GBM has a dismal prognosis despite surgery and chemoradiation.
