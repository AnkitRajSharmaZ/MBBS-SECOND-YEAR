TOPIC 05: URINARY TRACT PATHOLOGY

Syllabus Scope: Renal Failure, Glomerulonephritis (IgA, Lupus, Diabetic), Cystic Diseases, Tumors, and Tubulointerstitial Diseases (ATN, Pyelonephritis, Stones).

1. RENAL FAILURE

Definition: A significant loss of renal function involving the retention of nitrogenous wastes (Azotemia) and derangement of fluid/electrolyte balance.

A. Acute Renal Failure (ARF) / Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

  • Definition: Rapid decline in GFR (hours to days) causing oliguria or anuria and azotemia.
  • Etiology:
    • Pre-renal: Hypovolemia, Heart failure (Ischemia).
    • Renal: Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN), Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN).
    • Post-renal: Obstruction (Stones, BPH).
  • Morphology: Depends on cause (e.g., swollen pale cortex in ATN).
  • Labs: Rapid rise in Creatinine and Urea. Hyperkalemia. Metabolic Acidosis.

B. Chronic Renal Failure (CRF) / Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

  • Definition: Progressive, irreversible destruction of nephrons over months/years. End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) is the terminal stage (GFR < 5% normal).
  • Etiology: Diabetes (most common), Hypertension, Chronic Glomerulonephritis, Polycystic Kidney Disease.
  • Pathogenesis of Uremia: Retention of toxins (urea, creatinine, phenols) affects all organ systems.
    • Intact Nephron Hypothesis: Remaining nephrons undergo compensatory hypertrophy (super-nephrons) but eventually fail due to sclerosis.
  • Morphology: Kidneys are typically symmetrically contracted (small), granular surface, and firm (fibrosis). Exception: Normal/Large in Diabetic Nephropathy, Amyloidosis, PKD.
  • Clinical Features (Uremic Syndrome):
    • Fluid/Electrolytes: Edema, Hyperkalemia, Metabolic Acidosis.
    • Hematologic: Normocytic Anemia (Low Erythropoietin), Bleeding (Platelet dysfunction).
    • Bone: Renal Osteodystrophy (due to secondary Hyperparathyroidism).
    • Skin: Uremic Frost, Pruritus.

2. GLOMERULAR DISEASES (GLOMERULONEPHRITIS)

Disorders primarily affecting the glomerulus.

Classification (Clinical Syndromes)

  1. Nephritic Syndrome: Hematuria (RBC casts), HTN, Mild Proteinuria, Oliguria. (e.g., Post-Streptococcal GN).
  2. Nephrotic Syndrome: Massive Proteinuria (>3.5g/day), Hypoalbuminemia, Edema, Hyperlipidemia. (e.g., Minimal Change Disease, Membranous GN).
  3. Rapidly Progressive GN (RPGN): Rapid loss of function with Crescents in Bowman's space.
  4. Chronic GN: End-stage glomerular fibrosis.

Pathogenesis of Glomerular Injury

Most are immune-mediated.

  • Immune Complex Deposition:
    • Circulating Complexes: Trapped in glomerulus (e.g., SLE, Post-Strep).
    • In-Situ Formation: Antibodies react with intrinsic glomerular antigens (e.g., Anti-GBM in Goodpasture) or planted antigens.
  • Cell-Mediated: Sensitized T-cells cause injury (e.g., Minimal Change Disease).
  • Mediators: Complement activation (C5b-9 MAC), Neutrophils, Monocytes, Platelets, Cytokines.

Specific Glomerular Diseases

1. IgA Nephropathy (Berger Disease): Most common GN worldwide.

  • Pathogenesis: Increased mucosal IgA synthesis (after respiratory/GI infection) → Deposition of IgA immune complexes in the Mesangium. Activates Complement (Alternative pathway).
  • Morphology: Mesangial proliferation. IF shows granular IgA deposits.
  • Clinical: Recurrent gross hematuria in young adults.

2. Diabetic Nephropathy:

  • Pathogenesis: Metabolic defects (Advanced Glycation End-products), Hemodynamic changes (Hyperfiltration), oxidative stress.
  • Morphology:
    • Capillary Basement Membrane Thickening.
    • Diffuse Glomerulosclerosis: Matrix increase.
    • Nodular Glomerulosclerosis: Kimmelstiel-Wilson (KW) Nodules (Pathognomonic) - Acellular, PAS+ nodules in mesangium.
    • Armanni-Ebstein Lesion: Glycogen deposits in tubular epithelium.

3. Lupus Nephritis (SLE):

  • Pathogenesis: Deposition of DNA-Anti-DNA immune complexes.
  • WHO Classification (Classes I-VI):
    • Class I: Minimal mesangial.
    • Class II: Mesangial proliferative.
    • Class III: Focal proliferative.
    • Class IV: Diffuse Proliferative (Most common & severe). Shows "Wire-loop" lesions (Subendothelial deposits).
    • Class V: Membranous (Subepithelial deposits).
    • Class VI: Advanced Sclerosing.

3. CYSTIC DISEASES OF THE KIDNEY

Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)

  • Genetics: Mutation in PKD1 gene (Chr 16, Polycystin-1) - 85% cases; or PKD2 gene (Chr 4, Polycystin-2) - 15%.
  • Pathogenesis: Defective polycystin affects cilium function ("Ciliopathy") → Altered calcium signaling → Tubular epithelial proliferation & fluid secretion → Cysts.
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Bilaterally Enlarged Kidneys (massive, up to 4kg). Surface studded with numerous cysts (fluid-filled).
    • Microscopy: Cysts arise from all parts of the nephron. Intervening parenchyma is atrophic/fibrotic.
  • Clinical: Manifests in adults (30-50 yrs). Flank pain, Hematuria, Hypertension, Renal failure.
  • Extra-renal Anomalies: Liver cysts (40%), Berry Aneurysms (Circle of Willis - risk of subarachnoid hemorrhage).

Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD)

  • Genetics: Mutation in PKHD1 gene (Fibrocystin).
  • Pathology: Kidneys enlarged but smooth surface. Cysts are elongated channels (dilated collecting ducts) arranged radially (Spongy appearance).
  • Clinical: Infants/Children. Associated with Congenital Hepatic Fibrosis.

4. TUMORS OF THE KIDNEY

A. Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC)

Most common malignant tumor of kidney (Adults). Arises from tubular epithelium.

  • Risk Factors: Smoking (strongest), Obesity, HTN, Cadmium exposure, von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) syndrome.
  • Classification & Pathology:
    1. Clear Cell RCC (70-80%):
      • Genetics: Loss of VHL gene (3p).
      • Morphology: Solitary, large, yellow mass (lipid) with hemorrhage/necrosis.
        Microscopy: Nests of cells with Clear Cytoplasm (Glycogen/Lipid) separated by delicate vascular septa ("Chicken-wire" vasculature).
      • Spread: Tendency to invade Renal Vein.
    2. Papillary RCC (10-15%): Papillary growth pattern. Psammoma bodies. Multifocal.
    3. Chromophobe RCC (5%): Pale eosinophilic cells with Perinuclear Halo. Excellent prognosis.
  • Clinical Triad: Hematuria, Flank pain, Palpable mass (seen in only 10%). Paraneoplastic syndromes (Polycythemia, Hypercalcemia).

B. Wilms Tumor (Nephroblastoma)

Most common renal tumor in Children.
Genetics: WT1 mutation.
Morphology: Triphasic histology - Blastema (small blue cells), Epithelium (tubules), Stroma.

5. UROTHELIAL TUMORS (BLADDER CANCER)

90% are Urothelial Carcinomas (Transitional Cell Carcinoma).

  • Etiology: Smoking, Arylamines (Dye industry), Schistosomiasis (Squamous cell ca), Cyclophosphamide.
  • Pathogenesis: Two pathways:
    1. FGFR3 mutation pathway: Leads to Low-grade papillary tumors (Recurrent, but rarely invade).
    2. TP53 mutation pathway: Leads to High-grade invasive tumors (Carcinoma in situ precursor).
  • Morphology: Papillary exophytic masses (resembling sea anemone). Invasion of muscularis propria determines prognosis.
  • Clinical: Painless Hematuria.

6. TUBULOINTERSTITIAL DISEASES

Diseases affecting tubules and interstitium.
Classification: Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN), Tubulointerstitial Nephritis (Infectious/Drug-induced).

A. Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

Most common cause of Acute Renal Failure. Reversible injury to tubular epithelium.

  • Etiology:
    • Ischemic: Shock, hypotension (affects patchy segments).
    • Toxic: Drugs (Gentamicin), Heavy metals (Mercury), Myoglobin (Rhabdomyolysis). Affects extensive continuous segments (Proximal tubule).
  • Pathogenesis: Tubular cell injury (loss of polarity) + Vasoconstriction. Sloughed cells form casts causing obstruction.
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Pale swollen cortex, congested medulla.
    • Microscopy: Necrosis of epithelial cells (loss of nuclei), attenuation of brush border. Granular/Muddy Brown Casts in lumens.
  • Stages: Initiation, Maintenance (Oliguria), Recovery (Polyuria).

B. Pyelonephritis (Infection)

Inflammation of tubules, interstitium, and renal pelvis.

1. Acute Pyelonephritis

  • Etiology: Bacterial infection (E. coli). Usually Ascending infection (UTI).
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Yellowish abscesses on cortical surface.
    • Microscopy: Intratubular Neutrophils (Pus casts), interstitial inflammation. Glomeruli spared.
  • Complications: Papillary Necrosis (in Diabetics), Pyonephrosis.

2. Chronic Pyelonephritis & Reflux Nephropathy

  • Etiology: Chronic obstruction or Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR) in childhood.
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Irregular, broad, U-shaped corticomedullary scars (polar scarring).
    • Microscopy: Atrophic tubules containing eosinophilic proteinaceous material resembling thyroid follicles (Thyroidization of kidney). Chronic inflammation, fibrosis.

7. RENAL STONE DISEASE (UROLITHIASIS)

Etiology and Pathogenesis

Formation requires supersaturation of urine, nucleation centers, and lack of inhibitors (citrate).

Types and Morphology

  1. Calcium Stones (70%): Ca-Oxalate/Phosphate.
    Cause: Hypercalciuria, Hypercalcemia. Hard, radiopaque.
  2. Struvite Stones (Triple Stones): Mg-Ammonium-Phosphate.
    Cause: Infection by urease-producing bacteria (Proteus). Forms Staghorn Calculi (fills renal pelvis).
  3. Uric Acid Stones: Radiolucent. Seen in Gout, acidic urine.
  4. Cystine Stones: Genetic defect in amino acid transport.

Complications (Obstructive Uropathy)

  • Hydronephrosis: Dilation of renal pelvis/calyces and atrophy of parenchyma due to obstruction.
  • Infection (Pyelonephritis).
  • Renal Failure (if bilateral obstruction).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Glomerulonephritis. Describe the pathogenesis and morphology of Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN).

Introduction: Glomerulonephritis (GN) is inflammation of the glomeruli. PSGN is a classic example of nephritic syndrome occurring 1-4 weeks after a streptococcal infection.

Body:
Classification:

  • Nephritic Syndrome: PSGN, RPGN.
  • Nephrotic Syndrome: Minimal Change, Membranous, FSGS.
  • Chronic GN.

Pathogenesis of PSGN: It is an Immune Complex disease (Type III Hypersensitivity). Antibodies formed against Strep antigens (SpeB) circulate and deposit in the glomerulus or form in-situ. This activates Complement, attracting neutrophils.
Morphology:
  • Light Microscopy: Diffuse hypercellularity (proliferation of mesangial/endothelial cells + neutrophils).
  • Immunofluorescence: "Starry Sky" or granular pattern of IgG and C3 deposits.
  • Electron Microscopy: Characteristic Subepithelial Humps (immune deposits).

Conclusion: Prognosis is excellent in children (95% recover), but worse in adults.

Q2: Define Renal Failure. Discuss the Etiopathogenesis and Pathology of Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN).

Introduction: ATN is the destruction of tubular epithelial cells and is the most common cause of acute renal failure (intrinsic type).

Body:
Etiopathogenesis:

  • Ischemic ATN: Due to hypotension (shock). Affects straight segment of proximal tubule and thick ascending limb (patchy).
  • Nephrotoxic ATN: Poisons/Drugs (Gentamicin, Mercury). Affects proximal convoluted tubule (diffuse).

Mechanism: Injury causes cell detachment and cast formation, leading to tubular obstruction, back-leak of filtrate, and vasoconstriction → GFR drops.
Pathology:
  • Gross: Pale cortex, congested medulla.
  • Microscopy: Coagulative necrosis of epithelium, loss of nuclei, attenuation of brush border. Tubules are dilated and contain dirty brown granular casts.

Conclusion: Tubular cells have regenerative capacity; thus, ATN is reversible if the patient is supported during the oliguric phase.
