TOPIC 07: FEMALE GENITAL TRACT

Syllabus Scope: Carcinoma of Cervix, Endometrium, Leiomyoma/Sarcoma, Ovarian Tumors, and Gestational Trophoblastic Neoplasms.

1. CARCINOMA OF THE CERVIX

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

  • One of the most common cancers in women worldwide (especially in developing countries).
  • Peak Age: 45-55 years (Invasive carcinoma); CIN occurs earlier (30s).
  • Risk Factors:
    • Human Papillomavirus (HPV): The single most important factor. Detected in >99% of cases.
    • Early age of first intercourse and multiple sexual partners.
    • Multiparity (high number of births).
    • Oral contraceptive pills (long-term use).
    • Smoking (co-carcinogen).
    • Immunosuppression (HIV).

Etiology and Pathogenesis (Role of HPV)

  • High-Risk HPV Types: HPV 16 (most common, SCC) and HPV 18 (Adenocarcinoma).
  • Mechanism: HPV integrates its DNA into the host genome and expresses oncoproteins:
    • E6 protein: Binds and degrades p53 (tumor suppressor), preventing apoptosis.
    • E7 protein: Binds Retinoblastoma (RB) protein, releasing E2F transcription factor, pushing cell into cell cycle.
  • Result: Uncontrolled cell proliferation and genomic instability.

Precursor Lesions: CIN (Cervical Intraepithelial Neoplasia)

Pre-invasive stage arising at the Squamocolumnar Junction (Transformation Zone).

  • CIN I (Mild Dysplasia / LSIL): Dysplastic cells in lower 1/3 of epithelium. Koilocytic change (viral effect) is prominent.
  • CIN II (Moderate Dysplasia / HSIL): Dysplasia in lower 2/3.
  • CIN III (Severe Dysplasia / Carcinoma in Situ / HSIL): Dysplasia involves full thickness of epithelium. Basement membrane is intact.

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Gross:
    • Exophytic: Fungating, cauliflower-like mass (most common).
    • Ulcerative: Necrotic ulcer with indurated margins.
    • Infiltrative: Stone-hard cervix ("Barrel cervix").
  • Microscopy:
    • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (80%): Nests of malignant squamous cells invading the stroma. May show keratin pearls (well-differentiated) or be anaplastic.
    • Adenocarcinoma (15%): Glandular pattern. Associated with HPV 18.

Screening and Diagnosis

  • Pap Smear (Screening): Exfoliative cytology to detect SIL (Squamous Intraepithelial Lesion).
    Bethesda System: ASC-US, LSIL (Low grade), HSIL (High grade).
  • Colposcopy: Visualization of cervix with acetic acid (acetowhite areas) and Iodine (Schiller test).
  • Biopsy: Gold standard for diagnosis.

2. CARCINOMA OF THE ENDOMETRIUM

Most common invasive cancer of the female genital tract in developed countries. Divided into two pathogenic types.

Classification: Type I vs Type II

Etiology Unopposed Estrogen (Obesity, Nulliparity, PCOS) Estrogen Independent (Atrophy)

Pathology

  • Gross: Polypoid, fungating mass filling the uterine cavity. Myometrial invasion may be present.
  • Microscopy:
    • Endometrioid: Closely packed, back-to-back irregular glands with no intervening stroma. Cribriforming is common.
    • Serous: Papillary structures with high-grade nuclear atypia and Psammoma bodies.

Clinical Features

  • Post-menopausal bleeding (Cardinal symptom).
  • Diagnosis via Endometrial Biopsy / Curettage.

3. MYOMETRIAL TUMORS: LEIOMYOMA & LEIOMYOSARCOMA

A. Leiomyoma (Fibroid)

  • Definition: Benign smooth muscle tumor. Most common tumor in women (75% of reproductive age).
  • Pathogenesis: Estrogen and Progesterone dependent (shrink after menopause). Genetics: MED12 mutations.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Multiple, well-circumscribed, firm, grey-white masses. Whorled cut surface.
      Locations: Intramural (wall), Submucosal (cavity), Subserosal (surface).
    • Microscopy: Interlacing bundles of uniform smooth muscle cells with oval nuclei. No atypia, no necrosis.
  • Complications: Menorrhagia, Infertility, Pain, Red degeneration (in pregnancy).

B. Leiomyosarcoma

  • Definition: Malignant smooth muscle tumor. Rare.
  • Origin: Arises de novo from myometrium (DOES NOT arise from preexisting leiomyomas).
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Solitary, soft, hemorrhagic, necrotic, fish-flesh mass.
    • Microscopy: Diagnosed by presence of: 1. Tumor Necrosis. 2. Cytologic Atypia. 3. Mitotic Activity (>10 mitoses/10 HPF).
  • Prognosis: Poor. Spreads via blood to lungs.

4. OVARIAN TUMORS

Classified by the WHO based on cell of origin.

WHO Classification

  1. Surface Epithelial Tumors (65-70%): Serous, Mucinous, Endometrioid, Brenner.
  2. Germ Cell Tumors (15-20%): Teratoma, Dysgerminoma, Yolk Sac Tumor, Choriocarcinoma.
  3. Sex Cord-Stromal Tumors (5-10%): Granulosa Cell Tumor, Fibroma, Sertoli-Leydig.
  4. Metastasis: Krukenberg Tumor (from Stomach).

1. Surface Epithelial Tumors

  • Pathogenesis: "Incessant ovulation" theory (trauma to surface epithelium). BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations (High-grade serous).
  • Serous Tumors: Most common.
    • Benign (Cystadenoma): Smooth cyst wall, simple columnar lining.
    • Malignant (Cystadenocarcinoma): Solid/cystic, papillary projections. Invasion of stroma. Psammoma bodies (calcifications) are characteristic.
  • Mucinous Tumors: Large, multiloculated cysts filled with sticky gelatinous fluid. Lined by mucin-secreting epithelium. Rupture causes Pseudomyxoma Peritonei (Jelly belly).

2. Germ Cell Tumors

  • Teratoma: Derived from all 3 germ layers.
    • Mature (Benign Cystic Teratoma / Dermoid Cyst): Cyst containing hair, teeth, sebum (Rokitansky protuberance). Malignant transformation is rare (SCC).
    • Immature (Malignant): Contains immature neuroepithelium.
  • Dysgerminoma: Female counterpart of Seminoma. Sheets of large vesicular cells with lymphocytes. Radiosensitive.

3. Sex Cord-Stromal Tumors

  • Granulosa Cell Tumor: Produces Estrogen (precocious puberty or endometrial hyperplasia).
    Microscopy: Call-Exner Bodies (follicle-like structures filled with eosinophilic material) and "Coffee-bean" nuclei.
  • Fibroma: Benign. Associated with Meigs Syndrome (Ovarian fibroma + Ascites + Pleural effusion).

5. GESTATIONAL TROPHOBLASTIC NEOPLASMS (GTN)

Tumors arising from placental tissue (trophoblast).

A. Hydatidiform Mole

Characterized by cystic swelling of chorionic villi and variable trophoblastic proliferation.

Feature Complete Mole Partial Mole
Karyotype 46, XX (All paternal DNA). Empty egg + 1 sperm (duplicated). 69, XXY (Triploid). Normal egg + 2 sperms.
Fetal Parts Absent. Present.
Villi Edema Diffuse (All villi). "Bunch of grapes". Focal (Some villi).
hCG Levels Markedly Elevated. Mildly Elevated.
Risk of Choriocarcinoma 2.5% risk. Very rare.

B. Choriocarcinoma

  • Definition: Highly malignant, aggressive tumor of trophoblasts.
  • Etiology: 50% follow a Complete Mole, 25% follow abortion, 25% follow normal pregnancy.
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Soft, fleshy, hemorrhagic, necrotic mass in uterus.
    • Microscopy: Biphasic proliferation of Cytotrophoblasts and Syncytiotrophoblasts. Villi are ABSENT (Key feature distinguishing from mole). Extensive hemorrhage and necrosis.
  • Clinical Course: Rapid hematogenous spread (Lungs, Vagina, Brain). Produces very high Beta-hCG.
    Treatment: remarkably sensitive to Chemotherapy (Methotrexate). Nearly 100% cure rate.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Describe the Pathogenesis and Laboratory Diagnosis of Cervical Cancer.

Introduction: Cervical cancer is a preventable malignancy strongly associated with persistent high-risk HPV infection.

Body:
Pathogenesis:

  • HPV (Types 16, 18) infects the basal cells at the Transformation Zone.
  • Viral oncoproteins E6 and E7 inactivate host tumor suppressors p53 and RB, respectively.
  • This leads to genomic instability and progression from CIN I (mild dysplasia) to CIN III (carcinoma in situ) to Invasive Carcinoma.

Laboratory Diagnosis:
  1. Screening (Pap Smear): Identifies dysplastic cells (High N:C ratio, hyperchromasia, koilocytes). Classified by Bethesda System.
  2. Colposcopy: Visualizes acetowhite lesions for targeted biopsy.
  3. Biopsy: Confirms invasion.
  4. HPV DNA Testing: Detection of high-risk viral types.

Conclusion: Vaccination (Gardasil) and screening have significantly reduced mortality.

Q2: Classify Ovarian Tumors. Describe the morphology of Serous Cystadenocarcinoma and Teratoma.

Introduction: Ovarian tumors are diverse, classified by the WHO into Surface Epithelial, Germ Cell, and Sex Cord-Stromal tumors.

Body:
1. Serous Cystadenocarcinoma (Epithelial):

  • Gross: Large, bilateral, cystic masses with solid, friable papillary projections.
  • Microscopy: Complex branching papillae lined by atypical stratified epithelium. Invasion of stroma is present. Psammoma bodies (concentric calcifications) are a hallmark.

2. Mature Cystic Teratoma (Germ Cell):
  • Gross: Unilocular cyst containing sebaceous material ("gruel") and matted hair. A solid area called Rokitansky protuberance may contain teeth or bone.
  • Microscopy: Tissues from Ectoderm (skin, brain), Mesoderm (cartilage, fat), and Endoderm (thyroid, respiratory lining). Usually benign.

Conclusion: Serous carcinoma is the most common malignant ovarian tumor, while Dermoid cyst is the most common benign one in young women.

Q3: Differentiate between Complete and Partial Hydatidiform Mole.

Introduction: Hydatidiform moles are abnormal pregnancies characterized by trophoblastic proliferation and hydropic swelling of villi.

Body:

Genetics 46, XX (Diploid, androgenetic) 69, XXY (Triploid)
Embryo/Fetus Absent Present (abnormal)
Villi All villi enlarged (hydropic) Mix of normal and edematous villi

Conclusion: Complete moles have a higher risk of persistent disease and require strict hCG monitoring.
