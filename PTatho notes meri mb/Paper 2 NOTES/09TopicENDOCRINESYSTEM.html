TOPIC 09: ENDOCRINE SYSTEM

Syllabus Scope: Thyroid disorders (Goiter, Hyper/Hypothyroidism, Tumors), Diabetes Mellitus, Hyperparathyroidism, Pancreatic Cancer, Adrenal disorders (Cushing's, Insufficiency, Neoplasms).

1. THYROID SWELLINGS (GOITER)

Definition: Goiter is a generalized enlargement of the thyroid gland. It is a compensatory hypertrophy and hyperplasia of follicular epithelium in response to thyroid hormone deficiency.

Classification & Etiology

  1. Diffuse Nontoxic (Simple) Goiter:
    • Endemic: Iodine deficiency (most common cause worldwide). Seen in mountainous regions (e.g., Himalayas).
    • Sporadic: Goitrogens (cabbage, cauliflower), enzyme defects, puberty, pregnancy.
  2. Multinodular Goiter (MNG): Long-standing simple goiter leads to recurrent episodes of hyperplasia and involution, resulting in nodularity.

Pathogenesis

Iodine deficiency → Decreased T3/T4 synthesis → Negative feedback stimulates Pituitary to secrete TSH → TSH causes Hypertrophy & Hyperplasia of thyroid follicles → Goiter.

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Hyperplastic Phase: Diffuse enlargement. Follicles lined by tall columnar epithelium. Scant colloid.
  • Colloid Involution: If iodine supply increases, follicles fill with colloid and epithelium flattens.
  • Multinodular Goiter:
    • Gross: Asymmetric, extreme enlargement. Cut surface shows irregular nodules, gelatinous colloid, hemorrhage, fibrosis, and calcification.
    • Microscopy: Colloid-rich macrofollicles and small hyperplastic microfollicles.

2. THYROTOXICOSIS & HYPERTHYROIDISM

Definitions

  • Thyrotoxicosis: Hypermetabolic state caused by elevated circulating free T3 and T4.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Thyrotoxicosis caused by hyperfunction of the thyroid gland itself.

Etiology

  1. Diffuse Toxic Hyperplasia (Graves' Disease): Most common (85%).
  2. Toxic Multinodular Goiter (Plummer Disease).
  3. Toxic Adenoma.

Graves' Disease (Pathogenesis)

An autoimmune disorder caused by Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulins (TSI).

  • Mechanism: TSI mimics TSH and binds to the TSH receptor on thyroid cells → Stimulates adenylate cyclase → Increased T3/T4 release.
  • Triad: 1. Thyrotoxicosis. 2. Ophthalmopathy (Exophthalmos). 3. Dermopathy (Pretibial Myxedema).

Clinical Features

  • Metabolic: Heat intolerance, Weight loss despite increased appetite.
  • Cardiac: Tachycardia, Palpitations, Atrial Fibrillation.
  • Neuromuscular: Fine tremors, Anxiety, Hyperreflexia.
  • Ocular: Stare, lid lag, Proptosis (in Graves').

Laboratory Findings

  • TSH: Decreased (Primary).
  • Free T3/T4: Increased.
  • RAIU (Radioactive Iodine Uptake): Increased diffuse uptake.

3. HYPOTHYROIDISM

Structural or functional derangement resulting in inadequate thyroid hormone production.

Etiology

  • Primary: Hashimoto Thyroiditis (Autoimmune), Iodine Deficiency, Post-ablation (I-131, Surgery).
  • Secondary: Pituitary failure (Sheehan syndrome).

Hashimoto Thyroiditis (Pathogenesis)

Autoimmune destruction of the thyroid gland.

  • Mechanism: CD8+ Cytotoxic T-cell mediated killing of thyrocytes + Anti-thyroid antibodies (Anti-TPO, Anti-Thyroglobulin).
  • Pathology:
    • Gross: Diffusely enlarged, firm, pale.
    • Microscopy: Intense Lymphocytic Infiltrate with Germinal Centers. Atrophic follicles lined by Hürthle Cells (eosinophilic granular cytoplasm).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Cretinism (Infancy/Childhood): Severe mental retardation, short stature, coarse facial features, protruding tongue.
  • Myxedema (Adults): Mental sluggishness, Cold intolerance, Weight gain, Constipation, Non-pitting edema (accumulation of GAGs), Bradycardia.

4. THYROID TUMORS

Classification

  1. Benign: Follicular Adenoma.
  2. Malignant:
    • Papillary Carcinoma (85%) - Best prognosis.
    • Follicular Carcinoma (5-15%).
    • Medullary Carcinoma (5%) - Neuroendocrine.
    • Anaplastic Carcinoma (<5%) - Worst prognosis.

Pathology of Key Tumors

1. Papillary Carcinoma

  • Genetics: BRAF mutation, RET/PTC rearrangement. Radiation exposure is a risk factor.
  • Microscopy (Hallmarks):
    • Complex branching papillae.
    • Orphan Annie Eye Nuclei: Optically clear, empty-appearing nuclei.
    • Nuclear Grooves and Intranuclear Inclusions (Pseudo-inclusions).
    • Psammoma Bodies: Concentric calcifications.
  • Spread: Lymphatic spread to cervical nodes.

2. Follicular Carcinoma

  • Microscopy: Small follicles containing colloid.
  • Diagnosis: Requires evidence of Capsular or Vascular Invasion to distinguish from adenoma. (Cannot be diagnosed by FNAC).
  • Spread: Hematogenous spread to bones/lungs.

5. DIABETES MELLITUS (DM)

A metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycemia due to defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both.

Classification and Etiology

Feature Type 1 DM Type 2 DM
Age Childhood/Adolescence Adults (>40 yrs)
Pathogenesis Autoimmune destruction of Beta-cells. Insulin Resistance + Relative Beta-cell failure.
Insulin Levels Absent/Low Normal or High (early) → Low (late)
Genetics HLA-DR3, DR4 linkage. Polygenic (Strong family history). No HLA link.
Autoantibodies Yes (Anti-GAD, Anti-Insulin). No.
Obesity Not associated. Strongly associated (80%).

Pathogenesis of Complications

Chronic hyperglycemia causes tissue damage via:

  1. Formation of AGEs (Advanced Glycation End-products): Cross-link proteins, trap LDL in vessels, bind RAGE receptors inducing inflammation.
  2. Activation of Protein Kinase C (PKC): Increases VEGF (neovascularization) and TGF-beta (fibrosis).
  3. Polyol Pathway: Accumulation of Sorbitol causes osmotic damage (Lens cataracts, Nerves).

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Pancreas:
    • Type 1: Insulitis (lymphocytic infiltration), Reduction in number of islets.
    • Type 2: Amyloid deposition (Amylin) in islets.
  • Kidney (Diabetic Nephropathy):
    • Glomerular: Capillary basement membrane thickening, Diffuse Mesangial Sclerosis, Kimmelstiel-Wilson (KW) Nodules (Nodular glomerulosclerosis - Pathognomonic).
    • Vascular: Hyaline arteriolosclerosis (affects both afferent & efferent).
    • Tubular: Armanni-Ebstein lesion (Glycogen deposits).
  • Eye: Diabetic Retinopathy (Proliferative/Non-proliferative), Cataract, Glaucoma.

6. HYPERPARATHYROIDISM

Primary Hyperparathyroidism

  • Etiology: Adenoma (85-95%), Hyperplasia (5-10%), Carcinoma (1%).
  • Genetics: MEN1, MEN2A syndromes.
  • Pathogenesis: Autonomous overproduction of PTH → Hypercalcemia.
    • Bone resorption (Osteoclasts).
    • Renal Ca reabsorption & Phosphate excretion.
    • Vitamin D activation (Increased gut absorption).
  • Clinical Features ("Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans"):
    • Bones: Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica (Brown tumors).
    • Stones: Renal calculi (Calcium oxalate/phosphate).
    • Groans: Peptic ulcers, Pancreatitis, Constipation.
    • Moans: Depression, CNS dysfunction.
  • Labs: High Serum Calcium, Low Phosphate, High PTH.

7. PANCREATIC CANCER

Mostly Ductal Adenocarcinoma (Exocrine).

  • Etiology: Smoking (strongest risk), Chronic Pancreatitis, Diabetes, Obesity.
  • Genetics: KRAS mutation (90%), CDKN2A (p16), TP53, SMAD4.
  • Morphology:
    • Site: Head of Pancreas (60%).
    • Gross: Hard, stellate, grey-white, infiltrating mass. Desmoplastic response.
    • Microscopy: Infiltrating glandular structures lined by mucin-secreting atypical cuboidal cells. Perineural invasion is common.
  • Clinical: Painless Jaundice (Head tumors), Courvoisier’s Sign (palpable gallbladder), Weight loss. Trousseau's Sign (Migratory thrombophlebitis).
  • Prognosis: Very poor. Often metastatic at diagnosis (Liver, Lungs).

8. ADRENAL CORTEX DISORDERS

A. Cushing's Syndrome (Hypercortisolism)

  • Etiology:
    1. Exogenous: Steroid therapy (Most common cause).
    2. Endogenous:
      • Pituitary Adenoma (Cushing Disease): High ACTH. (70%).
      • Adrenal Adenoma/Carcinoma: Low ACTH. (20%).
      • Ectopic ACTH (Small cell lung Ca): Very High ACTH. (10%).
  • Manifestations: Moon face, Buffalo hump (Truncal obesity), Purple striae, Hypertension, Osteoporosis, Hyperglycemia.
  • Morphology:
    • Pituitary/Ectopic: Bilateral Diffuse Cortical Hyperplasia.
    • Adrenal Adenoma: Solitary yellow nodule. Contralateral adrenal atrophy.

B. Adrenal Insufficiency (Hypoadrenalism)

  • Acute (Adrenal Crisis): Sudden withdrawal of steroids, Massive hemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen Syndrome in Meningococcemia).
  • Chronic (Addison's Disease): Autoimmune adrenalitis (most common), Tuberculosis, Metastasis.
    • Manifestations: Weakness, Fatigue, Hyperpigmentation (High ACTH/MSH), Hypotension, Hyponatremia, Hyperkalemia.

9. ADRENAL NEOPLASMS

Adrenocortical Adenoma

  • Usually incidental ("Incidentaloma"). Benign.
  • Gross: Small, yellow-orange, well-circumscribed.
  • Microscopy: Lipid-rich clear cells resembling zona fasciculata.

Pheochromocytoma (Adrenal Medulla)

  • Definition: Tumor of chromaffin cells secreting Catecholamines (Adrenaline/Noradrenaline).
  • Rule of 10s: 10% Bilateral, 10% Extra-adrenal (Paraganglioma), 10% Malignant, 10% Familial (MEN2, VHL, NF1).
  • Clinical: Episodic Hypertension, Palpitations, Headache, Sweating.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Grey-tan, hemorrhagic. Reaction with Dichromate turns it brown (Chromaffin reaction).
    • Microscopy: Nests of cells ("Zellballen") separated by vascular stroma.
  • Labs: Urinary VMA and Metanephrines.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Diabetes Mellitus. Describe the renal lesions in Diabetes (Diabetic Nephropathy).

Introduction: DM is a group of metabolic disorders sharing the phenotype of hyperglycemia. Diabetic nephropathy is a leading cause of end-stage renal disease.

Body:
Classification: Type 1 (Autoimmune beta-cell destruction), Type 2 (Insulin resistance), Gestational, Secondary (Pancreatitis, Drugs).
Renal Lesions: The kidneys are prime targets of microangiopathy.

  1. Glomerular Lesions: Capillary basement membrane thickening, Diffuse mesangial sclerosis, and the pathognomonic Kimmelstiel-Wilson Nodules (nodular glomerulosclerosis).
  2. Vascular Lesions: Hyaline arteriolosclerosis of both afferent and efferent arterioles (highly specific).
  3. Tubulointerstitial: Tubular atrophy, fibrosis, and susceptibility to Pyelonephritis (Papillary Necrosis).

Conclusion: Microalbuminuria is the earliest marker of diabetic nephropathy.

Q2: Describe the etiology and pathology of Graves' Disease.

Introduction: Graves' disease is the most common cause of endogenous hyperthyroidism, characterized by the triad of thyrotoxicosis, ophthalmopathy, and dermopathy.

Body:
Etiology/Pathogenesis: An autoimmune disorder caused by Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulin (TSI). This antibody binds to the TSH receptor and mimics TSH action, stimulating thyroid growth and hormone synthesis unregulated by negative feedback.
Pathology:

  • Gross: Symmetrical, diffuse enlargement of the thyroid. It is soft and fleshy ("Beefy").
  • Microscopy: Diffuse hyperplasia. Follicles are lined by tall, crowded columnar epithelium which forms Papillary infoldings into the lumen. Colloid is pale and scalloped at the edges ("Scalloping"). Lymphocytic infiltrates are present in the stroma.

Conclusion: It leads to a hypermetabolic state requiring anti-thyroid drugs or radioiodine therapy.
