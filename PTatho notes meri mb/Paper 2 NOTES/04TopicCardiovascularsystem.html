TOPIC 04: CARDIOVASCULAR SYSTEM

Syllabus Scope: Arteriosclerosis, Aneurysms, Heart Failure, Rheumatic Fever, Ischemic Heart Disease.

1. ARTERIOSCLEROSIS

Definition: Arteriosclerosis literally means "hardening of the arteries". It is a generic term reflecting arterial wall thickening and loss of elasticity.

Distinction: Arteriosclerosis vs. Atherosclerosis

  • Arteriosclerosis: A broad umbrella term for any hardening of arteries. It includes 3 types:
    1. Arteriolosclerosis (Small arteries/arterioles).
    2. Mönckeberg Medial Sclerosis (Muscular arteries).
    3. Atherosclerosis (Large/Medium arteries).
  • Atherosclerosis: A specific type of arteriosclerosis characterized by the formation of Atheromas (fibrofatty plaques) in the intima.

Types and Pathology

1. Mönckeberg Medial Sclerosis

  • Definition: Calcific deposits in muscular arteries (e.g., Radial, Ulnar) in people >50 years.
  • Pathology: Ring-like calcifications in the media of the arteries. They do not encroach on the lumen and are clinically insignificant (incidental finding on X-ray).

2. Arteriolosclerosis

Affects small arteries and arterioles, typically associated with Hypertension and Diabetes. Two morphologic variants:

  • Hyaline Arteriolosclerosis:
    • Pathogenesis: Plasma protein leakage across injured endothelium + increased smooth muscle cell (SMC) matrix synthesis.
    • Microscopy: Homogeneous, pink, hyaline thickening of the wall with luminal narrowing.
    • Association: Benign Hypertension, Diabetes, Aging.
  • Hyperplastic Arteriolosclerosis:
    • Pathogenesis: Severe/Malignant Hypertension.
    • Microscopy: "Onion-skin" appearance due to concentric lamination of smooth muscle cells and thickened basement membrane. Fibrinoid necrosis may be present.

3. Atherosclerosis

  • Definition: Disease of large/medium arteries characterized by intimal lesions called Atheromas (fibrous cap + necrotic lipid core).
  • Pathogenesis (Response to Injury Hypothesis):
    1. Chronic Endothelial Injury: Due to Hyperlipidemia, HTN, Smoking, Homocysteine.
    2. Endothelial Dysfunction: Increased permeability, adhesion of leukocytes.
    3. Macrophage Activation: Monocytes adhere, enter intima, become macrophages, and engulf oxidized LDL to become Foam Cells.
    4. SMC Proliferation: Smooth muscle cells migrate from media to intima, proliferate, and deposit extracellular matrix (Collagen).
  • Morphology:
    • Fatty Streak: Early lesion. Lipid-filled foam cells. Flat, yellow spots.
    • Atheromatous Plaque: Raised white-yellow lesion.
      - Fibrous Cap: SMCs, collagen.
      - Necrotic Core: Lipid, debris, foam cells, calcium.

2. ANEURYSMS

Definition: A localized abnormal dilation of a blood vessel or the heart.

Etiology and Pathogenesis

Aneurysms form when the structure or function of the connective tissue within the vascular wall is compromised.

  • Atherosclerosis: Major cause of Abdominal Aortic Aneurysms (AAA). Plaque compresses media → nutrient/waste diffusion blocked → medial degeneration → weakening.
  • Hypertension: Major cause of Thoracic Aortic Aneurysms. Increases wall stress.
  • Cystic Medial Degeneration: Loss of elastic fibers and SMCs (seen in Marfan syndrome, scurvy).
  • Infections (Mycotic): Embolization of septic thrombi (e.g., Salmonella, Syphilis).
  • Syphilis (Tertiary): Obliterative endarteritis of vasa vasorum → Ischemic destruction of media → "Tree-bark" appearance.

Types and Pathology

  1. True Aneurysm: Involves all 3 layers (Intima, Media, Adventitia).
    • Saccular: Spherical outpouching (5-20 cm).
    • Fusiform: Diffuse, circumferential dilation.
  2. False Aneurysm (Pseudoaneurysm): A breach in the vascular wall leading to an extravascular hematoma (pulsating hematoma) contained by extravascular connective tissue.
  3. Dissecting Aneurysm (Aortic Dissection): Blood enters the wall through an intimal tear and dissects between laminar planes of the media. (Type A: Ascending aorta, Type B: Descending).

Complications

  • Rupture: Massive hemorrhage (Fatal). Risk correlates with size (>5-6 cm).
  • Obstruction: Compression of branch vessels (Ischemia of kidneys/legs).
  • Embolism: From mural thrombus within the aneurysm sac.
  • Compression: Of adjacent structures (ureter, vertebrae).

3. HEART FAILURE (CONGESTIVE CARDIAC FAILURE)

Definition: A clinical syndrome where the heart is unable to pump blood at a rate sufficient to meet the metabolic demands of the tissues, or can do so only at elevated filling pressures.

Etiology

  • Systolic Dysfunction (Pump failure): IHD, Dilated Cardiomyopathy, Valvular regurgitation.
  • Diastolic Dysfunction (Filling failure): LV Hypertrophy, Fibrosis, Amyloidosis, Constrictive Pericarditis.

Pathophysiology (Compensatory Mechanisms)

  1. Frank-Starling Mechanism: Increased end-diastolic volume (dilation) stretches fibers to increase contractility.
  2. Myocardial Hypertrophy:
    • Pressure Overload: Concentric Hypertrophy (Wall thickens, lumen normal/small).
    • Volume Overload: Eccentric Hypertrophy (Wall thickens + Chamber dilation).
  3. Neurohumoral Activation: RAAS activation, Sympathetic nervous system, ANP/BNP release.

Pathology and Clinical Features

Left-Sided Heart Failure

  • Pathology: Backpressure into pulmonary veins.
    • Lungs: Heavy, wet. Pulmonary Edema.
    • Microscopy: Heart Failure Cells (Hemosiderin-laden macrophages in alveoli).
  • Clinical: Dyspnea, Orthopnea, Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND), Cough, Crepitations.

Right-Sided Heart Failure

  • Etiology: Most commonly secondary to Left Heart Failure. Pure right failure = Cor Pulmonale.
  • Pathology: Systemic venous congestion.
    • Liver: Nutmeg Liver (Chronic Passive Congestion). Centrilobular necrosis.
    • Spleen: Congestive Splenomegaly.
    • Subcutaneous Tissue: Peripheral Edema (Anasarca).
  • Clinical: Distended Neck Veins (JVP), Hepatomegaly, Ascites, Pitting Edema.

4. RHEUMATIC FEVER (RF) & RHEUMATIC HEART DISEASE (RHD)

Definition: An acute, immunologically mediated, multisystem inflammatory disease occurring a few weeks after an episode of Group A Streptococcal pharyngitis.

Etiology and Pathogenesis

  • Cause: Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcus (Pharyngitis).
  • Mechanism: Molecular Mimicry (Type II Hypersensitivity). Antibodies against Streptococcal M-protein cross-react with host antigens in the heart (myosin, valves), joints, and brain.

Pathology (Morphology)

  1. Acute Phase (Pancarditis): Inflammation of all three layers.
    • Aschoff Bodies: The pathognomonic lesion. Foci of fibrinoid necrosis surrounded by lymphocytes, plasma cells, and macrophages.
    • Anitschkow Cells: Activated macrophages in Aschoff bodies ("Caterpillar cells" due to chromatin pattern).
    • Endocarditis: Small, warty vegetations (Verrucae) along the lines of closure of valves (Mitral > Aortic).
  2. Chronic Phase (RHD):
    • Organization of inflammation leads to fibrosis.
    • Mitral Stenosis: Leaflet thickening, commissural fusion, shortening of chordae tendineae ("Fish-mouth" or "Buttonhole" stenosis).
    • MacCallum's Patch: Subendocardial thickening in the left atrium.

Diagnosis (Jones Criteria)

Evidence of preceding Strep infection + 2 Major OR 1 Major + 2 Minor criteria.

  • Major: Carditis, Migratory Polyarthritis, Sydenham Chorea, Erythema Marginatum, Subcutaneous Nodules.
  • Minor: Fever, Arthralgia, Elevated ESR/CRP, Prolonged PR interval.

5. ISCHEMIC HEART DISEASE (IHD)

Definition: A group of syndromes resulting from myocardial ischemia, an imbalance between blood supply (perfusion) and myocardial oxygen demand.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

  • Leading cause of death globally.
  • Risk Factors:
    • Major (Modifiable): Dyslipidemia (High LDL, Low HDL), Hypertension, Smoking, Diabetes.
    • Major (Non-modifiable): Age, Gender (Male > Female pre-menopause), Genetics.

Pathophysiology

90% cases are due to Coronary Atherosclerosis.
Acute Coronary Syndromes (ACS): Triggered by Acute Plaque Change (Rupture/Erosion) → Superimposed Thrombosis → Occlusion.

Clinical Presentations

  1. Angina Pectoris: Transient ischemia (no necrosis).
    • Stable: Exertional, relieved by rest (Fixed stenosis).
    • Unstable: At rest, increasing frequency (Plaque disruption). Pre-infarction.
    • Prinzmetal: Vasospasm.
  2. Myocardial Infarction (MI): Irreversible necrosis.
  3. Chronic IHD: Heart failure due to ischemic damage.
  4. Sudden Cardiac Death: Fatal arrhythmia (V-fib).

Morphology of Myocardial Infarction (Timeline)

Evolution of ischemic necrosis.

Time Gross Appearance Microscopic Appearance
0-4 Hours None (Normal). None or Waviness of fibers.
4-12 Hours Dark mottling (occasional). Coagulative Necrosis (loss of nuclei), Edema, Hemorrhage.
12-24 Hours Dark mottling (Red-Blue). Pyknosis of nuclei, Hypereosinophilia, Neutrophilic Infiltration begins.
1-3 Days Mottling with yellow-tan center. Coagulative necrosis (loss of striations), Dense Neutrophilic infiltrate.
3-7 Days Hyperemic border, yellow soft center. Macrophage infiltration (phagocytosis of debris).
7-10 Days Maximally yellow-tan and soft. Granulation Tissue formation at margins.
2-8 Weeks Grey-white scar. Collagen deposition (Fibrosis).
> 2 Months Dense white scar. Dense collagenous scar.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Cardiac Troponins (TnI, TnT): Gold standard. Rise in 3-4 hrs, peak 24-48 hrs, persist 7-10 days.
  • CK-MB: Rises in 4 hrs, peaks 24 hrs, normal by 72 hrs (Useful for Re-infarction).
  • ECG: ST-segment elevation (STEMI), T-wave inversion, Q waves.

Complications of MI

  • Arrhythmias: Most common cause of death in first few hours.
  • Cardiogenic Shock: If >40% LV is infarcted.
  • Myocardial Rupture (Day 3-7): Free wall (Tamponade), Septum (VSD), Papillary muscle (Mitral Regurgitation).
  • Ventricular Aneurysm: Late complication.
  • Mural Thrombus: Risk of embolism.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Rheumatic Fever. Describe the genesis and morphology of Aschoff Bodies.

Introduction: Rheumatic fever is an immune-mediated inflammatory disease following Group A Streptococcal infection. It is a major cause of acquired valvular disease.

Body:
Pathogenesis: It is a Type II Hypersensitivity reaction involving molecular mimicry. Antibodies against Strep M-protein cross-react with cardiac antigens (myosin, laminar).
Aschoff Body (Morphology):

  • It is the pathognomonic granulomatous lesion of RF, found in the myocardium/interstitium.
  • Composition: Central fibrinoid necrosis surrounded by lymphocytes (T-cells), plasma cells, and activated macrophages.
  • Anitschkow Cells: Specialized macrophages with abundant cytoplasm and central round nucleus with chromatin condensed into a wavy ribbon ("Caterpillar cells"). In cross-section, they look like "Owl eyes".
  • Aschoff Giant Cells: Multinucleated giant cells derived from macrophages.

Conclusion: Aschoff bodies eventually heal by fibrosis, contributing to the scarring of RHD.

Q2: Describe the sequential morphological changes in Myocardial Infarction.

Introduction: Myocardial infarction is coagulative necrosis of the heart muscle due to ischemia. The morphology changes predictably over time.

Body:
Gross & Microscopic Evolution:

  • 0-12 Hours: Grossly normal or mild mottling. Micro: Waviness of fibers (1-3 hrs) followed by onset of coagulative necrosis.
  • 12-24 Hours: Grossly dark mottling. Micro: Pyknosis of nuclei, intense eosinophilia, and early neutrophilic infiltrate.
  • 1-3 Days: Grossly yellow-tan center. Micro: Peak neutrophilic infiltration causing disintegration of myofibers.
  • 3-7 Days: Grossly hyperemic border. Micro: Macrophages replace neutrophils to clear debris.
  • 1-2 Weeks: Grossly red-grey margins. Micro: Granulation tissue (new vessels, fibroblasts) fills the defect.
  • > 2 Months: Grossly white scar. Micro: Dense collagenous fibrosis.

Conclusion: Dating an infarct is crucial for medicolegal purposes and correlating with clinical complications (e.g., rupture occurs when the wall is softest at 3-7 days).
