TOPIC 10: BONE AND SOFT TISSUE

Syllabus Scope: Osteomyelitis, Bone Tumors (Osteosarcoma, Ewing's, Giant Cell Tumor), Paget's Disease of Bone.

1. OSTEOMYELITIS

Definition: Inflammation of the bone and marrow, virtually always implying infection.

Classification

  1. Pyogenic Osteomyelitis: Caused by bacteria.
  2. Tuberculous Osteomyelitis: Caused by TB (Pott's spine).
  3. Chronic Sclerosing Osteomyelitis (Garr√©'s): Extensive new bone formation.

Etiology

  • Staphylococcus aureus: Most common cause (80-90%) due to cell wall receptors binding bone matrix.
  • Escherichia coli / Pseudomonas: IV drug abusers, urinary tract infections.
  • Salmonella: Patients with Sickle Cell Disease.
  • Haemophilus influenzae / Group B Strep: Neonates.

Pathogenesis

Routes of infection:

  1. Hematogenous spread: Most common in children. Affects the Metaphysis of long bones (rich vascular supply, sluggish flow).
  2. Direct extension: From adjacent soft tissue infection.
  3. Traumatic implantation: Compound fractures, orthopedic surgery.

Pathology (Morphology & Sequence)

  • Acute Stage (First 48 hrs): Bacteria proliferate → Neutrophilic inflammation → Increased intramedullary pressure → Thrombosis of vessels → Ischemic Necrosis of Bone.
  • Spread: Pus lifts the periosteum (Subperiosteal abscess), disrupting blood supply further.
  • Sequestrum: A piece of dead bone separated from viable bone. It acts as a foreign body preventing healing.
  • Involucrum: A sleeve of new living bone formed by the elevated periosteum that surrounds the sequestrum.
  • Cloaca: Sinus tract in the involucrum allowing pus to drain.

Complications

  • Pathologic fracture.
  • Secondary Amyloidosis (AA type).
  • Sinus tract formation.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma in the sinus tract (Marjolin's ulcer).
  • Septic arthritis (if metaphysis is intracapsular e.g., hip in infants).

Radiologic Features

  • Early (<10 days): Soft tissue swelling, no bony changes.
  • Late: Lytic bone destruction (Sequestrum is dense/white; surrounding bone is osteoporotic) + New bone formation (Involucrum).

2. BONE TUMORS

Classified based on the matrix they produce.

Classification

Cell Origin Benign Malignant
Bone forming Osteoma, Osteoid Osteoma Osteosarcoma
Cartilage forming Osteochondroma, Chondroma Chondrosarcoma
Unknown Giant Cell Tumor (GCT) Ewing Sarcoma

A. Osteosarcoma (Osteogenic Sarcoma)

Most common primary malignant bone tumor (after myeloma/lymphoma).

  • Epidemiology: Bimodal age distribution. 75% in Teenagers (10-20 yrs); second peak in Elderly (Paget's disease, radiation).
  • Site: Metaphysis of long bones (Distal Femur > Proximal Tibia > Proximal Humerus) - "Around the knee".
  • Genetics: Mutations in RB gene (Retinoblastoma) and TP53 (Li-Fraumeni syndrome).
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Large, destructive, gritty, grey-white mass with hemorrhage/cystic degeneration. Invades medullary canal and soft tissue.
    • Microscopy: Malignant mesenchymal cells producing Osteoid Matrix (pink, lace-like material) - Diagnostic feature. Pleomorphic cells, abnormal mitoses.
  • Radiology:
    • Codman's Triangle: Lifting of periosteum by tumor creates a triangular angle.
    • Sunburst Appearance: Spicules of new bone radiating perpendicular to the shaft.
  • Spread: Hematogenous to Lungs (early).

B. Giant Cell Tumor (Osteoclastoma)

  • Epidemiology: Adults (20-40 yrs).
  • Site: Epiphysis of long bones (Distal Femur, Proximal Tibia). Arises only after epiphyseal closure.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: "Soap-bubble" appearance on X-ray. Lytic, eccentric lesion. Brown color due to hemorrhage.
    • Microscopy: Uniform oval mononuclear stromal cells (Neoplastic) + Numerous Osteoclast-like Giant Cells (Reactive).
  • Behavior: "Locally aggressive" benign tumor. High recurrence rate.

C. Ewing Sarcoma

  • Epidemiology: Children (5-15 yrs). Second most common pediatric bone sarcoma.
  • Site: Diaphysis of long bones (Femur, Tibia, Pelvis).
  • Genetics: Translocation t(11;22) forming EWS-FLI1 fusion protein.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Soft, white, necrotic mass arising in medullary cavity.
    • Microscopy: Sheets of Small Round Blue Cells. Homer-Wright Rosettes (cells circling a central fibrillary core). PAS positive (Glycogen).
  • Radiology: "Onion-skin" appearance (layered periosteal reaction).
  • Spread: Early hematogenous spread to lungs/bones.

D. Metastatic Bone Tumors

  • More common than primary tumors.
  • Primary sites: Prostate, Breast, Kidney, Lung, Thyroid ("PB KLT").
  • Types:
    • Osteoblastic (Sclerotic): Prostate cancer.
    • Osteolytic (Lytic): Kidney, Lung, Melanoma.
    • Mixed: Breast.

3. PAGET'S DISEASE OF BONE (OSTEITIS DEFORMANS)

Definition: A chronic disorder of bone remodeling characterized by excessive bone resorption followed by disorganized and excessive bone formation, leading to enlarged, weak, and deformed bones.

Etiology

  • Viral: Paramyxovirus (Measles, RSV) antigens found in osteoclasts.
  • Genetic: Mutations in SQSTM1 gene (enhances NF-κB signaling).

Pathogenesis (3 Phases)

  1. Osteolytic Phase: Frenzied osteoclastic activity. Massive resorption.
  2. Mixed Phase: Osteoblasts start forming new bone while resorption continues.
  3. Osteosclerotic Phase (Burned-out): Osteoblastic activity dominates. Bone is hypervascular and dense but structurally weak.

Morphology

  • Microscopy: The hallmark is the Mosaic Pattern of lamellar bone (Jigsaw puzzle appearance) due to irregular cement lines. Marrow shows fibrosis and increased vascularity.
  • Gross: Bones are thickened, enlarged, and bowed (e.g., Tibia, Femur, Skull).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Pain: Deep bone pain, warmth over bone (hypervascularity).
  • Deformity: Leontiasis ossea (Lion face), Platybasia (skull base flattening), Bowing of legs.
  • Fractures: Chalk-stick fractures.
  • High-output Heart Failure: Due to AV shunting in vascular bone.

Radiologic Features

  • Thickened cortex, coarsened trabeculae, bone enlargement. "Cotton wool" appearance of skull.

Complications

  • Sarcoma: Osteosarcoma occurs in 1% of cases (very poor prognosis).
  • Nerve compression (Deafness due to VIII nerve compression).
  • Heart failure.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Bone Tumors. Describe the pathogenesis and morphology of Osteosarcoma.

Introduction: Bone tumors are classified based on the matrix produced by the tumor cells. Osteosarcoma is the most common primary malignancy of bone.

Body:
Classification: Osteogenic (Osteosarcoma), Chondrogenic (Chondrosarcoma), Ewing Sarcoma, Giant Cell Tumor.
Osteosarcoma:

  • Pathogenesis: Arises from primitive mesenchymal cells. Mutations in RB and p53 genes are common.
  • Morphology: It is a metaphyseal tumor (distal femur/proximal tibia).
    - Gross: Large, destructive, gritty mass penetrating the cortex.
    - Microscopy: Pleomorphic malignant cells producing Osteoid (unmineralized bone matrix).
  • Radiology: Codman's Triangle (periosteal elevation) and Sunburst appearance.

Conclusion: It is aggressive but responds well to neoadjuvant chemotherapy and limb-sparing surgery.

Q2: Discuss the phases and complications of Paget's Disease of Bone.

Introduction: Paget's disease (Osteitis Deformans) is a disorder of uncoupled bone remodeling resulting in weak, woven bone.

Body:
Phases:

  1. Lytic: Dominant osteoclast resorption.
  2. Mixed: Osteoblasts lay down woven bone alongside resorption.
  3. Sclerotic: Dense, mineralized, mosaic bone with little cellular activity.

Morphology: Mosaic pattern of cement lines is pathognomonic.
Complications: Pathologic fractures, nerve compression (deafness), high-output cardiac failure, and malignant transformation to Osteosarcoma.

Conclusion: It is often an incidental radiological finding in the elderly (elevated Alkaline Phosphatase with normal Calcium).
