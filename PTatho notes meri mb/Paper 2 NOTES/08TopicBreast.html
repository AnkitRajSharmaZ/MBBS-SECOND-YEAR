TOPIC 08: BREAST

Syllabus Scope: Benign Breast Disease (Types, Etiology, Hormonal Dependency) and Carcinoma of Breast (Epidemiology, Pathogenesis, Classification, Morphology, Staging, Spread).

1. BENIGN BREAST DISEASES (BBD)

Classification

Benign epithelial lesions are divided into three groups based on the risk of developing subsequent carcinoma:

  1. Non-Proliferative Breast Changes (Fibrocystic Changes) - No increased risk.
  2. Proliferative Breast Disease without Atypia - Mildly increased risk (1.5 - 2 times).
  3. Proliferative Breast Disease with Atypia - Moderately increased risk (4 - 5 times).

Etiology and Hormonal Dependency

  • Estrogen Excess: Most benign changes are driven by an absolute or relative increase in Estrogen and a deficiency of Progesterone.
  • Reproductive Life: Symptoms typically worsen during the premenstrual phase (cyclical pain/mastalgia) and regress after menopause.
  • OCP/HRT: Use of exogenous hormones can exacerbate these conditions.

A. Fibrocystic Change (Non-Proliferative)

Most common disorder of the breast ("Lumpy Bumpy Breast").

Morphology: Three key features:

  • Cysts: Dilation of lobules. Small to large. Contain turbid fluid ("Blue-domed cysts"). Lining epithelium may show Apocrine Metaplasia (large granular eosinophilic cells).
  • Fibrosis: Rupture of cysts releases material into stroma, causing chronic inflammation and fibrous scarring.
  • Adenosis: Increase in the number of acini per lobule.

B. Proliferative Breast Disease (Without Atypia)

  • Epithelial Hyperplasia: Increase in layers of luminal cells (>2 cell layers). Fenestrated spaces are irregular/slit-like.
  • Sclerosing Adenosis: Proliferation of acini which are distorted by central fibrosis. Mimics carcinoma histologically but myoepithelial layer is preserved.
  • Complex Sclerosing Lesion (Radial Scar): Irregular central hyalinized scar with radiating arms of glandular proliferation. Mimics cancer on mammogram (spiculated).
  • Intraductal Papilloma: Branching fibrovascular cores lined by epithelium within a dilated duct. Causes Bloody Nipple Discharge.

C. Proliferative Breast Disease (With Atypia)

  • Atypical Ductal Hyperplasia (ADH): Resembles DCIS but involves only part of the duct.
  • Atypical Lobular Hyperplasia (ALH): Resembles LCIS but fills < 50% of acini.

D. Fibroadenoma (Benign Tumor)

  • Definition: Most common benign tumor of the female breast. Biphasic tumor (Epithelium + Stroma).
  • Hormonal Dependency: Estrogen-dependent. Enlarge during pregnancy, regress after menopause.
  • Gross: Well-circumscribed, encapsulated, firm, grey-white, mobile mass ("Breast Mouse").
  • Microscopy: Loose fibromyxoid stroma compressing ducts.
    • Intracanalicular: Stroma compresses ducts into slit-like clefts.
    • Pericanalicular: Stroma encircles patent ducts.

2. CARCINOMA OF THE BREAST

Definition: Malignant tumor arising from the ductal or lobular epithelium of the breast.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

  • Most common cancer in women worldwide. Rare in men (1%).
  • Age: Risk peaks after menopause (>50 years).
  • Genetic Factors:
    • BRCA1: Associated with Triple Negative cancers.
    • BRCA2: Associated with ER+ cancers and Male breast cancer.
    • TP53 (Li-Fraumeni), HER2 amplification.
  • Hormonal Factors (Estrogen Exposure): Early menarche, Late menopause, Nulliparity, Late age at first child (>30y), Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT).
  • Others: Obesity, Alcohol, Radiation exposure.

Pathogenesis

  • Hereditary (5-10%): Germline mutations in tumor suppressor genes (BRCA1/2).
  • Sporadic (90%): Major driver is Estrogen stimulation leading to accumulation of mutations.
  • Precursor Lesions:
    • Ductal Carcinoma In Situ (DCIS) → Invasive Ductal Carcinoma.
    • Lobular Carcinoma In Situ (LCIS) → Invasive Lobular Carcinoma.

Classification (WHO)

  1. Non-Invasive (In Situ):
    • Ductal Carcinoma In Situ (DCIS).
    • Lobular Carcinoma In Situ (LCIS).
  2. Invasive (Infiltrating):
    • Invasive Carcinoma of No Special Type (NST) - formerly Ductal (70-80%).
    • Invasive Lobular Carcinoma (10-15%).
    • Special Types: Tubular, Mucinous (Colloid), Medullary, Papillary.
  3. Molecular Classification:
    • Luminal A: ER+, HER2-, Low Ki67 (Best prognosis).
    • Luminal B: ER+, HER2+/-, High Ki67.
    • HER2-Enriched: ER-, HER2+ (Aggressive).
    • Basal-like (Triple Negative): ER-, PR-, HER2- (Worst prognosis, BRCA1 associated).

Morphology

1. Invasive Carcinoma NST (Ductal)

  • Gross: Hard, gritty, grey-white mass with irregular stellate borders. Retracts cut surface.
  • Microscopy: Nests, cords, and tubules of malignant cells invading stroma.
    Desmoplasia: Dense fibrous stromal reaction (makes tumor hard/scirrhous).

2. Invasive Lobular Carcinoma

  • Gross: Ill-defined, rubbery thickening. Often bilateral/multicentric.
  • Microscopy: Discohesive small cells due to loss of E-cadherin.
    Indian File Pattern: Single distinct rows of cells.
    Targetoid Pattern: Cells arranged concentrically around normal ducts.

3. Medullary Carcinoma

  • Soft, fleshy mass. Syncytial sheets of large pleomorphic cells with a prominent Lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate. BRCA1 associated.

4. Paget Disease of Nipple

  • Extension of DCIS along lactiferous ducts into nipple skin. Eczematous, crusted nipple. Paget Cells (large clear cells) seen in epidermis.

Spread

  1. Direct: Skin (Peau d'orange, ulceration), Pectoral muscle (fixation), Chest wall.
  2. Lymphatic: Axillary nodes (most common), Internal mammary nodes.
  3. Hematogenous: Lungs, Bone, Liver, Brain.

Staging (TNM System)

  • T: Tumor size (<2cm, 2-5cm, >5cm).
  • N: Lymph node status (Fixed/Mobile).
  • M: Metastasis.

Prognostic Factors

Major Factors Minor Factors
Invasive vs In-situ (Invasion = Risk). Histologic Type (Tubular/Mucinous = Better).
Distant Metastasis (Worst sign). Grade (Nottingham Score).
Lymph Node Metastasis (Most important for prognosis in absence of distant mets). ER/PR Status: ER+ respond to Tamoxifen (Better).
Tumor Size. HER2 Status: HER2+ is aggressive but responds to Trastuzumab.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Benign Breast Diseases. Describe the morphology of Fibroadenoma.

Introduction: Benign breast diseases are non-cancerous disorders classified into Non-proliferative (Fibrocystic), Proliferative without atypia, and Proliferative with atypia. The most common benign tumor is Fibroadenoma.

Body:
Fibroadenoma:

  • Etiology: Estrogen-dependent. Common in young women (20-30 yrs).
  • Gross: Solitary, well-circumscribed, firm, mobile, grey-white rubbery mass.
  • Microscopy: Biphasic tumor with proliferation of both stroma and epithelium.
    1. Intracanalicular: Stroma compresses ducts into slits.
    2. Pericanalicular: Stroma surrounds patent round ducts.

Conclusion: Fibroadenoma carries no significant risk of malignancy unless complex features are present.

Q2: Classify Carcinoma of the Breast. Discuss the prognostic factors.

Introduction: Breast carcinoma is a heterogeneous disease classified by WHO into In-situ and Invasive types, and further by molecular subtypes.

Body:
Classification:

  • Non-invasive: DCIS, LCIS.
  • Invasive: Invasive Carcinoma NST (Ductal), Lobular, Tubular, Mucinous.

Prognostic Factors:
The most important factor is Stage (specifically Axillary Lymph Node status).
  • Tumor Size: Larger tumors have worse prognosis.
  • Grade: High grade (Poorly differentiated) is worse.
  • Receptors: ER+/PR+ tumors have a better prognosis (respond to hormonal therapy). HER2+ tumors are aggressive but treatable. Triple-negative tumors have the worst prognosis.

Conclusion: Accurate staging and molecular profiling are essential for personalized therapy.
