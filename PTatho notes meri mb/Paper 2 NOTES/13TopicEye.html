TOPIC 13: RETINOBLASTOMA

Syllabus Scope: Etiology, Genetics, Pathogenesis, Pathology, Presentation, Sequelae, and Complications.

1. INTRODUCTION

Definition: Retinoblastoma is the most common primary intraocular malignant tumor in children, arising from the retinal germ cells (neuroepithelial origin).
Incidence: 1 in 20,000 live births. 90% cases occur before 5 years of age.

2. ETIOLOGY AND GENETICS

Retinoblastoma is the prototype of hereditary cancers caused by a mutation in a Tumor Suppressor Gene.

The RB1 Gene

  • Location: Chromosome 13q14.
  • Function: The RB1 gene encodes the pRb protein, which is a negative regulator of the cell cycle.
    • Active pRb (Hypophosphorylated): Binds to E2F transcription factor → Blocks cell cycle at G1-S checkpoint (Prevents division).
    • Inactive pRb (Hyperphosphorylated): Releases E2F → Cell enters S phase (Division proceeds).
    • Mutation: Loss of function → Uncontrolled cell proliferation.

Knudson's "Two-Hit" Hypothesis

Alfred Knudson proposed that two mutational events ("hits") are required to inactivate both alleles of the RB1 gene.

  1. Hereditary / Familial Retinoblastoma (40%):
    • First Hit: Germline mutation (inherited from parent or de novo in germ cell). Present in all cells of the body.
    • Second Hit: Somatic mutation in a single retinal cell.
    • Features: Bilateral, Multifocal, Younger age (approx. 1 year), Family history may be present.
  2. Sporadic Retinoblastoma (60%):
    • First Hit: Somatic mutation in one retinal cell.
    • Second Hit: Somatic mutation in the same retinal cell.
    • Features: Unilateral, Unifocal, Older age (approx. 2 years), No family history.

3. PATHOGENESIS

  • Origin: Neuronal progenitor cells of the retina.
  • Loss of both RB1 alleles leads to deregulation of the cell cycle (E2F release) and genomic instability.
  • Further mutations (e.g., MYCN amplification) drive tumor progression.

4. PATHOLOGY (MORPHOLOGY)

Gross Appearance

  • Creamy-white or yellowish nodular mass arising from the retina.
  • Growth Patterns:
    • Endophytic: Grows inward into the vitreous humor. May seed the vitreous (floating clumps).
    • Exophytic: Grows outward into the subretinal space. Causes retinal detachment.
  • Advanced cases show chalky white calcification (dystrophic).

Microscopic Features

  • Cells: Sheets of Small Round Blue Cells (undifferentiated) with hyperchromatic nuclei and scant cytoplasm. High mitotic activity.
  • Rosettes (Diagnostic Features):
    • Flexner-Wintersteiner Rosettes: Specific for Retinoblastoma. Columnar cells arranged around a central lumen. Nuclei are basal.
    • Homer-Wright Rosettes: Cells arranged around a central core of pink neurofibrillary material (neuropil). No lumen. (Also seen in Neuroblastoma).
  • Fleurettes: Flower-like arrangement of cells showing photoreceptor differentiation (better prognosis).
  • Necrosis and Calcification: Extensive areas of necrosis with dystrophic calcification are characteristic.

5. CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Common signs in children:

  1. Leukocoria (White Pupillary Reflex): The most common sign (60%). Also called "Cat's eye reflex". Seen when light shines on the pupil.
  2. Strabismus (Squint): Due to loss of central vision (20%).
  3. Red, painful eye: Secondary to glaucoma or inflammation.
  4. Poor vision.

6. SPREAD AND SEQUELAE

Routes of Spread

  1. Direct Extension:
    • Anteriorly into the anterior chamber (Hypopyon).
    • Posteriorly along the Optic Nerve to the Brain (most critical route).
    • Through the sclera into the orbit (Proptosis).
  2. Hematogenous Metastasis: To Bones (Skull, long bones), Liver, Lungs, Lymph nodes.
  3. CSF Seeding: Via optic nerve sheath to subarachnoid space.

Sequelae and Complications

  • Visual Loss: Blindness in the affected eye.
  • Secondary Glaucoma: Tumor blocks outflow or causes neovascularization.
  • Phthisis Bulbi: Atrophy and shrinkage of the eye in advanced necrosis.
  • Trilateral Retinoblastoma: Bilateral Retinoblastoma + Pinealoblastoma (midline intracranial tumor). Very poor prognosis.
  • Second Malignancies: Survivors of hereditary retinoblastoma have a markedly increased risk of developing other cancers later in life, especially Osteosarcoma and Soft Tissue Sarcomas.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Discuss the Etiopathogenesis and Genetics of Retinoblastoma.

Introduction: Retinoblastoma is a malignant embryonic tumor of the retina, serving as the paradigm for hereditary cancers.

Body:
Genetics (RB1 Gene):

  • The RB1 gene is a tumor suppressor located on 13q14.
  • It encodes the pRb protein, which regulates the G1-S cell cycle checkpoint.
  • Loss of both alleles leads to unchecked proliferation.

Knudson's Two-Hit Hypothesis:
  • Familial Cases: 1st hit is germline (inherited), 2nd hit is somatic. Result: Multiple tumors, bilateral, early onset.
  • Sporadic Cases: Both hits are somatic mutations in the same cell. Result: Single tumor, unilateral, later onset.

Conclusion: Genetic counseling is crucial for bilateral cases due to the risk of transmission and second malignancies.

Q2: Describe the Gross and Microscopic features of Retinoblastoma.

Introduction: Retinoblastoma presents as a soft, white intraocular mass.

Body:
Gross:

  • Creamy white mass arising from the retina.
  • Can be Endophytic (growing into vitreous) or Exophytic (growing under retina).
  • Chalky calcification is common.

Microscopy:
  • Sheets of Small Round Blue Cells with high N:C ratio and mitoses.
  • Flexner-Wintersteiner Rosettes: Diagnostic. Columnar cells around a true central lumen.
  • Homer-Wright Rosettes: Cells around a fibrillary core.
  • Areas of necrosis and dystrophic calcification.

Conclusion: The presence of rosettes indicates neuroepithelial differentiation. Invasion of the optic nerve worsens the prognosis.
