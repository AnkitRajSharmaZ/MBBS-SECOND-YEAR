TOPIC 11: SKIN PATHOLOGY

Syllabus Scope: Squamous Cell Carcinoma, Basal Cell Carcinoma, and Malignant Melanoma (Pathogenesis, Morphology, and Natural History).

1. SQUAMOUS CELL CARCINOMA (SCC) OF SKIN

Definition: A malignant tumor arising from the keratinizing stratified squamous epithelium of the epidermis.

Risk Factors

  • Sun Exposure: Chronic exposure to UV radiation (UVB is most carcinogenic) is the #1 cause.
  • Chemical Carcinogens: Arsenic (in drinking water/pesticides), Tars, Oils, Soot.
  • Ionizing Radiation: X-rays, Gamma rays.
  • Precursor Lesions: Actinic Keratosis (Solar keratosis), Bowen's Disease (SCC in situ).
  • Chronic Inflammation: Chronic ulcers (Marjolin's ulcer in burn scars), Sinus tracts (Osteomyelitis).
  • Immunosuppression: Organ transplant recipients, Xeroderma Pigmentosum (DNA repair defect).

Pathogenesis

  • UV Radiation: Causes DNA damage (formation of pyrimidine dimers). If repair fails (e.g., XP), mutations accumulate.
  • Genetic Mutations:
    • TP53 mutations: An early event (often seen in actinic keratosis).
    • HRAS activating mutations.
    • Loss of Notch receptor signaling (which normally promotes differentiation).

Pathology (Morphology)

Gross Features:

  • Usually on sun-exposed sites (Face, Ears, Hands).
  • Starts as a scaling, indurated (hard) plaque or nodule.
  • Progresses to an Ulcerative lesion with raised, everted edges or a Fungating mass.

Microscopic Features:

  • Invasive nests and cords of malignant squamous epithelial cells extending into the dermis.
  • Keratin Pearls: Concentric layers of keratinization (Hallmark of well-differentiated SCC).
  • Intercellular Bridges: Prickles visible between cells.
  • Atypia: Pleomorphism, hyperchromasia, abnormal mitoses.

Natural History

  • Generally slow-growing.
  • Metastasis: Less common than in mucosal SCC (e.g., oral cancer). Occurs late to regional lymph nodes (< 5% risk in sun-induced SCC).
  • Note: SCC arising in burn scars/ulcers is more aggressive.

2. BASAL CELL CARCINOMA (BCC)

Definition: The most common invasive cancer in humans. It is a locally aggressive but rarely metastasizing tumor arising from the basal layer of the epidermis or hair follicles.

Risk Factors

  • Sun Exposure: Chronic UV exposure (cumulative).
  • Skin Type: Fair-skinned individuals (Types I and II) who burn easily.
  • Immune suppression.
  • Genetic Syndromes: Nevoid Basal Cell Carcinoma Syndrome (Gorlin Syndrome).

Pathogenesis

Almost all BCCs have dysregulation of the Hedgehog Signaling Pathway.

  • PTCH Gene: A tumor suppressor gene on Chromosome 9q22.
  • Normal PTCH inhibits the SMO (Smoothened) protein.
  • Mutation: Loss of PTCH function (or activating mutation of SMO) leads to constitutive activation of the Hedgehog pathway, driving cell proliferation.

Pathology (Morphology)

Gross Features:

  • Commonly on face (above line joining angle of mouth and ear lobule).
  • Nodulo-ulcerative type: Pearly, translucent papule/nodule with Telangiectasia (dilated vessels) on surface.
  • Rodent Ulcer: Central ulceration with rolled, beaded, pearly margins. It "gnaws" into underlying tissue.

Microscopic Features:

  • Nests of uniform basaloid cells (resembling stratum basale) invading the dermis.
  • Peripheral Palisading: The cells at the periphery of the nests are arranged parallel to each other (like a picket fence).
  • Retraction Clefting: Separation artifact between the tumor nest and stroma.
  • Stroma is mucinous.

Natural History

  • Slow growing.
  • Locally Invasive: Can destroy underlying cartilage, bone, and soft tissue ("Rodent ulcer").
  • Metastasis: Extremely rare.

3. DISTINGUISHING FEATURES: NEVUS VS. MELANOMA

Use the ABCDE Rule for clinical differentiation.

Feature Benign Nevus (Mole) Malignant Melanoma
A - Asymmetry Symmetrical. Asymmetrical.
B - Borders Regular, smooth, defined. Irregular, notched, scalloped.
C - Color Uniform (tan or brown). Variegated (black, brown, red, blue, white).
D - Diameter Usually < 6 mm. Usually > 6 mm.
E - Evolution Stable over time. Rapid change in size, shape, color, or symptoms (itching/bleeding).
Microscopy Maturation (cells get smaller/deeper). No atypia. No maturation (deep cells large). Cytologic atypia, Mitoses, upward spread (Pagetoid spread).

4. MALIGNANT MELANOMA

Definition: A malignant tumor arising from melanocytes. It is the most deadly form of skin cancer due to its high metastatic potential.

Etiology and Risk Factors

  • Sunlight: Intense intermittent exposure (Sunburns) is more damaging than chronic exposure. UVB is the main culprit.
  • Pre-existing Lesions: Dysplastic Nevi (Multiple atypical moles).
  • Family History: Familial Melanoma.
  • Pigmentation: Light skin, blue eyes, blonde/red hair (Pheomelanin), inability to tan.
  • Xeroderma Pigmentosum.

Pathogenesis (Genetics)

  1. BRAF Mutations (40-50%): Activating mutation in BRAF V600E kinase (part of RAS/RAF/MEK pathway) drives proliferation.
  2. CDKN2A Mutation: Loss of tumor suppressors p16/INK4a (cell cycle control). Common in familial cases.
  3. KIT Mutations: Seen in mucosal and acral melanomas.
  4. TERT Promoter Mutations: Maintains telomerase (immortality).

Morphology (Growth Phases)

Melanoma progression typically shows two phases:

1. Radial Growth Phase (Horizontal):

  • Initial phase. Tumor cells spread laterally within the epidermis and superficial dermis.
  • Lacks capacity to metastasize.
  • Types showing this: Superficial Spreading Melanoma, Lentigo Maligna.

2. Vertical Growth Phase (Invasive):

  • Tumor cells invade downward into the deep dermis.
  • Appearance of a nodule.
  • Metastatic potential emerges.
  • Types showing this: Nodular Melanoma (starts directly in vertical phase).

Microscopy: Large, pleomorphic cells with prominent eosinophilic nucleoli ("Cherry red nucleoli"). Pigment (melanin) may or may not be present (Amelanotic melanoma). HMB-45 and S-100 positive.

Clinical Features

  • Change in a mole (ABCDE rule).
  • Itching or pain (early sign).
  • Ulceration or bleeding (late sign).
  • Subtypes:
    • Superficial Spreading (70%): Flat, irregular plaque. Sun-exposed skin.
    • Nodular (15%): Dome-shaped blue/black nodule. Rapid growth. Aggressive.
    • Lentigo Maligna: Face of elderly ("Hutchinson's freckle"). Slow growing.
    • Acral Lentiginous: Palms, soles, subungual. Most common type in dark-skinned individuals. Not UV-related.

Metastases and Prognosis

  • Prognostic Factor: The most important factor is the Depth of Invasion (Breslow Thickness).
    • < 1 mm: Good prognosis.
    • > 4 mm: Poor prognosis.
  • Spread:
    • Lymphatic: Regional lymph nodes (Sentinel node biopsy is crucial).
    • Hematogenous: Liver, Lungs, Brain (Melanoma loves the brain), Heart.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Skin Cancers. Describe the Pathogenesis and Morphology of Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC).

Introduction: Skin cancers are broadly divided into Melanoma and Non-Melanoma Skin Cancers (NMSC). NMSCs include Basal Cell Carcinoma (most common) and Squamous Cell Carcinoma.

Body:
Pathogenesis of BCC: It is driven by the dysregulation of the Hedgehog Pathway. Mutations in the PTCH1 tumor suppressor gene (often UV-induced) prevent it from inhibiting SMO, leading to uncontrolled cell growth. This is also seen in Gorlin Syndrome.
Morphology:

  • Gross: A pearly, translucent nodule with telangiectasia. May ulcerate centrally ("Rodent Ulcer").
  • Microscopy: Nests of basaloid cells originating from the epidermis. The peripheral cells show Palisading (parallel alignment). Retraction artifacts (clefts) are seen between tumor nests and stroma.

Conclusion: BCC is locally invasive ("Rodent ulcer") but virtually never metastasizes.

Q2: Differentiate between Benign Nevus and Melanoma. Discuss the Prognostic Factors of Melanoma.

Introduction: Distinguishing a benign mole from melanoma is critical as melanoma is highly lethal.

Body:
Differences: (Use ABCDE rule).

  • Nevus: Symmetrical, Regular borders, Uniform color, Diameter <6mm. Micro: Maturation present.
  • Melanoma: Asymmetrical, Irregular borders, Variegated color, Diameter >6mm. Micro: No maturation, atypical mitoses, pagetoid spread.

Prognostic Factors:
  1. Breslow Thickness: Vertical depth of tumor (Most important). Thinner is better.
  2. Ulceration: Presence indicates worse prognosis.
  3. Mitotic Rate: High rate is worse.
  4. Lymph Node Status: Sentinel node positivity decreases survival.

Conclusion: Early detection while the tumor is in the radial growth phase is the key to survival.
