TOPIC 03: RESPIRATORY SYSTEM

1. PNEUMONIA

Definition: Pneumonia is defined as the inflammation of the lung parenchyma (alveoli and interstitium), usually caused by an infectious agent.

Etiology

  • Bacterial: Streptococcus pneumoniae (Most common), Haemophilus influenzae, Staphylococcus aureus, Klebsiella pneumoniae.
  • Viral: Influenza, RSV, Adenovirus, SARS-CoV-2.
  • Atypical: Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia.
  • Fungal/Parasitic: In immunocompromised hosts.

Classification (Anatomical)

  1. Lobar Pneumonia: Consolidation of an entire lobe (or large part). Usually S. pneumoniae.
  2. Bronchopneumonia (Lobular): Patchy consolidation centering around bronchioles. Often bilateral and basal. Staph, H. flu, Klebsiella.
  3. Interstitial Pneumonia: Inflammation of alveolar septa. Viral/Atypical agents.

Pathogenesis & Stages of Lobar Pneumonia

Classically described in 4 stages:

  1. Stage of Congestion (Day 1-2):
    • Gross: Heavy, boggy, red lung.
    • Microscopy: Vascular engorgement, intra-alveolar fluid with few neutrophils and many bacteria.
  2. Stage of Red Hepatization (Day 3-4):
    • Gross: Lung is solid, red, and liver-like ("Hepatization").
    • Microscopy: Alveoli packed with Neutrophils, RBCs, and Fibrin.
  3. Stage of Grey Hepatization (Day 5-7):
    • Gross: Lung remains solid but turns grey-brown.
    • Microscopy: RBCs disintegrate. Fibrino-purulent exudate persists. Macrophages appear.
  4. Stage of Resolution (Day 8+):
    • Gross: Normal architecture restored.
    • Microscopy: Enzymatic digestion of exudate, resorption by macrophages, or expectoration.

Complications

  • Lung Abscess: Tissue necrosis and cavitation.
  • Empyema: Pus in the pleural cavity.
  • Organization: Fibrosis causing solid lung tissue (Carnification).
  • Bacteremic Dissemination: Meningitis, Arthritis, Endocarditis.

2. LUNG ABSCESS

Definition: A localized area of suppurative necrosis within the pulmonary parenchyma, resulting in the formation of one or more large cavities (>2 cm).

Etiology

  • Aspiration: Infective material from carious teeth/tonsils (Anaerobes: Bacteroides, Fusobacterium). Common in alcoholics/coma.
  • Post-Pneumonic: S. aureus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas.
  • Septic Embolism: From infective endocarditis or thrombophlebitis.
  • Neoplasms: Bronchial obstruction by cancer (10-15% cases).

Morphology

  • Gross: Cavity varying in size (few mm to 6 cm).
    • Aspiration: Usually right lung (vertical bronchus), single.
    • Post-pneumonic/Septic: Multiple, basal.
    • Cavity contains pus and air (Air-fluid level on X-ray).
  • Microscopy: Suppurative destruction of lung parenchyma with central liquefactive necrosis. Chronic cases show a fibrous wall.

Complications

  • Rupture into pleura (Empyema, Pneumothorax).
  • Hemorrhage (Hemoptysis).
  • Brain Abscess (Metastatic).
  • Secondary Amyloidosis (in chronic cases).

3. OBSTRUCTIVE AIRWAY DISEASE (COPD) & BRONCHIECTASIS

Definition: Diseases characterized by an increase in resistance to airflow due to partial or complete obstruction at any level.

A. Chronic Bronchitis

  • Definition: Persistent productive cough for at least 3 consecutive months in at least 2 consecutive years.
  • Pathogenesis: Inhalation of irritants (Tobacco smoke, dust) → Hypertrophy of mucus glands + Goblet cell metaplasia → Mucus hypersecretion → Airway obstruction.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Hyperemic, edematous mucosa with mucopurulent secretions.
    • Microscopy: Enlargement of mucus-secreting glands. Reid Index > 0.4 (Ratio of gland layer thickness to bronchial wall thickness). Squamous metaplasia.
  • Clinical: "Blue Bloaters" (Cyanosis, edema, hypercapnia).

B. Emphysema

  • Definition: Abnormal permanent enlargement of airspaces distal to the terminal bronchiole, accompanied by destruction of their walls without obvious fibrosis.
  • Pathogenesis: Protease-Antiprotease Imbalance.
    • Smoking recruits neutrophils → Release Elastase.
    • Smoke inhibits α1-Antitrypsin (the anti-elastase).
    • Result: Destruction of alveolar elastic tissue.
  • Types:
    • Centriacinar (Centrilobular): Affects respiratory bronchioles (proximal). Upper lobes. Common in Smokers.
    • Panacinar (Panlobular): Affects entire acinus. Lower lobes. Associated with α1-Antitrypsin deficiency.
    • Paraseptal (Distal Acinar): Subpleural bullae. Cause of spontaneous pneumothorax.
  • Clinical: "Pink Puffers" (Dyspnea, barrel chest, thin).

C. Bronchiectasis

  • Definition: Permanent dilation of bronchi and bronchioles caused by destruction of muscle and elastic tissue resulting from or associated with chronic necrotizing infections.
  • Etiology:
    • Obstruction: Tumor, foreign body.
    • Congenital: Cystic Fibrosis, Kartagener Syndrome (Immotile cilia).
    • Infection: Necrotizing pneumonia (TB, Staph).
  • Pathogenesis: Obstruction → Atelectasis → Retention of secretions → Infection → Wall destruction → Dilation.
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Lower lobes. Airways are dilated (up to 4x normal) and cylindrical/saccular. Can be traced to pleural surface.
    • Microscopy: Ulceration of mucosa, intense acute and chronic inflammation, fibrosis.
  • Clinical: Cough with copious foul-smelling sputum, hemoptysis, clubbing.

4. TUBERCULOSIS (TB)

Etiology: Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Acid-fast bacilli.

A. Primary Tuberculosis

  • Definition: Infection in a previously unexposed host.
  • Pathology (Ghon Complex):
    1. Ghon Focus: Subpleural caseous granuloma (usually lower part of upper lobe or upper part of lower lobe).
    2. Lymph Node: Caseation of draining hilar lymph nodes.
  • Fate: 95% heal by fibrosis and calcification (Ranke complex). 5% progress (Progressive Primary TB).

B. Secondary Tuberculosis (Reactivation)

  • Definition: Infection in a previously sensitized host (Reactivation or Reinfection).
  • Site: Apical lobes (high oxygen tension favor bacilli).
  • Morphology:
    • Gross: Central caseation, cavitation (fibrocaseous TB).
    • Microscopy: Caseating Granulomas (Epithelioid cells, Langhans giant cells, Lymphocytes, Central necrosis).
  • Complications:
    • Miliary TB: Hematogenous spread to liver, spleen, kidneys (millet seed-like nodules).
    • Cavitary TB: Erosion into bronchus (highly infectious).
    • Amyloidosis.

5. OCCUPATIONAL LUNG DISEASES (PNEUMOCONIOSIS)

Lung reaction to inhalation of mineral dusts, organic dusts, fumes, and vapors.

A. Silicosis

  • Exposure: Sandblasting, mining, stone cutting, glass manufacturing.
  • Pathogenesis: Inhaled silica particles are phagocytosed by macrophages → Macrophages die/activate → Release fibrogenic cytokines (TNF, IL-1) → Collagen deposition.
  • Morphology:
    • Silicotic Nodules: Concentric, whorled hyalinized collagen fibers.
    • Upper zones of lungs.
    • "Eggshell calcification" of hilar lymph nodes.
  • Complications: Increased susceptibility to Tuberculosis (Silico-tuberculosis), Caplan Syndrome (Rheumatoid arthritis + nodules).

B. Asbestosis

  • Exposure: Insulation, shipbuilding, construction.
  • Pathogenesis: Direct toxicity + macrophage activation.
  • Morphology:
    • Diffuse interstitial fibrosis (lower lobes).
    • Asbestos Bodies (Ferruginous Bodies): Golden-brown, beaded rods with a translucent center (iron-coated fibers).
    • Pleural Plaques: Collagenous plaques on parietal pleura (most common manifestation).
  • Complications: Bronchogenic Carcinoma (most common malignancy), Mesothelioma (most specific malignancy).

C. Coal Worker's Pneumoconiosis (CWP)

  • Simple CWP: Coal macules and nodules.
  • Complicated CWP: Progressive Massive Fibrosis (PMF) - Blackened scars > 2cm.

6. TUMORS OF THE LUNG AND PLEURA

A. Bronchogenic Carcinoma

Most common cancer mortality worldwide.
Etiology: Smoking (85-90% cases), Radon, Asbestos, Air pollution.
Genetics: p53 (Squamous/Small cell), EGFR/ALK/KRAS (Adenocarcinoma).

WHO Classification (2021)

  1. Adenocarcinoma (Most common): Glandular differentiation. Peripheral location. Common in non-smokers/females. Markers: TTF-1.
  2. Squamous Cell Carcinoma: Keratinization, Intercellular bridges. Central location. Cavitation common. Smoking-associated. Markers: p40, p63.
  3. Small Cell Carcinoma: Neuroendocrine. Central. Small blue cells, salt-and-pepper chromatin, necrosis. Paraneoplastic syndromes (ACTH, ADH). Worst prognosis.
  4. Large Cell Carcinoma: Undifferentiated. Diagnosis of exclusion.

Metastasis:
• Lymphatic: Hilar, mediastinal nodes.
• Hematogenous: Adrenals (>50%), Liver, Brain, Bone.

Complications: Pancoast tumor (Horner syndrome), SVC syndrome, Paraneoplastic syndromes (Cushing, SIADH, Hypercalcemia).

B. Pleural Tumors: Malignant Mesothelioma

  • Etiology: Strong association with Asbestos exposure (latent period 25-40 years).
  • Morphology: Diffuse lesion spreading widely in the pleural space, encasing the lung ("rind").
    Microscopy: Biphasic pattern (Epithelioid and Sarcomatoid cells).

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Define Pneumonia. Describe the Stages of Lobar Pneumonia.

Introduction: Pneumonia is the inflammation of the lung parenchyma. Lobar pneumonia affects a large portion of a lobe, typically caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae.

Body:
Stages:

  1. Congestion (1-2 days): Vascular engorgement, fluid exudate, bacteria present. Lung is heavy/red.
  2. Red Hepatization (3-4 days): Massive confluent exudation of neutrophils, RBCs, and fibrin. Lung is solid, liver-like, and red.
  3. Grey Hepatization (5-7 days): RBCs disintegrate, fibrin persists. Lung is grey-brown and solid.
  4. Resolution (8+ days): Enzymatic digestion of exudate, resorption, and restoration of architecture.

Conclusion: With antibiotics, these classical stages are rarely seen in full sequence today.

Q2: Classify Bronchogenic Carcinoma. Discuss the pathogenesis and morphology of Adenocarcinoma.

Introduction: Bronchogenic carcinoma is the leading cause of cancer death. It is classified into Small Cell and Non-Small Cell types.

Body:
Classification: Adenocarcinoma, Squamous Cell Carcinoma, Small Cell Carcinoma, Large Cell Carcinoma.
Adenocarcinoma:

  • Pathogenesis: Associated with driver mutations in EGFR, ALK, ROS1 (targetable) or KRAS (smoking). Often arises from precursor Atypical Adenomatous Hyperplasia (AAH).
  • Morphology: Peripherally located. Forms glands or produces mucin. Growth patterns include Acinar, Papillary, and Lepidic (growth along alveolar septa, formerly Bronchioloalveolar Ca). Immunohistochemistry shows TTF-1 positivity.

Conclusion: Adenocarcinoma is the most common subtype in non-smokers and has specific targeted therapy options.
