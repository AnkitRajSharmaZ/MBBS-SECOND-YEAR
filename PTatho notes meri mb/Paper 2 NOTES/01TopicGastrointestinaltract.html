1. ORAL CANCER (SQUAMOUS CELL CARCINOMA)

Definition: Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) is the most common malignant tumor of the oral cavity (95% of cases), arising from the stratified squamous epithelium.

Etiology and Risk Factors

  • Tobacco: Major risk factor. [cite_start]Smoking (cigarettes, beedis) and smokeless forms (paan/betel quid with areca nut, lime, tobacco)[cite: 23539].
  • Alcohol: Acts synergistically with tobacco to increase risk significantly.
  • Human Papillomavirus (HPV): High-risk types (HPV-16, 18) are implicated, especially in oropharyngeal cancers (tonsil, base of tongue).
  • Precancerous Lesions:
    • Leukoplakia: White patch that cannot be scraped off. [cite_start]5-25% premalignant potential[cite: 23539].
    • Erythroplakia: Red, velvety patch. [cite_start]Higher risk of malignancy (>50%)[cite: 23539].
  • Diet: Deficiency of Vitamins A, C, E and Iron (Plummer-Vinson syndrome).
  • Chronic Irritation: Ill-fitting dentures, jagged teeth.

Pathogenesis

It is a multistep process driven by the accumulation of genetic mutations (e.g., p53, p16, Cyclin D1). Chronic carcinogen exposure leads to "Field Cancerization", where large areas of mucosa are genetically altered, increasing the risk of multiple primary tumors.

Sequence: Normal → Hyperplasia → Dysplasia → Carcinoma in situ → Invasive SCC.

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Sites: Tongue (ventral/lateral), Floor of mouth, Lip, Gingiva.
  • Gross:
    • Ulcerative: Malignant ulcer with raised, everted/rolled edges and necrotic base.
    • Exophytic: Fungating, cauliflower-like mass.
    • Verrucous: Slow-growing warty variant.
      • [cite_start]
    • Infiltrative: Deeply indurated plaque[cite: 23541].
  • Microscopy:
    • Invasion of underlying connective tissue by nests and cords of malignant squamous cells.
    • Keratin Pearls: Concentric layers of keratin (hallmark of well-differentiated SCC).
    • Intercellular Bridges: Prickles connecting cells.
    • Cytologic Atypia: Pleomorphism, hyperchromasia, abnormal mitoses.

2. PEPTIC ULCER DISEASE (PUD)

Definition: A chronic mucosal ulceration affecting the GIT (usually duodenum or stomach) caused by the aggressive action of acid-peptic juices.

Etiology

    [cite_start]
  • H. pylori Infection: Most common cause (90% of duodenal, 70% of gastric ulcers)[cite: 21832].
  • NSAIDs (Aspirin): Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis (COX-1), reducing mucosal protection.
  • Lifestyle: Smoking (impairs healing), Alcohol (mucosal injury), Stress.
  • Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome: Gastrinoma causes massive acid hypersecretion.

Pathogenesis

PUD results from an imbalance between Aggressive Factors (Acid, Pepsin) and Defensive Factors (Mucus, Bicarbonate, Prostaglandins, Blood flow).

Role of H. pylori:

  • Urease: Produces ammonia (neutralizes acid for survival but damages mucosa).
  • Cytotoxins (CagA, VacA): Cause inflammation and epithelial damage.
    • [cite_start]
  • Hyperacidity: In antral gastritis, it increases gastrin and acid production[cite: 21832].

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Sites: 1st part of Duodenum (most common), Lesser curvature of Stomach (Antrum).
  • Gross:
    • Usually solitary, small (< 2cm).
    • Punched-out defect: Round/oval with straight walls.
    • Margins: Sharp, overhanging, level with surrounding mucosa (not heaped up like cancer).
    • Base: Clean, smooth.
    • Radiating mucosal folds: Due to fibrosis (spoke-wheel appearance).
    • [cite_start]
  • Microscopy (4 Zones - Askanazy Zones): From superficial to deep[cite: 23555]:
    1. Necrotic Zone: Fibrinoid necrosis and debris.
    2. Superficial Exudative Zone: Acute inflammatory cells (neutrophils).
    3. Granulation Tissue Zone: Vascularized connective tissue.
    4. Zone of Cicatrization: Dense collagenous scar replacing the muscle wall.

3. CARCINOMA OF THE STOMACH

Definition: Malignant epithelial tumor of the gastric mucosa. 90% are Adenocarcinomas.

Etiology and Risk Factors

  • Diet: Nitrates/Nitrosamines (smoked/preserved foods), Salt, Low fiber/vitamins.
  • H. pylori: Classified as a Group I carcinogen (causes chronic atrophic gastritis).
  • Host Factors: Chronic Atrophic Gastritis (Pernicious Anemia), Partial Gastrectomy.
  • Genetics: Blood Group A, Hereditary Diffuse Gastric Cancer (CDH1/E-cadherin mutation).

Pathogenesis

  • Intestinal Type: Follows Correa's Cascade: Chronic Gastritis → Atrophy → Intestinal Metaplasia → Dysplasia → Carcinoma. [cite_start]Associated with H. pylori and diet[cite: 23560].
  • Diffuse Type: Arises de novo. Associated with loss of E-cadherin function (discohesive cells). Genetic link is stronger.

Pathologic Features (Morphology)

  • Site: Pylorus/Antrum (50-60%), Lesser Curvature (40%).
  • Gross Classification (Borrmann):
    • Polypoid (Type I): Cauliflower-like mass.
    • Ulcerated (Type II/III): Malignant ulcer with heaped-up, beaded, irregular margins and necrotic base.
    • Diffuse (Type IV): Linitis Plastica (Leather Bottle Stomach). [cite_start]Diffuse infiltration thickens and rigidifies the wall; rugae are flattened[cite: 23561].
  • Microscopy (Lauren Classification):
    • Intestinal Type: Gland-forming columnar cells. Cohesive. Better prognosis.
    • Diffuse Type: Sheets of discohesive cells. Signet Ring Cells (mucin vacuole pushes nucleus to periphery). Induces desmoplasia. [cite_start]Worse prognosis[cite: 23561].

4. TUBERCULOSIS OF THE INTESTINE

Etiology: Mycobacterium tuberculosis. (Secondary to pulmonary TB usually).

Pathogenesis

Swallowing of infected sputum → Bacilli penetrate mucosa (Peyer's patches) → Lymphatics → Granulomatous inflammation.

Pathologic Features

  • Site: Ileocecal region (most common due to lymphoid tissue abundance).
  • Gross Types:
    • Ulcerative: Transverse ulcers (perpendicular to long axis) with undermined edges. (Typhoid ulcers are longitudinal).
    • Hyperplastic: Massive thickening of cecal wall (Ileocecal mass) mimicking cancer.
  • Microscopy:
    • Caseating Granulomas: Epithelioid cells, Langhans giant cells, central caseous necrosis.
    • Located in mucosa, submucosa, and lymph nodes.

5. INFLAMMATORY BOWEL DISEASE (IBD)

Chronic relapsing inflammatory disorders of unknown etiology: Crohn's Disease (CD) and Ulcerative Colitis (UC).

Distinguishing Features

Feature Crohn's Disease (CD) Ulcerative Colitis (UC)
Site Any part of GIT (Mouth to Anus). Ileum common. Colon and Rectum only. Ascends from rectum.
Distribution Skip Lesions (Patchy). Continuous (No skips).
Wall Involvement Transmural (All layers). Thickened wall. Mucosa and Submucosa only (Superficial). Thin wall.
Gross Features Cobblestone mucosa, Deep fissures, Strictures ("String sign"), Creeping fat. Diffuse granularity, Ulceration, Pseudopolyps. No strictures.
Microscopy Non-caseating Granulomas (hallmark). Lymphoid aggregates. Crypt Abscesses (neutrophils in crypts), Crypt distortion. [cite_start]No granulomas[cite: 23567].
Complications Fistulas, Sinuses, Strictures, Malabsorption. Toxic Megacolon, Carcinoma (Higher risk).

6. CARCINOMA OF THE COLON

Definition: Malignant tumor of the large intestine, mostly Adenocarcinoma.

Etiology and Pathogenesis

  • Diet: Low fiber (decreased transit time), High fat/red meat (carcinogens from bile acids).
  • Genetic Pathways:
    • APC/β-catenin Pathway (80%): Chromosomal instability. APC mutation → KRAS → p53. (Adenoma-Carcinoma Sequence) [cite_start][cite: 23572].
    • Microsatellite Instability (MSI): Defective DNA mismatch repair (e.g., HNPCC/Lynch Syndrome).
  • Precursors: Adenomatous polyps (Tubular, Villous).

Pathology (Morphology)

  • Site: Rectosigmoid (most common).
  • Gross Features:
    • Right Colon (Proximal): Polypoid, fungating, cauliflower-like masses. Rarely obstruct (liquid feces). [cite_start]Present with anemia/fatigue[cite: 23574].
    • Left Colon (Distal): Annular, constricting, "Napkin-ring" lesions. Cause obstruction ("Apple-core" on X-ray) and changes in bowel habits.
  • Microscopy:
    • Adenocarcinoma: Malignant glands invading the muscularis propria. Cytologic atypia, necrosis ("dirty necrosis").
    • Mucinous Carcinoma: Abundant extracellular mucin pools (>50%). Worse prognosis.

IMPORTANT UNIVERSITY QUESTIONS

Q1: Classify Gastric Carcinoma. Describe the morphology of Linitis Plastica.

Introduction: Gastric carcinoma is the most common malignant tumor of the stomach, predominantly adenocarcinoma. It is classified based on gross appearance (Borrmann) and microscopy (Lauren).

Body:
Classification:

  • Lauren Classification: Intestinal type (glandular, cohesive) and Diffuse type (signet-ring cells, discohesive).
  • Gross (Borrmann): Polypoid, Ulcerated, Ulcerative-infiltrative, Diffuse infiltrative.

Linitis Plastica (Leather Bottle Stomach):
This is the Diffuse Infiltrative type (Type IV).
  • Gross: The stomach wall is diffusely thickened, rigid, and leathery. The rugal folds are flattened or obliterated. The lumen is narrowed.
  • Microscopy: Diffuse infiltration by discohesive Signet Ring Cells (cells with mucin pushing nucleus to one side). There is a dense desmoplastic (fibrous) reaction, which causes the wall rigidity.

Conclusion: Linitis plastica has a very poor prognosis due to its diffuse nature and late presentation.

Q2: Differentiate between Crohn's Disease and Ulcerative Colitis.

Introduction: Both are chronic inflammatory bowel diseases but differ in distribution and pathology.

Body:
1. Distribution: UC is restricted to the colon and rectum and is continuous. Crohn's can affect any part of the GIT (skip lesions).
2. Gross Pathology: UC shows superficial ulcers and pseudopolyps. Crohn's shows deep linear ulcers ("rake ulcers"), cobblestone mucosa, strictures, and creeping fat.
3. Microscopic Pathology: UC inflammation is limited to mucosa/submucosa with crypt abscesses. Crohn's shows transmural inflammation (all layers) and non-caseating granulomas.
4. Complications: UC leads to toxic megacolon and cancer. Crohn's leads to fistulas, sinus tracts, and malabsorption.

Conclusion: Differentiating them is crucial for management, as surgery is curative for UC but not for Crohn's.
